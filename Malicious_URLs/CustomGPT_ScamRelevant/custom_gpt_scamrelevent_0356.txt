* **Equity**: The ownership value in the company after subtracting liabilities from assets.

### 2. **Types of Financial Statements**

This GPT pulls data from three major financial statements, and understanding them is crucial for interpreting the data:

* **Income Statement**: Shows a company’s profitability over a period (e.g., quarterly or annually). Key figures to focus on include **revenue**, **cost of goods sold (COGS)**, **operating expenses**, and **net income**.
* **Balance Sheet**: Provides a snapshot of a company’s financial position, including its **assets**, **liabilities**, and **equity**.
* **Cash Flow Statement**: Shows the cash inflows and outflows, broken down into operating, investing, and financing activities.

### 3. **Understanding Ratios and Metrics**

This GPT helps calculate and analyze key financial metrics. Knowing how to interpret them is important:

* **CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate)**: Measures the mean annual growth rate of an investment over a specified period of time longer than one year.
* **Sharpe Ratio**: Assesses the risk-adjusted return of an investment (higher is better).
* **Sortino Ratio**: Similar to the Sharpe ratio but focuses only on downside risk (lower volatility).
* **Standard Deviation**: Measures the volatility of an asset or portfolio.
* **Max Drawdown**: The largest peak-to-trough decline during a specific period, indicating potential risk.

### 4. **Portfolio Theory and Diversification**

* **Diversification**: A strategy that involves spreading investments across various assets to reduce risk.
* **Asset Allocation**: The process of deciding how to distribute investments across different asset classes (stocks, bonds, real estate, etc.).

* **Benchmarking**: Compare the performance of your portfolio with a benchmark (e.g., S\&P 500 or other indices) to evaluate its success.

### 5. **Backtesting a Portfolio**

* **Understand Allocation**: Decide how much of your capital to invest in different assets (e.g., 50% in stocks, 50% in bonds).

### 7. **Understanding Risk**

### 1. **Basic Financial Knowledge:**

* **Understanding of Financial Statements:**

  * **Income Statement:** This shows a company’s revenue, costs, and profits over a period of time. Key metrics to understand include:

    * **Revenue (Sales)**: Total income generated from business operations.
    * **Net Income:** Profit after all expenses are deducted from revenue.
    * **Operating Expenses:** The costs related to running the business (e.g., salaries, rent, etc.).
    * **Gross Profit & Operating Income:** These provide insights into a company’s core business performance.
  * **Balance Sheet:** This presents a snapshot of a company’s financial position at a particular point in time. Key components include:

    * **Assets:** What the company owns (e.g., cash, property).
    * **Liabilities:** What the company owes (e.g., debts, loans).
    * **Equity:** The company’s net worth (Assets - Liabilities).
  * **Cash Flow Statement:** This tracks the inflow and outflow of cash within a company, broken down into:

    * **Operating Activities:** Cash generated from core operations.
    * **Investing Activities:** Cash spent on or earned from investments like property or equipment.
    * **Financing Activities:** Cash related to borrowing, issuing stock, or paying dividends.

### 2. **Investment Metrics and Ratios:**

* **CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate):** Helps assess the mean annual growth rate of an investment over a specified period of time.
* **Sharpe Ratio:** Measures the return of an investment relative to its risk, helping you assess risk-adjusted performance.
* **Sortino Ratio:** Similar to Sharpe, but focuses only on the downside risk, which is often more relevant to investors concerned with avoiding losses.
* **Standard Deviation:** Indicates the volatility or risk of an asset’s returns.
* **Max Drawdown:** The greatest loss from a peak to a trough during a specific period.

### 3. **Investment Strategy and Portfolio Management:**

* **Asset Allocation:** This refers to how you divide your investments among different asset classes (e.g., stocks, bonds, real estate) to balance risk and return.
* **Diversification:** Spreading your investments across different assets to reduce the risk of a total loss.
* **Rebalancing:** Periodically adjusting your portfolio to maintain your target allocation.

### 4. **Backtesting Portfolios:**

* **Benchmarking:** You can compare your portfolio’s performance to a benchmark (like an index) to evaluate its success.

### 5. **Risk Assessment:**

* **Risk vs. Return:** Every investment carries some level of risk, and higher returns usually come with higher risk. Understanding this relationship is key to building a portfolio that aligns with your financial goals and risk tolerance.
