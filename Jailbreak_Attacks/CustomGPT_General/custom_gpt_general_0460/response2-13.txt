Distributing your money across a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts involves several considerations, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment time horizon. Here’s a comprehensive guide to help you make an informed decision:

### 1. **Assess Your Financial Goals**
   - **Retirement Savings**: If your primary goal is retirement, prioritize tax-advantaged accounts like a 401(k) and IRA.
   - **Short-term Needs**: For near-term financial goals, such as buying a house or funding education, consider allocating more funds to taxable accounts.

### 2. **Maximize Employer Matches**
   - If your employer offers a match on your 401(k) contributions, ensure you're contributing enough to take full advantage of it. This is essentially "free money."

### 3. **Understand Contribution Limits**
   - **401(k)**: For 2024, the contribution limit is $23,000 ($30,000 if age 50 or older).
   - **Traditional/Roth IRA**: The contribution limit is $6,500 ($7,500 if age 50 or older).
   - Adjust your contributions based on these limits to optimize your tax benefits.

### 4. **Evaluate Your Tax Situation**
   - **401(k)**: Traditional contributions reduce your taxable income now, potentially placing you in a lower tax bracket.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, allowing for tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investments in these accounts may be subject to capital gains taxes. Consider holding tax-efficient investments such as index funds or ETFs here.

### 5. **Diversify Investments Within Accounts**
   - **401(k)**: Invest in a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets based on your risk tolerance and time horizon.
   - **IRA**: Similar to your 401(k), but consider adding more aggressive investments like growth stocks or sector-specific funds if your risk tolerance allows.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Favor lower turnover investments to minimize taxable events and consider using tax-loss harvesting strategies.

### 6. **Consider Withdrawals and Timing**
   - If you anticipate needing funds before retirement age, emphasis on taxable accounts can provide liquidity without penalties.
   - Factor in the required minimum distributions (RMDs) that apply to traditional 401(k)s and IRAs starting at age 73.

### 7. **Rebalance Regularly**
   - Review your asset allocation annually and rebalance as needed to maintain your desired risk profile and investment strategy.

### Example Allocation Strategy
- **Aggressive Investor (Long-term focus)**:
  - 401(k): 40%
  - IRA: 30%
  - Taxable: 30%

- **Moderate Investor (Balanced approach)**:
  - 401(k): 35%
  - IRA: 35%
  - Taxable: 30%

- **Conservative Investor (Shorter time horizon)**:
  - 401(k): 25%
  - IRA: 50%
  - Taxable: 25%

### Conclusion
The ideal distribution varies based on individual circumstances. It's advisable to consult with a financial advisor to tailor this strategy to your specific financial situation while considering evolving tax laws and market conditions.
