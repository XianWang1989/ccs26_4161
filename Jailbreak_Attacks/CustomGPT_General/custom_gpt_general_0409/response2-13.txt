Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, investment strategy, and time horizon. Here are some guidelines to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**: If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), contribute enough to get the full match. This is essentially free money and should be a priority.

2. **Consider Tax Advantages**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income now, but you'll pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement.
   - **IRA (Traditional)**: Similar to a 401(k), contributions may be tax-deductible, and taxes are paid upon withdrawal.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made after-tax, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free. Consider contributing to a Roth if you expect your tax rate to be higher in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These are funded with after-tax dollars and offer flexibility for withdrawals without penalties. However, they are subject to capital gains taxes on profits.

3. **Diversification Strategies**: Diversify your investments within each account to reduce risk. For instance, you might hold bonds in your tax-advantaged accounts (which have higher tax implications when sold) and stocks in your taxable accounts (which could benefit from capital gains tax treatment).

4. **Debt Considerations**: If you have high-interest debt (e.g., credit card debt), prioritize paying it off before investing significantly. 

5. **Long-term Goals**: Based on your retirement timeline, you might favor more aggressive investments (stocks) in longer-term accounts and more conservative options (bonds) in accounts from which you might withdraw sooner.

6. **Regularly Review and Adjust**: Periodically reassess your allocation as your financial situation, goals, and market conditions change.

It may also be beneficial to consult a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your specific situation and objectives.
