[🔒CLASSIC] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts typically depends on your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **Maximize employer match**: If your employer offers a matching contribution to your 401(k), aim to contribute enough to take full advantage of this benefit first.

2. **Consider tax implications**: Traditional 401(k) and IRA contributions are made pre-tax, lowering your taxable income. However, withdrawals in retirement will be taxed. Roth accounts, on the other hand, are funded with post-tax dollars, meaning withdrawals are tax-free. Taxable accounts are subject to capital gains taxes.

3. **Diversify investments**: Each account type can hold different investments (stocks, bonds, mutual funds). Consider your overall asset allocation to ensure it's balanced according to your risk tolerance and investment timeline.

4. **Debt considerations**: If you have high-interest debt, it might be wise to pay that down before maxing out retirement contributions.

5. **Withdrawal strategy**: Consider the sequence of withdrawals in retirement. Ideally, you might want to withdraw from taxable accounts first to allow tax-deferred accounts to continue growing.

Consult with a financial advisor to create a personalized strategy that fits your unique situation.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money depends on your personal financial situation, but here's a bold approach you might consider:

1. **401(k) First**: Start with maximizing your 401(k), especially if your employer offers matching contributions. It's basically free money. 

2. **Roth IRA Option**: After your 401(k) contributions, consider putting money into a Roth IRA if you're eligible. The tax-free withdrawals in retirement can be a huge advantage.

3. **Taxable Account**: Finally, allocate extra funds to a taxable account for flexibility. This helps with liquidity, and you can invest in stocks that might yield dividends, which can be taxed at a lower rate.

4. **Age and Goals**: Adjust the percentages based on your age and retirement goals. Younger? Load up on growth stocks in the taxable accounts. Closer to retirement? Shift to bonds in the 401(k) for stability.

5. **Continuous Review**: Life changes, so regularly adjust your investments based on your current financial circumstances, market conditions, and future outlook.

Ultimately, it’s your money, so take the road that feels right for you, but be sure to consult someone who knows your full picture to optimize your investment strategy!
