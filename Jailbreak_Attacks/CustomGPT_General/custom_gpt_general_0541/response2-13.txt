Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here's a general strategy to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**
   - **Action**: Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match if available. This is essentially “free money” and a great way to boost your retirement savings.

### 2. **Consider Tax Advantages**
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are pre-tax, reducing your taxable income now, but withdrawals are taxed in retirement.
   - **Traditional IRA**: Similar to a 401(k) in terms of tax treatment. Contributions may be tax-deductible, but withdrawals are taxed.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made after-tax, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free in retirement. This is beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These provide flexibility since they don't have contribution limits or withdrawal penalties, but you will owe capital gains taxes on profits.

### 3. **Fill Tax-Advantaged Accounts First**
   - If you have maxed out contributions to your 401(k) (up to IRS limits) and any IRAs, then consider putting additional savings into taxable accounts for liquidity and access.

### 4. **Diversify Investments Across Accounts**
   - **Tax-efficient investments** like index funds or ETFs are suitable for taxable accounts due to lower capital gains distributions.
   - **Bonds** or high-dividend stocks may be better in tax-deferred accounts to shield interest income from taxes.

### 5. **Consider Your Retirement Timeline**
   - If you’re closer to retirement, shift more funds into conservative investments in tax-deferred accounts to reduce risk.
   - Younger individuals can afford to take on more risk since they have time to recover from market fluctuations.

### 6. **Reassess Regularly**
   - Review your asset allocation annually or after significant life changes (such as marriage or job changes) to ensure it aligns with your financial goals.

### Summary Example Allocation:
- **401(k)**: 15-20% of income (maximize employer match)
- **IRA/Roth IRA**: Up to $6,500 (2023 limit), or $7,500 if 50 or older
- **Taxable Accounts**: Any additional savings after maximizing tax-advantaged accounts

Consulting with a financial advisor can personalize this strategy based on your specific situation and objectives.
