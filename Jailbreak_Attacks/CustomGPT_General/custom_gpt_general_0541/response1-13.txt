Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax considerations, time horizon, and personal circumstances. Here are some general guidelines to help you determine the best allocation:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match**
   - If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), contribute enough to take full advantage of this benefit. It’s essentially free money.

### 2. **Prioritize Tax-Advantaged Accounts**
   - **401(k) and IRA:** Both accounts allow tax-deferred growth. If you're eligible, consider maximizing contributions to these accounts before investing in taxable accounts. The contribution limits for 2023 are $22,500 for 401(k) plans and $6,500 for IRAs (with an additional $1,000 catch-up contribution for those over age 50).
   - **Traditional vs. Roth IRA:** Consider your tax situation now versus retirement. A Roth IRA allows for tax-free withdrawals in retirement, which can be beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in the future.

### 3. **Consider Investment Options**
   - 401(k) plans may have limited investment options and potentially higher fees. If your 401(k) has poor investment choices or high fees, it may make sense to prioritize contributing to your IRA or taxable accounts instead.

### 4. **Assess Your Financial Goals**
   - Different accounts serve different purposes:
     - **Retirement accounts** are for long-term savings and typically have restrictions on withdrawals.
     - **Taxable accounts** provide flexibility to access funds for short- to medium-term goals without penalties.

### 5. **Understand Tax Implications**
   - Investments in taxable accounts are subject to capital gains tax, while growth in tax-advantaged accounts is tax-deferred. Consider holding investments that generate interest or dividends in tax-advantaged accounts to avoid immediate tax liabilities.

### Example Allocation Strategy:
- **40% in 401(k)** (to get the full employer match and possibly maximize contributions)
- **30% in IRA** (to take advantage of tax-deferred growth)
- **30% in taxable accounts** (for flexibility and diversifying your investment strategy)

### 6. **Review and Adjust Regularly**
   - Periodically review your allocations and adjust as your financial situation, goals, and market conditions change.

### Conclusion
Creating a balanced distribution among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is crucial for achieving your financial goals. Consider consulting with a financial advisor to tailor a plan specific to your needs.
