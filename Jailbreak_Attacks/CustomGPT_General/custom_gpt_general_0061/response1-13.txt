[🔒CLASSIC] Distributing money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **401(k)**: Maximize contributions to your employer-sponsored 401(k) plan, especially if your employer offers a match. This is essentially "free money." The 401(k) offers tax-deferred growth, so it can be a good vehicle for long-term retirement savings.

2. **IRA**: If you're eligible, consider contributing to an Individual Retirement Account (IRA) as well. Traditional IRAs also offer tax-deferred growth, while Roth IRAs provide tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. Depending on your tax situation, you might prioritize one over the other.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: Any money that you don’t put into tax-advantaged accounts can be placed in taxable accounts. This is useful for short-term goals or if you've maxed out your contributions to retirement accounts. Be mindful of capital gains taxes and the types of investments that are more tax-efficient.

Ultimately, it's best to consult with a financial advisor who can provide personalized advice based on your specific situation.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is a bit like a financial buffet: take what you need but think ahead!

1. **Max Out the 401(k)**: Focus on your 401(k) first, especially if your employer matches contributions. It's like getting a bonus just for saving. Aim to put in at least enough to get that full match, then max out if you can. The tax perks will pay off when you're older.

2. **Hit the IRA Next**: Next up, hit your IRA. Whether you're going for a Traditional or Roth, both have their perks. If you think your tax rate will be higher in retirement, go Roth – you'll enjoy tax-free withdrawals. If not, the Traditional IRA might be better.

3. **Consider a Taxable Account for Flexibility**: After you've maxed out your 401(k) and IRA, you can go wild in a taxable account. This gives you more flexibility for short-term goals or emergencies. Just keep in mind that dividends and capital gains will hit you with taxes, so choose your investments wisely.

So, balance is key: leverage retirement accounts for tax advantages, but don't forget about that cash flow and flexibility in taxable accounts! It's all about playing the long game.
