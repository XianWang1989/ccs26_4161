Balancing your investments among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can be pivotal for long-term financial health. Here’s a structured approach to consider:

### 1. **Understand Account Types and Tax Implications**
   - **401(k)**: Usually offers pre-tax contributions, which grow tax-deferred. If your employer matches contributions, prioritize getting the full match.
   - **IRA**: Traditional IRAs offer tax-deferred growth, while Roth IRAs provide tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Your choice here depends on your current versus expected future tax rates.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investments here are subject to capital gains tax. They provide flexibility for early withdrawals without penalties but don't offer tax advantages like retirement accounts.

### 2. **Assess Your Financial Goals**
   - Define your short- and long-term goals (e.g., retirement age, desired lifestyle).
   - Determine your risk tolerance to guide your investment choices.

### 3. **Maximize Contributions**
   - **401(k)**: Aim to contribute at least enough to get the full employer match first.
   - **IRA**: If you're eligible, contribute the maximum allowed; consider a Roth IRA if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: After maximizing tax-advantaged accounts, invest extra funds here.

### 4. **Asset Allocation Strategy**
   - Allocate investments based on your risk tolerance. A common strategy might include:
     - **Equities**: Higher growth potential, suitable for tax-advantaged accounts due to their long-term growth.
     - **Bonds**: Can provide income and stability, often better suited for taxable accounts to take advantage of tax-exempt interest if applicable.
   - Ensure diverse asset classes in each account type to mitigate risk.

### 5. **Rebalance Periodically**
   - Review and adjust your portfolio at least annually to maintain your desired allocation.

### 6. **Strategic Withdrawals**
   - Plan how and when you'll withdraw funds. Generally:
     - Withdraw from taxable accounts first to allow 401(k) and IRA to grow.
     - Consider tax implications of each withdrawal.

### 7. **Consult a Financial Advisor**
   - Personalized advice can greatly enhance your strategy, especially regarding tax implications and specific investment choices.

### Example Scenario
1. **Contribute Enough to 401(k) for Match**: If your employer matches 100% up to 5%, contribute at least that amount.
2. **Max out IRA Contributions**: Both for traditional and Roth IRAs if eligible.
3. **Invest Remaining Funds in Taxable Accounts**: After maxing out retirement accounts, use this for investments that provide liquidity or short-term needs.

This general plan can be tailored based on individual circumstances and should evolve with changes in financial situations and tax laws.
