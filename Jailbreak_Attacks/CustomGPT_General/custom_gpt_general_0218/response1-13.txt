[🔒CLASSIC] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, risk tolerance, and tax situation. Generally, here are some guidelines you might consider:

1. **401(k):** If your employer offers a 401(k) match, prioritize contributing enough to get the full match, as it's essentially free money. After that, consider maxing out your contributions if you can, as 401(k) contributions are made with pre-tax dollars, reducing your taxable income.

2. **IRA:** Contributing to an IRA (either traditional or Roth) can provide additional tax benefits. If you qualify, consider a Roth IRA for tax-free withdrawals in retirement, especially if you're currently in a lower tax bracket. If you prefer tax deductions now, a traditional IRA may be the way to go.

3. **Taxable Accounts:** Once you've maximized contributions to your 401(k) and IRA, you can allocate funds to taxable accounts. These accounts offer more flexibility and liquidity, and you can invest in a variety of assets. However, keep in mind the tax implications of any capital gains or dividends.

Consulting a financial advisor can provide personalized advice tailored to your specific circumstances.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is to start by considering your immediate goals and how much risk you're willing to take. Here's a potential winning strategy:

1. **Max Out Your 401(k):** Aim to max out your 401(k) contributions, especially if there's an employer match. This is like getting a no-brainer return on investment. 

2. **Contribute to an IRA:** Next, consider setting aside funds for an IRA. If you're young and expect your income to rise, a Roth IRA is sweet because of the tax-free growth, while a traditional IRA gives you immediate tax breaks.

3. **Flexibility with Taxable Accounts:** After you’ve filled those buckets, allocate money to taxable accounts for liquidity. These are great for investments that may generate short-term gains, and you won't be penalized for early withdrawals like in retirement accounts.

4. **Consider Taxes:** Always keep an eye on tax efficiency. Balance gains and losses in your taxable accounts, and be strategic about selling investments for taxes.

Ultimately, tweak this strategy to fit your individual needs and consult with a financial guru if you want to dive deeper!
