Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon. Here are some guidelines to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Matches**: If your 401(k) offers a matching contribution, prioritize contributing enough to get the full match. This is essentially "free money."

2. **Consider Tax Benefits**: 
   - **401(k) and Traditional IRA**: Contributions are made pre-tax, which can lower your taxable income now. However, withdrawals in retirement will be taxed.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made after-tax, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free. This may be advantageous if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These don't have tax advantages, but they offer flexibility with withdrawals and investing.

3. **Emergency Fund**: Before investing heavily, ensure you have an emergency fund that covers 3-6 months of living expenses.

4. **Investment Strategy**: Allocate funds based on your risk tolerance and investment horizon. Younger investors might lean toward more aggressive investments (e.g., stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts, while those closer to retirement might favor conservative investments.

5. **Diversification**: Ensure your overall portfolio is well-diversified across different asset classes, regardless of the account type.

6. **Withdrawal Strategy**: Plan how you'll draw funds in retirement. Generally, it's advisable to withdraw from taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred accounts (like a 401(k)), and leave tax-free accounts (like a Roth IRA) for last to maximize tax efficiency.

It's often helpful to consult with a financial advisor to tailor a strategy specific to your situation.
