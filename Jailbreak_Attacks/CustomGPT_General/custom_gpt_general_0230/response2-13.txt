The distribution of your money across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts really depends on your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here’s a breakdown of how to consider allocating funds among these three options:

### 1. **401(k) Accounts**
- **Match Contributions**: If your employer offers a matching contribution, prioritize contributing enough to get the full match; it’s essentially free money.
- **Tax Benefits**: Contributions are typically tax-deferred, which means you'll pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement. This can be beneficial if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket later.
- **Investments**: Your choices may be limited based on your employer’s plan. Ensure you are selecting a diversified portfolio within this account.

### 2. **IRAs (Traditional or Roth)**
- **Tax Advantages**: Traditional IRAs offer tax-deferred growth, while Roth IRAs allow for tax-free withdrawals in retirement (provided your account has been open for at least 5 years and you're 59½ or older).
- **Contribution Limits**: Be aware of the annual contribution limits for IRAs and consider maxing out contributions if it aligns with your financial capabilities.
- **Flexibility**: Depending on your income and tax situation, choosing between a Traditional and a Roth IRA can significantly impact your future tax burden.

### 3. **Taxable Accounts**
- **Flexible Access**: Unlike retirement accounts, you can withdraw from taxable accounts without penalties at any age.
- **Capital Gains Tax**: Be mindful that selling investments in taxable accounts could lead to capital gains taxes. It's often a good idea to hold investments for longer periods to benefit from lower long-term capital gains rates.
- **Diversification**: This account can help you invest in areas that may not be available or ideal within your tax-advantaged accounts, such as individual stocks or alternative investments.

### **General Distribution Strategy:**
1. **Max Out the Employer Match in 401(k)**: Focus here first, as that is typically more rewarding.
2. **Contribute to an IRA**: Depending on your financial situation, consider maxing out your IRA next. If eligible, a Roth IRA could be incredibly beneficial for tax-free withdrawals later on.
3. **Fund Your Taxable Accounts**: After contributing to your retirement accounts, allocate any remaining funds to taxable accounts for flexibility and additional investment opportunities.

### **Review Regularly**:
Make it a habit to review your allocation annually or as your financial situation changes. Rebalancing your portfolio is crucial to ensure your investments align with your risk tolerance and goals.

It's always a good idea to consult with a financial advisor to tailor these strategies to your specific situation. 

Remember, striking the right balance between these accounts can provide both immediate and long-term benefits for your financial future!
