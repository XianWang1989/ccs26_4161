When it comes to distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, the best strategy often depends on your individual financial situation, goals, tax considerations, and investment time horizon. Here are some general guidelines to help you make informed decisions:

1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts:**
   - **401(k):** If your employer offers a matching contribution, aim to contribute enough to get the full match, as this is essentially free money. Consider maximizing your contributions to take advantage of tax deferral, especially if you're in a higher tax bracket now.
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth):** Depending on your income level and tax situation, you may benefit from contributing to a Traditional IRA (tax-deductible contributions) or a Roth IRA (tax-free withdrawals in retirement). If eligible, consider maxing out your IRA contributions as well.

2. **Consider the Tax Implications:**
   - Tax-deferred accounts (like a 401(k) and Traditional IRA) allow your investments to grow without being taxed until you withdraw in retirement. This can be beneficial if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket when you retire.
   - Roth IRA investments grow tax-free, so consider this if you believe your tax rate will be higher in the future.
   - Taxable accounts do not offer tax advantages, but they provide flexibility. Capital gains tax is incurred only when you sell investments, and you can access funds without penalties.

3. **Emergency Fund:**
   - Ensure you have an adequate emergency fund (3-6 months of expenses) in a high-yield savings account or another easily accessible account to avoid early withdrawals from tax-advantaged accounts.

4. **Investment Strategy:**
   - Determine your risk tolerance and investment goals. Allocate investments in taxable accounts to more tax-efficient investments (like index funds or municipal bonds) to minimize taxes. In tax-advantaged accounts, you can hold less tax-efficient investments (like actively managed funds).

5. **Withdrawal Strategy Planning:**
   - As you approach retirement, consider how you’ll withdraw from these accounts. You may want to be strategic about which accounts to withdraw from first to manage your tax burden effectively.

6. **Regular Review and Adjustment:**
   - Periodically reassess your allocation based on changes in your financial situation, market conditions, and retirement goals. 

It's often helpful to consult with a financial advisor who can provide personalized guidance based on your specific circumstances.
