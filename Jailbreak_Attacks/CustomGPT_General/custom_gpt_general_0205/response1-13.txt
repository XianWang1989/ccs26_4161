Distributing your money among a 401(k), Individual Retirement Account (IRA), and taxable accounts can significantly impact your long-term financial growth and tax efficiency. Here are some general guidelines to consider when allocating your investments:

1. **Maximize Contributions to Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: Prioritize contributions to your 401(k) and IRA due to their tax benefits. For the 401(k), strive to contribute at least enough to get any employer match, as this is essentially free money. After reaching the employer match, consider maxing out your IRA contributions.

2. **Consider Your Tax Bracket**: If you are in a higher tax bracket, prioritize pre-tax contributions to your 401(k) and traditional IRA, which may lower your taxable income in the present. Conversely, if you anticipate being in a lower tax bracket during retirement, Roth accounts may be more beneficial, as withdrawals from Roth IRAs are tax-free.

3. **Investment Selection**: Use your tax-advantaged accounts to hold investments that are expected to generate a lot of taxable income or capital gains, such as bonds or actively managed funds. In contrast, consider placing investments that can take advantage of long-term capital gains tax rates in taxable accounts, such as index funds or ETFs.

4. **Evaluate Your Time Horizon**: If you have a long investment horizon, your 401(k) and IRA can invest aggressively in stocks. However, if you anticipate needing funds sooner, keep a balanced approach in your taxable accounts to maintain liquidity.

5. **Rebalance Regularly**: Periodically review your asset allocation across your accounts to ensure it aligns with your risk tolerance and investment goals. Adjustments may be necessary as market conditions change or as you approach retirement.

6. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: If uncertainties remain about the best approach for your circumstances, consider consulting a financial advisor. They can help tailor a strategy to meet your individual financial situation.

Ultimately, the ideal distribution will depend on several factors, including your financial goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance. Evaluating these elements carefully will help you make informed decisions about your investment allocations.
