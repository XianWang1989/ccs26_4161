To determine the best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following factors:

### 1. **Tax Implications**
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are often made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income in the year you contribute. Taxes are due upon withdrawal in retirement.
   - **IRA (Traditional/Roth)**:
     - **Traditional IRA**: Similar to a 401(k), contributions may be tax-deductible, but withdrawals are taxed.
     - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investments are subject to capital gains tax when sold, and dividends are taxed as income in the year they’re received.

### 2. **Investment Choices**
   - 401(k) plans often have a limited selection of investment options.
   - IRAs (both traditional and Roth) generally provide a wider range of investment choices.
   - Taxable accounts offer flexibility but may have higher tax implications on gains.

### 3. **Contribution Limits**
   - **401(k)**: For 2023, the contribution limit is $22,500 for employees under 50 and $30,000 for those 50 and older.
   - **IRA**: The contribution limit is $6,500 (or $7,500 for those 50 and older).
   - **Taxable Accounts**: No contribution limits, but watch out for tax efficiency in this account.

### 4. **Withdrawal Flexibility**
   - 401(k) and traditional IRA withdrawals can incur penalties if taken before age 59½, with exceptions.
   - Roth IRAs allow you to withdraw contributions without penalties at any time.
   - Taxable accounts offer the most flexibility for withdrawals without penalty.

### Suggested Distribution Strategy
1. **Max out employer match in the 401(k)**: If your employer offers a match, contribute at least enough to get the full match.
2. **Contribute to IRAs**: After maximizing your 401(k) match, contribute to either a Traditional or Roth IRA, depending on your tax situation.
3. **Continue funding your 401(k)**: Aim to contribute up to the maximum limit based on your retirement goals.
4. **Allocate remaining funds to taxable accounts**: Use these for additional savings or investments, particularly if you want flexibility for short-term goals.

### Example Allocation
- **Retirement Age 50 and Under**:
  - 401(k): 15%
  - Traditional/Roth IRA: 10%
  - Taxable: 5%
  
- **Retirement Age 50 and Older** (for catch-up contributions):
  - 401(k): 20%
  - Traditional/Roth IRA: 10%
  - Taxable: 5%

### Final Thoughts
Adjust your allocation based on your financial goals, risk tolerance, and timeline. Regularly review and rebalance your investments to stay aligned with your retirement plan.
