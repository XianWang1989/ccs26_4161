[🔒CLASSIC] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts generally depends on your individual financial goals, tax situation, and investment horizon. Here are some guidelines to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Contributions**: First, contribute enough to your 401(k) to take full advantage of any employer match, as this is essentially free money.

2. **Consider Tax Benefits**: If you qualify, consider contributing to a Roth IRA for tax-free growth and withdrawals. Traditional IRAs provide tax-deductible contributions, which can lower your taxable income.

3. **Tax Efficiency**: Taxable accounts can provide more flexibility and access for short-term needs but may have tax implications on capital gains. Consider holding tax-efficient investments, such as index funds or ETFs, in these accounts.

4. **Investment Horizons**: Longer-term investments might be better suited for retirement accounts due to their tax advantages, while shorter-term savings could be kept in taxable accounts.

5. **Diversification**: Ensure your overall investment strategy is balanced across all accounts, considering asset allocation that aligns with your risk tolerance and retirement goals.

Consulting a financial advisor can also help tailor these strategies specifically to your situation.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is to adopt a strategy tailored to your unique goals, risk tolerance, and tax situation. Here's a straightforward plan:

1. **401(k) Contributions**: Start by maxing out your employer match in your 401(k). This is free money that grows tax-deferred. Aim for at least 15% of your salary if possible.

2. **IRA Contributions**: After maximizing your 401(k) match, consider contributing to a Roth IRA if you qualify. This allows for tax-free growth, which is fantastic in the long run. If you're looking for tax deductions, put money into a Traditional IRA.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: Use taxable accounts for more immediate goals or for additional savings. This can be where you hold investments that yield qualified dividends and long-term capital gains, which are taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income.

4. **Withdrawal Flexibility**: Remember, retirement accounts have penalties for early withdrawals, so keep some cash accessible. 

5. **Balance Your Portfolio**: Make sure your investments across these accounts align with your risk tolerance and objectives. Just because one account is intended for retirement doesn’t mean you can’t have a mix of aggressive and conservative assets spread out.

Adjust this strategy as your life circumstances change, and don’t hesitate to consult with a financial advisor for personalized advice!
