Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors such as your retirement goals, tax situation, risk tolerance, and investment timeline. Here are some general principles to consider:

1. **Maximize Contributions to Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: 
   - **401(k)**: If your employer offers a matching contribution, aim to contribute at least enough to get the full match. This is essentially free money. The 401(k) often has higher contribution limits than IRAs.
   - **IRA**: After maximizing your 401(k) match, consider funding a Traditional or Roth IRA. Roth IRAs allow for tax-free growth and withdrawals, while Traditional IRAs may provide tax deductions now.
  
2. **Consider Your Tax Situation**:
   - If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, favor Roth accounts (like a Roth IRA) to benefit from tax-free withdrawals.
   - If you expect to be in a lower tax bracket, Traditional accounts (like a Traditional IRA and 401(k)) can be beneficial because you receive tax deductions now.

3. **Evaluate Investment Options**:
   - Different accounts offer different investment options. 401(k)s may offer a limited selection of funds, while IRAs generally provide a broader range, including individual stocks, bonds, and ETFs. Choose accounts that allow you to invest in asset classes that align with your risk tolerance and financial goals.

4. **Diversification**:
   - Diversifying your investments across all three accounts can mitigate risk. Ensure you are not overly concentrated in any one asset or market sector.

5. **Liquidity Needs**:
   - Assess your liquidity needs. Taxable accounts allow for easier access to cash without penalties, so they might be better for short-term goals or emergencies.

6. **Long-Term Perspective**:
   - For long-term growth, consider prioritizing tax-advantaged accounts. The compounding effect can significantly enhance your retirement savings.

In summary, a balanced approach that maximizes tax-advantaged accounts while keeping some liquidity in taxable accounts is often best. It's recommended to consult a financial advisor to tailor your strategy to your individual situation.
