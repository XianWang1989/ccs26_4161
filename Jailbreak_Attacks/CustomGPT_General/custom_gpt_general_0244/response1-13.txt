The optimal distribution of your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**
   - If your employer offers a matching contribution to your 401(k), contribute enough to receive the full match. This is essentially "free money."

### 2. **Contribute to a Roth IRA (if eligible)**
   - If you're eligible, consider contributing to a Roth IRA after maximizing your 401(k) contributions. Roth IRAs allow tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement.

### 3. **Consider Tax Bracket and Taxation of Accounts**
   - **Tax-Deferred Accounts (401(k) and Traditional IRA):** Contributions reduce your taxable income now, but you'll pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement. Ideal if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Tax-Free Account (Roth IRA):** No taxes on qualified withdrawals. Beneficial if you anticipate being in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** These are subject to capital gains taxes when you sell investments. Consider keeping assets with high growth potential here, as long-term capital gains rates are generally lower than ordinary income tax rates.

### 4. **Diversification and Liquidity Needs**
   - Maintain a balanced investment strategy across all accounts, considering your risk tolerance and investment timeline.
   - Ensure you have enough liquidity in your taxable accounts for any short-term needs.

### 5. **Adjust Based on Age and Retirement Goals**
   - Younger investors may prioritize tax-deferred accounts for growth, while those nearing retirement might focus on asset preservation and liquidity.

### 6. **Regular Reviews and Rebalancing**
   - Periodically review your allocations based on market performance, changes in income, and shifting financial goals.

### Example Distribution Strategy:
- Contribute enough to your 401(k) to maximize the employer match.
- Allocate additional funds to a Roth IRA if eligible.
- Use a taxable account for investments that may not benefit from tax-advantaged growth, or for short-term financial goals.

Consulting with a financial advisor can provide personalized recommendations based on your specific situation.
