Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here are some strategies to consider:

### 1. **401(k)**
   - **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to take full advantage of it. This is essentially "free money."
   - **Tax Advantages**: Contributions are made pre-tax (or Roth if it's a Roth 401(k)), which can lower your taxable income now or allow for tax-free withdrawals later.
   - **High Contribution Limits**: Max out your contributions if possible, given the higher limits compared to IRAs.

### 2. **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**
   - **Consider IRA Type**:
     - **Traditional IRA**: Good for tax deductions now, but withdrawals in retirement will be taxed.
     - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made post-tax, allowing for tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. Ideal if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.
   - **Contribution Limits**: Ensure you’re aware of the limits and contribute accordingly, aiming to max out if you can.

### 3. **Taxable Accounts**
   - **Flexibility**: Offers the most flexibility with contributions and withdrawals. Use this for shorter-term goals.
   - **Tax Considerations**: Be mindful of capital gains tax. Holding investments for over a year can qualify for lower long-term capital gains rates.
   - **Investment Choices**: Unlike retirement accounts, you can choose a variety of investments without restrictions.

### 4. **General Distribution Strategy**
   - **Emergency Fund**: Before investing, ensure you have an adequate emergency fund (3-6 months' worth of expenses).
   - **Investment Goals and Risk Tolerance**: Balance your allocations based on your risk tolerance and when you plan to access the funds.
   - **Diversification**: All accounts should be diversified across different asset classes (stocks, bonds, etc.) to manage risk.

### Suggested Allocation Approach
- **Age and Timeline**: Younger investors might favor aggressive growth (more in stocks), while those closer to retirement might shift toward stability (more in bonds).
- **Tax Efficiency**: Place high-growth investments (like stocks) in retirement accounts to defer taxes, while income-producing investments (like bonds) might be more efficient in taxable accounts.

### Regular Reviews
- Monitor and rebalance your portfolio periodically to maintain your desired asset allocation and adjust for any changes in your financial situation.

### Conclusion
Consult a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your specific circumstances. This ensures your strategy aligns with your long-term financial goals and risk tolerance.
