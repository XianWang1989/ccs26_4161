Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts effectively involves considering several factors, including your goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here’s a general guide:

### 1. **Understand Each Account Type**

- **401(k):** 
  - Contributions are typically pre-tax, reducing taxable income.
  - Often employer-matched, which is "free money."
  - Limited investment choices.

- **IRA (Traditional/Roth):**
  - Traditional IRA contributions may be tax-deductible, and taxes are paid upon withdrawal.
  - Roth IRA contributions are made with after-tax dollars, allowing tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
  - More investment choices than a 401(k).

- **Taxable Accounts:**
  - No tax advantages, but you have flexibility in withdrawals.
  - Capital gains taxes apply on profits from selling investments.

### 2. **Asset Allocation**

- **Start with the 401(k):** Max out contributions to ensure you receive any employer match. Focus on higher-risk investments here, as taxes are deferred.

- **Then Contribute to an IRA:** If eligible, consider maxing out a Roth IRA first for tax-free growth, then a Traditional IRA if that fits better with your tax situation.

- **Use Taxable Accounts for Additional Funds:** After maximizing tax-advantaged accounts, invest any additional savings in taxable accounts. Focus on investments that produce lower turnover to minimize capital gains taxes.

### 3. **Consider Tax Implications**

- Aim to hold stocks in tax-advantaged accounts (401(k) and IRA) and bonds or cash in taxable accounts to minimize taxes on interest income.
- Use tax-loss harvesting strategies in taxable accounts to offset gains.

### 4. **Investment Timeline**

- If you are years away from retirement, lean toward aggressive growth investments (stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts for maximum growth potential.
- As you approach retirement, shift toward more stable investments (bonds, cash) to protect your capital.

### 5. **Regular Review and Adjustment**

- Regularly review your investment allocation and reallocate as necessary based on changes in your financial situation, market conditions, and retirement plans.

### Conclusion

A balanced approach that maximizes contributions to tax-advantaged accounts and strategically utilizes taxable accounts is often the best strategy. Consulting a financial advisor can also help tailor a plan that fits your specific needs.
