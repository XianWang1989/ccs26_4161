Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your age, income, tax situation, and retirement goals. Here are some key considerations for each account type:

### 1. **401(k) Accounts**
   - **Benefits**: Contributions are often tax-deferred, meaning you don't pay taxes until withdrawals in retirement. Employer matching contributions can boost savings.
   - **Strategy**: Maximize employer matching first, then contribute enough to meet your personal savings goal. Aim to contribute at least 15% of your salary if possible.

### 2. **IRA Accounts (Traditional and Roth)**
   - **Traditional IRA**: Tax-deferred, with potential for upfront tax deduction.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.
   - **Strategy**: If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, consider maxing out your Roth IRA first. If you need a tax deduction now, prioritize your Traditional IRA. 

### 3. **Taxable Accounts**
   - **Benefits**: Flexibility to access funds at any time without penalties; investments can include stocks, bonds, and mutual funds.
   - **Strategy**: Use these accounts for short-term goals or to take advantage of tax-efficient investments like index funds or ETFs. Consider tax-loss harvesting to offset gains.

### **General Distribution Strategy**
1. **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have 3-6 months of expenses in a liquid savings account before heavy investments.
2. **Employer Match**: Put enough in your 401(k) to get the full employer match.
3. **Retirement Accounts**: Max out IRA contributions next, focusing on a Roth if you qualify.
4. **Taxable Accounts**: Once retirement accounts are maxed out or if you need liquidity, invest in taxable accounts.

### **Final Tips**
- Regularly review and adjust your contributions based on life changes or market conditions.
- Consider consulting a financial advisor for personalized advice.
