When considering the best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, it's essential to take into account a few important factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon. Here’s a breakdown of each option with its pros and cons:

### 401(k) Accounts
**Pros:**
- Often comes with employer matching, which is essentially "free money."
- Contributions lower your taxable income for the year.
- Investments grow tax-deferred until withdrawal.

**Cons:**
- Limited investment choices, often restricted to a preset list.
- Early withdrawal penalties if taken out before age 59½.
  
### IRA (Individual Retirement Account)
**Pros:**
- More investment flexibility compared to a 401(k).
- Can choose between a Traditional IRA (tax-deferred) or a Roth IRA (tax-free withdrawals in retirement).
- Tax benefits, depending on the type of IRA.

**Cons:**
- Contribution limits are lower than 401(k) limits.
- Income limits may affect your ability to contribute to a Roth IRA.

### Taxable Accounts
**Pros:**
- No contribution limits or withdrawal restrictions, allowing for more flexibility.
- You can access the funds at any time for any purpose without penalty.
- Can take advantage of tax-loss harvesting.

**Cons:**
- Capital gains are taxed, which can reduce investment returns.
- No tax-deferred growth.

### Distribution Strategy
1. **Maximize Employer Match:** First, contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match, as this is essentially free money.

2. **Contribute to IRA:** Next, consider contributing to a Traditional or Roth IRA. If eligible, the Roth IRA might be advantageous for tax-free growth and withdrawals.

3. **Continue 401(k) Contributions:** After maximizing your IRA contributions, resume contributing to your 401(k) if you have additional savings.

4. **Add to Taxable Accounts:** Once you're maxing out tax-advantaged accounts, you can invest in taxable accounts. This is particularly useful if you need flexible access to funds or expect to be in a lower tax bracket during retirement.

### Final Considerations
- **Age and Risk Tolerance:** Younger individuals typically should aim for higher stock allocations, while those nearing retirement might shift towards more conservative investments.
- **Tax Efficiency:** Diversifying across account types can help manage tax implications both now and in retirement.

Consulting a financial advisor can provide personalized advice tailored to your specific financial situation and goals. Happy investing!
