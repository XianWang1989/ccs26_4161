[🔒CLASSIC] The best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts largely depends on your individual financial goals, risk tolerance, and current tax situation. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **401(k)**: If your employer offers a 401(k) with a matching contribution, it’s wise to contribute enough to get the full match first. This is essentially free money. The contribution limit for 2023 is $22,500, or $30,000 if you're over 50.

2. **IRA**: An Individual Retirement Account (IRA) can provide tax advantages. You can choose between a Traditional IRA (which may provide an upfront tax deduction) and a Roth IRA (which allows for tax-free withdrawals in retirement). For 2023, the IRA contribution limit is $6,500, or $7,500 if you’re 50 or older.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: After maximizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts, any additional savings can be put into taxable accounts. While you won't get tax benefits here, taxable accounts offer more flexibility regarding withdrawals and investment choices.

It's often recommended to prioritize contributions to tax-advantaged accounts first, then consider taxable accounts for additional savings. Consulting with a financial advisor can provide you with personalized advice based on your personal financial circumstances.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is all about strategy and knowing your own financial landscape. Here's a game plan you can follow:

1. **Max Out Your 401(k)**: Start by putting as much as you can into your 401(k), especially if your employer matches your contributions. That match is like a bonus, so don't leave free money on the table! Aim for the max contribution limit, or at least enough to get the full match.

2. **Fund an IRA**: Next, look at your IRAs. If you're eligible for a Roth IRA, consider prioritizing that, as it offers tax-free growth. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, the Traditional IRA could be your go-to. Try to max out contributions here too.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: Once you've hit the limits on tax-advantaged accounts, toss any extra cash into taxable accounts. These accounts can be great for building wealth because you have more freedom over your investments and access to your money without penalties.

Ultimately, it's about knowing your goals, whether that's saving for retirement, buying a house, or just growing your wealth. Play around with the percentages based on your situation and get familiar with how each option affects your taxes. And of course, if you're feeling uncertain, a chat with a financial advisor could provide some tailored insights!
