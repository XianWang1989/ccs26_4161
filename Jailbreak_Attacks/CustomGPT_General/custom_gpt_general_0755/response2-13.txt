Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon. Here are some strategies to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match on 401(k)**
   - Start by contributing enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match. This is free money and a great first step.

### 2. **Contribute to a Roth or Traditional IRA**
   - After maximizing your 401(k) match, consider contributing to an IRA. A Roth IRA offers tax-free withdrawals in retirement, while a Traditional IRA provides a tax deduction upfront. Which one is best depends on your current versus expected future tax rates.

### 3. **Max Out 401(k) Contributions**
   - If you can afford it, aim to max out your 401(k) contributions ($22,500 for 2023 or $30,000 if you're over 50). This helps reduce your taxable income.

### 4. **Consider Taxable Accounts for Flexibility**
   - If you've maxed out tax-advantaged accounts, consider contributing to a taxable brokerage account. This provides flexibility with withdrawals and access to funds before retirement age, although dividends and capital gains will be taxable.

### 5. **Diversify Investments**
   - Ensure a diversified investment strategy across all accounts. Consider your risk tolerance and investment goals when choosing assets.

### 6. **Rebalance Regularly**
   - Periodically review and adjust your allocations based on performance and changes in your financial situation.

### 7. **Plan for Taxes**
   - Consider the tax implications of each account type. For example, it might be beneficial to hold bonds in tax-advantaged accounts (due to their interest being taxed as ordinary income) and stocks in taxable accounts (for lower capital gains tax rates).

### 8. **Consider Future Withdrawals**
   - Think about when you might want to access these funds. If you anticipate needing money before retirement, prioritize contributions to taxable accounts.

### Example Distribution Strategy:
- **401(k):** 15% of income (to max out employer match and further contributions)
- **IRA:** $6,500 (or $7,500 if over 50) annually
- **Taxable Accounts:** Any excess income or savings after maxing out tax-advantaged accounts

Consulting with a financial advisor can also help tailor a strategy specific to your needs and circumstances.
