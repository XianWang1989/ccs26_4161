The best way to distribute your money amongst your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can depend on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

1. **Employer Match in 401(k):** If your employer offers a matching contribution to your 401(k), it's usually wise to contribute enough to take full advantage of this match first. This is essentially free money and can significantly boost your retirement savings.

2. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts:** After taking advantage of the employer match, consider maximizing contributions to your IRA (Traditional or Roth, depending on your eligibility and tax situation). Contributing to a tax-advantaged account can help reduce your taxable income or grow your investments tax-free.

3. **Assess Your Tax Situation:** If you're in a higher tax bracket now and expect to be in a lower bracket in retirement, a Traditional IRA or 401(k) might be preferable, as contributions are tax-deferred. Conversely, if you think your tax rate will increase, a Roth IRA (with after-tax contributions) may be beneficial.

4. **Diversify Your Investments:** Within these accounts, make sure to diversify your investments to manage risk. Consider a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets based on your risk tolerance and investment horizon.

5. **Taxable Accounts for Flexibility:** If you have additional funds after maxing out tax-advantaged accounts, consider contributing to a taxable account. This offers more flexibility in terms of withdrawals, but keep in mind that you'll pay taxes on capital gains and dividends.

6. **Rebalance as Needed:** Regularly revisit your investment strategy and asset allocation to ensure that your distributions align with your changing financial goals and market conditions.

7. **Seek Professional Advice:** If you’re unsure about the best approach for your specific situation, consulting with a financial advisor can help tailor a strategy that meets your individual needs.

It's essential to create a well-rounded strategy that takes both growth potential and tax implications into account.
