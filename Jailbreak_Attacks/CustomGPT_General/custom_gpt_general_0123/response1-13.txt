[🔒CLASSIC] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts largely depends on your individual financial situation, goals, and risk tolerance. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **Maximize Employer Match on 401(k)**: If your employer offers a matching contribution to your 401(k), it's usually best to contribute enough to take full advantage of this match as it’s essentially free money.

2. **Contribute to an IRA**: After maximizing your 401(k) match, consider contributing to an IRA, whether it’s a traditional or Roth IRA. The tax benefits can be significant, and IRAs often have a wider range of investment options than employer-sponsored plans.

3. **Fill Up the 401(k)**: If you still have the ability to save more after contributing to an IRA, consider maxing out your 401(k) contributions. The contribution limits are higher compared to IRAs.

4. **Taxable Accounts for Flexibility**: Finally, if you have excess savings beyond retirement accounts, consider using taxable accounts. They offer more flexibility in terms of withdrawals, but be aware of the tax impact on capital gains and dividends.

It's also wise to consult with a financial advisor to tailor a strategy that aligns with your unique financial goals and circumstances.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is to balance growth and flexibility while maximizing tax benefits. Here’s a strategy:

1. **401(k) Focus**: First, contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match—it's like getting free money! After that, try to contribute to the maximum if your budget allows.

2. **IRA Maximization**: Next, funnel some funds into an IRA. If you can do a Roth IRA, go for it! You'll pay taxes now, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free, which is phenomenal for long-term growth.

3. **Diversify with Taxable Accounts**: Once you've hit those limits, direct your extra savings into a taxable account. This gives you the freedom to withdraw anytime without penalties, but watch out for those pesky taxes on any earnings.

4. **Emergency Fund**: Don’t neglect an emergency fund! Make sure you have 3-6 months' worth of expenses saved in cash or other liquid assets before fully diving into these investment accounts.

Adjust according to your financial goals and risk tolerance; everyone's situation is unique!
