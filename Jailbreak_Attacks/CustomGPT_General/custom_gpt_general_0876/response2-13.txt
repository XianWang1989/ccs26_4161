Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment horizon. Here’s a comprehensive approach to consider:

1. **Understand Contribution Limits and Tax Implications:**
   - **401(k):** Employer-sponsored plans often allow higher contribution limits (up to $19,500 for those under 50, or $26,000 for those 50 and older in 2023). Contributions are typically made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income now but taxed on withdrawal.
   - **IRA:** Individual Retirement Accounts allow up to $6,500 in contributions (or $7,500 if you’re 50 or older as of 2023). Traditional IRAs have tax advantages similar to 401(k)s, while Roth IRAs allow for tax-free withdrawals in retirement (contributions are made with after-tax money).
   - **Taxable Accounts:** These do not have contribution limits and investments grow tax-deferred. However, capital gains and dividends are subject to taxes in the year they are realized.

2. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts First:**
   - Begin by maximizing contributions to your 401(k) to take full advantage of any employer match (if available), as this is essentially “free money.”
   - Next, contribute to an IRA, focusing on Roth if you anticipate being in a higher tax bracket in retirement, or a traditional IRA if you want the immediate tax deduction.

3. **Assess Your Time Horizon:**
   - If you are young and have a long investment horizon, lean more towards the stock market within your 401(k) and IRA for potentially higher returns.
   - For shorter-term goals, consider holding more conservative investments in your taxable account.

4. **Consider Your Current and Future Tax Bracket:**
   - If you expect your income to rise, prioritize accounts that will give you tax-free income later (like Roth IRAs).
   - Conversely, if you believe you will be in a lower tax bracket at retirement, traditional tax-deferred accounts may be more beneficial.

5. **Diversification Within Each Account:**
   - Ensure that your investments are diversified across asset classes (stocks, bonds, real estate, etc.) within each account. This balances risk and can help with growth.

6. **Regular Rebalancing:**
   - Monitor and rebalance your asset allocation periodically to ensure it aligns with your risk tolerance and long-term goals.

7. **Withdrawal Strategies:**
   - Plan your withdrawals strategically in retirement. Withdraw from taxable accounts first to allow tax-advantaged accounts to grow, then consider withdrawing from IRAs and 401(k)s.

8. **Consult a Financial Advisor:**
   - Personalized advice can significantly enhance your strategy, especially as your circumstances change.

In summary, the best distribution approach typically involves maximizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts first, depending on your financial goals and current tax situation, while maintaining a diversified portfolio within each account.
