When considering the distribution of your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, it's important to review the specific characteristics and tax implications of each option:

1. **401(k) Plan**:
   - **Tax Benefits**: Contributions are made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income for the year. Taxes are deferred until withdrawal.
   - **Employer Match**: If your employer offers a matching contribution, aim to contribute enough to get the full match, as this is essentially "free money."
   - **Contribution Limits**: The contribution limit for 2023 is $22,500, with an additional $7,500 catch-up contribution if you're over 50.

2. **IRA (Individual Retirement Account)**:
   - **Types**: You can choose between a Traditional IRA (pre-tax contributions, taxed upon withdrawal) and a Roth IRA (after-tax contributions, tax-free withdrawals).
   - **Contribution Limits**: The limit for 2023 is $6,500, with a $1,000 catch-up contribution for those over 50.
   - **Flexibility**: Roth IRAs provide tax-free growth, and you can withdraw contributions without penalties, providing more flexibility for early withdrawal compared to a 401(k).

3. **Taxable Accounts**:
   - **Tax Treatment**: Investments grow without tax until realized. You pay capital gains taxes on profits and taxes on dividends and interest.
   - **Liquidity**: These accounts offer the most flexibility in terms of access to funds without penalties, making them suitable for shorter-term investments or emergency funds.

### Suggested Strategy:
1. **Maximize 401(k) Contributions**: Contribute enough to get any employer match first.
2. **Contribute to an IRA**: If eligible, contribute to a Roth IRA for tax-free growth or a Traditional IRA for pre-tax benefits, depending on your tax strategy.
3. **Use Taxable Accounts for Additional Investments**: Once you've maximized tax-advantaged accounts, consider taxable accounts for additional investment flexibility, especially for shorter-term goals.

### Considerations:
- **Current and Future Tax Brackets**: Evaluate your current income level and expected retirement income to help decide between Traditional and Roth options.
- **Diversification of Tax Risk**: Using a combination of tax-deferred, tax-free, and taxable accounts can provide tax diversification, giving you greater flexibility in retirement.

It’s essential to consult with a financial advisor for personalized advice based on your specific financial situation and goals.
