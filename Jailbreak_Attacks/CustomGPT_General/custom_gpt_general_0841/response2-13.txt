Distributing your money across a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can depend on various factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here’s a general guide to help you consider your options:

1. **Maximize Employer Match (401(k))**: If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), contribute at least enough to get the full match. This is essentially free money.

2. **Tax-Deferred Growth (401(k) and IRA)**: 
   - **401(k)**: Contributions reduce your taxable income, and investments grow tax-deferred until you withdraw them in retirement.
   - **Traditional IRA**: Similar benefits as a 401(k) but with different contribution limits. If eligible, consider contributing to this account as well.
   - **Roth IRA**: If you’re eligible based on income limits, consider a Roth IRA for tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. This can be particularly beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.

3. **Evaluate Taxable Accounts**: 
   - Once you’ve maximized tax-advantaged accounts (like your 401(k) and IRAs), consider contributing to a taxable account. This account offers more flexibility for withdrawals but doesn't provide the same tax benefits.
   - In taxable accounts, invest in tax-efficient funds to minimize tax liability. Consider holding investments long enough to benefit from lower long-term capital gains tax rates.

4. **Asset Allocation**: Focus on a diversified investment strategy based on your risk tolerance and investment horizon:
   - **Stocks vs. Bonds**: Depending on your age and risk tolerance, a higher allocation to stocks may be suitable for long-term growth.
   - **Diversification**: Within your accounts, diversify your investments across asset classes and sectors to mitigate risk.

5. **Regular Review and Rebalance**: Revisit your investment strategy periodically. As you approach retirement, gradually shift towards more conservative investments to protect your capital.

6. **Consider Tax Implications**: Keep in mind the taxation of withdrawals from different accounts. For example, withdrawals from a traditional 401(k) and IRA are taxable, while qualified withdrawals from a Roth account are tax-free.

7. **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have an adequate emergency fund outside these accounts before investing heavily.

It's advisable to consult with a financial advisor or tax professional to create a strategy tailored to your specific situation and goals.
