It's important to consider your individual financial situation, goals, and the tax implications of each type of account. Here’s a general framework:

### 1. **Prioritize Contributions:**
   - **401(k):** If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to get the full match first. This is essentially free money.
   - **IRA:** After maximizing any 401(k) match, consider contributing to an IRA (either Traditional or Roth, depending on your tax situation).
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Use these for investments after maximizing tax-advantaged accounts.

### 2. **Account Types:**
   - **401(k):** Good for higher contribution limits and potential employer matches. However, you'll pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement.
   - **IRA:** Offers tax-deferred growth (Traditional) or tax-free withdrawals (Roth, if eligible). Contribution limits are generally lower than 401(k)s.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Flexibility for withdrawals without penalties, but you'll pay capital gains taxes on profits.

### 3. **Investment Strategy:**
   - Consider a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets suitable for your risk tolerance.
   - Typically, you might hold more aggressive investments in tax-advantaged accounts (to defer taxes) and more conservative ones in taxable accounts (to manage capital gains taxes).

### 4. **Adjust Over Time:**
   - As you approach retirement, consider shifting towards more conservative investments, especially in your tax-deferred accounts.

### 5. **Seek Professional Guidance:**
   - Consult with a financial advisor to tailor this strategy to your specific needs and to ensure you’re making the most tax-efficient decisions.

Ultimately, the best allocation will depend on your current financial situation, risk tolerance, and future goals.
