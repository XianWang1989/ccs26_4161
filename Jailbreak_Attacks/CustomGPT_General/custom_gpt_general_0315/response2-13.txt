When it comes to distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, a balanced and strategic approach can help you maximize your investment potential and manage taxes effectively. Here are some key considerations:

1. **401(k) Contributions**:
   - **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to take full advantage of it—this is essentially free money.
   - **Contribution Limits**: For 2023, the contribution limit for a 401(k) is $22,500 (or $30,000 if you're over 50). Aim to contribute as much as you can afford, especially if you're within striking distance of those limits.

2. **IRA Contributions**:
   - **Consider Roth vs. Traditional**: A Roth IRA allows for tax-free withdrawals in retirement, whereas a Traditional IRA offers tax deductions now with taxable withdrawals later. Your income level and tax situation will help dictate which is better for you.
   - **Contribution Limits**: The contribution limit for IRAs in 2023 is $6,500 (or $7,500 if you're over 50). If you have the means, aim to contribute fully.

3. **Taxable Accounts**:
   - **Invest for Flexibility**: Money in taxable accounts can be accessed at any time without penalties, offering more flexibility for short-term investments or emergency funds.
   - **Tax Efficiency**: Invest in tax-efficient assets, like qualified dividends and long-term capital gains, to minimize your tax burden.

4. **Diversification Across Accounts**:
   - Balance asset types (stocks, bonds, etc.) across all accounts instead of trying to have a perfect balance within each account. For example, you might hold more aggressive investments in your 401(k) and IRA, while using your taxable account for more conservative or dividend-paying investments.

5. **Revisit Regularly**:
   - Reassess your allocation regularly based on changes in your financial situation, investment goals, and market conditions. Adjust your contributions accordingly.

6. **Seek Professional Advice**:
   - Consider consulting a financial advisor for personalized advice based on your specific financial situation and goals.

By considering these factors, you can create a well-rounded and effective distribution strategy among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts.
