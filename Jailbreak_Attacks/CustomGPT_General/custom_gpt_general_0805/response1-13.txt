When considering how to distribute your investments among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, here are some key principles to guide your decisions:

1. **Tax-Advantaged Accounts (401(k) and IRA)**:
   - **401(k)**: Typically allows for pre-tax contributions, reducing your taxable income for the year. If your employer offers a matching contribution, try to contribute enough to get the full match—it's essentially free money.
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**: 
     - **Traditional IRA**: Contributions may be tax-deductible, with taxes due upon withdrawal in retirement.
     - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free, making it an excellent option for tax diversification.

2. **Taxable Accounts**:
   - Investments in these accounts are subject to capital gains taxes when you sell. However, they offer flexibility in terms of access to funds without penalties for early withdrawal, unlike retirement accounts.

3. **General Strategy for Distribution**:
   - **Maximize Contributions**: Aim to contribute to your 401(k) to capture the full employer match first, then consider maxing out your IRA if possible.
   - **Asset Location**: Place more tax-efficient investments (like index funds or ETFs) in taxable accounts to minimize tax exposure. Conversely, hold less tax-efficient investments (like bonds or actively managed funds) in tax-advantaged accounts.
   - **Withdrawals and Income Strategy**: Consider your expected tax situation in retirement. If you anticipate being in a higher tax bracket, it might be beneficial to prioritize Roth accounts (for tax-free withdrawals) in retirement.

4. **Review and Adjust**: Regularly review your investment performance and tax strategy to ensure it aligns with your evolving financial goals and tax situation.

Ultimately, the best distribution strategy depends on your individual financial goals, current income level, tax situation, and investment horizon. It’s wise to consult a financial advisor for personalized guidance.
