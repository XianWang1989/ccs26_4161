Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here are some guidelines to consider for each account type:

### 1. **401(k) Accounts**
   - **Employer Match:** Contribute enough to get the full employer match if offered; it’s essentially free money.
   - **Pre-Tax Contributions:** Contributions are often made pre-tax, lowering your taxable income for the year. This is beneficial if you're currently in a higher tax bracket.
   - **Investment Choices:** Be aware of the limited investment options available in your plan.

### 2. **Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)**
   - **Roth vs. Traditional:** 
     - **Traditional IRA:** Contributions may be tax-deductible, but withdrawals are taxed in retirement. 
     - **Roth IRA:** Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free. This is ideal if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Contribution Limits:** Keep in mind the annual contribution limits, which can influence how much you allocate here.

### 3. **Taxable Accounts**
   - **Flexibility:** These accounts offer more flexibility with withdrawals and fewer penalties for accessing your money.
   - **Capital Gains Tax:** Be mindful of potential taxes on dividends and capital gains; consider holding investments longer to benefit from lower long-term capital gains rates.
   - **Emergency Fund:** You might consider this account for short-term savings goals or as an emergency fund.

### **General Strategies**
- **Diversification:** Ensure a diversified investment portfolio across all accounts to spread risk.
- **Tax Efficiency:** Prioritize tax-advantaged accounts (401(k) and IRA) for long-term savings, then allocate excess funds to taxable accounts.
- **Age and Risk Tolerance:** Your age, risk tolerance, and retirement timeline significantly influence how aggressively you should allocate funds. Younger investors might lean more into stocks for growth, while those closer to retirement may prefer bonds or conservative investments.

### **Conclusion**
A typical strategy might start with maximizing your employer match in the 401(k), followed by contributing to an IRA (considering the benefits of Roth vs. Traditional), and then funneling any additional savings into a taxable account for flexibility. Consulting with a financial advisor can also provide personalized advice based on your unique situation.
