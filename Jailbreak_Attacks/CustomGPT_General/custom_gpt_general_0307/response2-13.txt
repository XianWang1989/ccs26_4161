Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here’s a general guideline to help you create an effective strategy:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**
   - **Contribute enough to get the full employer match.** This is essentially free money and a great way to start.

### 2. **Consider Tax Implications**
   - **401(k) and IRA Contributions**: These are typically tax-deferred. Contributions reduce your taxable income now, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
   - **Roth Accounts**: If eligible, consider contributing to a Roth IRA or Roth 401(k). Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

### 3. **Prioritize High-Interest Debt**
   - Pay off high-interest debt before aggressively funding retirement accounts, as the interest on such debt usually exceeds potential investment returns.

### 4. **Set a Contribution Hierarchy**
   - **401(k)**: Maximize the employer match, then contribute aggressively until you reach the annual limit (currently $22,500, or $30,000 if age 50+ in 2024).
   - **IRA**: Once you’re contributing enough to the 401(k), consider maxing out a traditional or Roth IRA (currently $6,500, or $7,500 if age 50+).
   - **Taxable Accounts**: After maxing out tax-advantaged accounts, consider investing in a taxable brokerage account for additional flexibility and access to funds.

### 5. **Diversification and Asset Allocation**
   - Ensure your investment strategy is diversified across asset classes (stocks, bonds, etc.) based on your risk tolerance and timeline. Adjust the allocation based on the specific accounts.
   - Generally, you might want to hold more aggressive investments (like stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts and safer investments (like bonds) in taxable accounts due to the tax implications on dividends and capital gains.

### 6. **Keep an Eye on Your Tax Bracket**
   - Distributing investments strategically can help you manage your tax bracket both now and in retirement. Withdrawing from taxable accounts might help keep you in a lower tax bracket during retirement.

### 7. **Regular Review and Rebalance**
   - Regularly review your portfolio and rebalance your accounts as needed based on performance and changing financial goals.

### Final Thoughts
Consulting with a financial advisor can provide personalized insights based on your specific situation and goals. Using tools and calculators available online can also help you visualize the outcomes based on different scenarios.
