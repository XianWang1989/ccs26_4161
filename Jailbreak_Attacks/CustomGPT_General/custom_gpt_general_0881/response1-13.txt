When determining the best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following factors:

1. **Tax Treatment**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are typically made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income for the year. Taxes are paid upon withdrawal during retirement. This is beneficial if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement.
   - **IRA (Traditional and Roth)**: Traditional IRAs offer tax-deferred growth similar to a 401(k), while Roth IRAs provide tax-free withdrawals in retirement (funded with after-tax contributions). Depending on your current income and expected future income, choose between these carefully.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investment income and capital gains are taxed in the year they are realized. This could be suitable for savings goals with a shorter time horizon and for investments expected to yield lower tax implications.

2. **Contribution Limits**: 
   - Each account type has specific contribution limits. Ensure you are maximizing any employer matching contributions in your 401(k) first, as this is essentially "free money." 

3. **Retirement Goals and Timeline**:
   - Assess your short-term vs. long-term financial goals. If you need the money in the near future, it might be wiser to keep it in a taxable account where you can access it without penalties. For long-term retirement savings, prioritize 401(k) and IRA contributions.

4. **Investment Strategy**:
   - Diversification is important. Consider the types of investments each account holds and ensure they align with your overall risk tolerance and investment strategy. For example, you might want to hold more aggressive investments in your tax-advantaged accounts and more conservative investments in taxable accounts.

5. **Withdrawal Strategies**: 
   - Understand how and when you plan to withdraw funds. Different strategies can be employed for withdrawing from tax-advantaged accounts, considering factors like required minimum distributions (RMDs) for IRAs and 401(k)s after age 72.

6. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: 
   - Given the complexities involved in tax implications and personal financial circumstances, it may be worthwhile to consult with a certified financial planner or tax advisor.

In conclusion, a balanced approach often includes maximizing employer-sponsored plans and using tax-advantaged accounts for retirement savings while using taxable accounts for flexibility in short-term needs. Adjust your strategy as your financial situation and goals evolve.
