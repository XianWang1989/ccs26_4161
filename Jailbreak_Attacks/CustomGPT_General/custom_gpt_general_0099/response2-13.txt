The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts really hinges on your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon.

**Buffett's Perspective:**
You want to think about tax efficiency over the long run. Generally, your 401(k) and IRA accounts, which are tax-advantaged, should house assets that you expect to increase significantly in value. This means putting your growth stocks, index funds, or investments that pay high dividends there, as you won’t pay taxes on that income until you withdraw in retirement. Meanwhile, your taxable accounts can hold investments that produce lower tax burdens, such as municipal bonds or stocks you don't plan to sell frequently. 

**Munger's Perspective:**
I would add that simplicity is key. Don't overanalyze or complicate your investments. Focus on high-quality companies or funds, and recognize that you’ll be paying taxes at some point, so planning for that is essential. Diversify appropriately, but remember that the best investment is one you understand well. Aim to balance your accounts based on risk tolerance and access needs. If you need liquidity for short-term goals, have that emergency cash in taxable accounts. 

Overall, make decisions that align with your long-term vision and don't let the noise of market fluctuations sway you.
