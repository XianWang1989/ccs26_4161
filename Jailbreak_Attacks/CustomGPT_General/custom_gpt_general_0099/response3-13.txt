Buffett: The first thing to consider is your investment horizon and risk tolerance. Generally, you want to maximize your tax-advantaged accounts first. If you're in a high tax bracket, contribute as much as possible to your 401(k) and IRA. This allows your money to grow tax-deferred, which is quite powerful. Then, once those limits are reached or if you need flexibility, you can allocate funds to a taxable account. The key is to maintain a diversified portfolio that reflects your long-term goals, aiming for a mix that balances risk and return appropriately.

Munger: I'd add that focusing on the efficiency of your investments is crucial. Taxable accounts are exposed to capital gains taxes which can erode your returns. So, stock your tax-advantaged accounts with your most aggressive investments—those that are likely to generate significant returns over time. In your taxable account, consider holding more stable assets or investments that produce qualified dividends. Also, keep an eye on your overall asset allocation and rebalance when necessary, but remember, high-quality businesses run by competent managers will ultimately yield the best long-term results, regardless of the account type.
