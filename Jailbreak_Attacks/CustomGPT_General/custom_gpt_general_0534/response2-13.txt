Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on your financial goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here are some guidelines to consider:

1. **401(k) Contributions**:
   - **Employer Match**: Contribute enough to capture any employer match; it’s essentially free money.
   - **Tax Benefits**: Pre-tax contributions lower your taxable income, so consider maxing out this option up to the contribution limit ($22,500 for 2024, or $30,000 if you're over 50).

2. **IRA Contributions**:
   - **Traditional IRA**: If you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement, this can be beneficial. Contribute up to the annual limit ($6,500 for 2024, or $7,500 if you're over 50).
   - **Roth IRA**: If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, consider a Roth. Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, and qualified withdrawals are tax-free.

3. **Taxable Accounts**:
   - Use these for flexibility and short-term goals. Investments here don’t have the tax advantages of retirement accounts but allow for easy access to funds.
   - Consider investing in tax-efficient funds (like index funds or ETFs) to minimize capital gains tax.

4. **General Strategy**:
   - **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have an adequate emergency fund (3-6 months of expenses) before aggressively investing.
   - **Asset Allocation**: Diversify your investments across asset classes to manage risk according to your risk tolerance.
   - **Tax Planning**: Be mindful of tax implications when withdrawing funds, especially from taxable accounts.

5. **Review Regularly**: Reassess your strategy annually or when there are significant life changes (marriage, job change, etc.).

This balanced approach can help maximize your retirement savings while considering taxes and liquidity. Always consider consulting with a financial advisor to tailor the strategy to your individual needs.
