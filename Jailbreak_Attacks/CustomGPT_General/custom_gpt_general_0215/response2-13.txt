The best way to distribute your money amongst your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on your individual financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here’s a breakdown to help you make an informed decision:

1. **401(k) Accounts**:
   - **Benefits**: Often offer employer matching contributions, which is essentially “free money.” Contributions are made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income now.
   - **Strategy**: Max out contributions to get the full employer match first. After that, consider the investment options available in your 401(k) and ensure they're aligned with your risk tolerance and investment goals.

2. **IRA (Individual Retirement Account)**:
   - **Types**: You can choose between a Traditional IRA (tax-deductible contributions, taxes paid on withdrawals) and a Roth IRA (contributions are taxed, but withdrawals are tax-free if certain conditions are met). 
   - **Benefits**: A Roth IRA offers tax-free growth, which can be beneficial if you expect your tax rate to be higher in retirement.
   - **Strategy**: If eligible, contribute to a Roth IRA for tax-free withdrawals later, or maximize a Traditional IRA if you need the immediate tax deduction.

3. **Taxable Accounts**:
   - **Benefits**: More flexibility in terms of withdrawals, investments, and no contribution limits. However, dividends and capital gains are subject to taxes.
   - **Strategy**: Allocate funds here after maxing out tax-advantaged accounts. It’s ideal for short-term goals, emergency funds, or investments that generate qualified dividends (taxed at a lower rate).

### Overall Distribution Strategy:
- **Emergency Savings First**: Ensure you have an emergency fund before allocating significant funds to retirement accounts.
- **Maximize Employer Match**: Invest enough in your 401(k) to get any employer match provided.
- **Consider Tax Implications**: Balance contributions between pre-tax (401(k), Traditional IRA) and post-tax accounts (Roth IRA, taxable) based on your anticipated future tax situation.
- **Rebalance Regularly**: Assess your investments at least annually to ensure your asset allocation aligns with your risk tolerance and financial goals.

### General Rule:
A common guideline is the “age in bonds” rule for asset allocation: if you're 30, aim for 30% in bonds; the rest can be in stocks across the accounts, factoring in tax implications for optimal placement. 

Ultimately, consider consulting a financial advisor to tailor a plan that aligns with your financial goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon.
