Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on your financial goals, tax considerations, and investment timeline. Here are some general principles to consider:

### 1. **Prioritize Accounts Based on Tax Advantages:**
   - **401(k):** If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to get the full match—it’s essentially free money. Maximize contributions if you can.
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth):** If eligible, prioritize a Roth IRA for tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement, or a Traditional IRA for tax-deferred growth. Consider your current vs. expected future tax rates.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Use for accessibility or investing goals that don't fit in retirement accounts. They have fewer tax advantages, but withdrawals are not restricted by age.

### 2. **Contributions Limits:**
   - Be aware of annual contribution limits for each account type ($22,500 for 401(k) in 2023, $6,500 for IRA). Beyond these limits, consider taxable accounts.

### 3. **Asset Allocation:**
   - Place higher-growth, potentially more volatile investments (like stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts to defer or eliminate taxes on gains.
   - Consider holding more stable investments (like bonds) in taxable accounts, where you may realize lower capital gains.

### 4. **Withdrawals Strategies:**
   - Plan withdrawal strategies based on your tax bracket. Generally, use taxable accounts first for liquidity, then tax-deferred accounts, and lastly, tax-free accounts.

### 5. **Long-Term Planning:**
   - Regularly review your investment strategy based on changing tax laws, retirement needs, and financial goals. Adjust contributions and asset allocation as needed.

### 6. **Consult a Financial Advisor:**
   - Consider professional advice tailored to your specific circumstances, especially if you have complex financial needs or tax issues.

### Conclusion
The best distribution strategy balances maximizing tax advantages and aligning with your financial goals. Regularly reviewing this allocation can help you adapt to changes in income, tax laws, and retirement planning.
