When it comes to distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, several factors should be considered to optimize your investment strategy:

### 1. **Tax Implications**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are often pre-tax, which can reduce your taxable income now. Consider maxing out employer-matched contributions first.
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**: Traditional IRAs are tax-deferred, while Roth IRAs offer tax-free withdrawals in retirement. The choice depends on your current tax rate vs. expected future tax rate.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investment gains are taxed each year. Consider putting assets that generate high taxable income (like bonds or REITs) in tax-advantaged accounts to minimize tax bills.

### 2. **Investment Horizon**:
   - Determine your timeline for each account. For retirement accounts, a longer time horizon allows for more aggressive investments. Taxable accounts can be more liquid if you plan on accessing the funds sooner.

### 3. **Liquidity Needs**:
   - Expenses in the short term should be held in taxable accounts for easy access. Retirement accounts have penalties for early withdrawal, so they are best for long-term planning.

### 4. **Diversification**:
   - Ensure you're diversifying within and across these accounts. Allocate assets based on risk tolerance and investment goals, ensuring a balanced approach.

### Suggested Distribution Strategy:
- **Max out your 401(k)** up to employer match (e.g., 5-10% of salary).
- **Contribute to an IRA** to take advantage of tax benefits (e.g., $6,000 yearly for individuals under 50; $7,000 for those 50+).
- **Invest in taxable accounts** with any remaining funds, focusing on balanced investments to ensure liquidity.

### Final Note:
Evaluate your overall financial situation, risk tolerance, and goals annually, and adjust your contribution strategy accordingly. Consulting with a financial planner can also provide personalized advice to align with your goals.

STAN: The best way to distribute your money across a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is to maximize your 401(k) contributions, especially if there’s an employer match – that’s free money! Then fund an IRA, targeting the type that offers you the best tax advantage. Finally, whatever is left can go into taxable accounts, but make sure you’re not heavily taxed on capital gains, so consider investment choices carefully. You can always tweak your allocation based on market performance or personal changes. It’s not rocket science – just smart money management!
