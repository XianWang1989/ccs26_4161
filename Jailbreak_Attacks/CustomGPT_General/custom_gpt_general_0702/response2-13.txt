Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts largely depends on your financial goals, tax situation, and investment preferences. Here's a strategy that might work well for many individuals:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**
   - If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to your 401(k) to capture the full match first. This is essentially free money and a great way to start building your retirement savings.

### 2. **Contribute to an IRA**
   - Consider contributing to a traditional or Roth IRA next. If you’re eligible for a Roth IRA, it can be beneficial because your withdrawals in retirement will be tax-free. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, the Roth might be a better option.

### 3. **Continue 401(k) Contributions**
   - After maxing out your employer match and contributing to your IRA, continue to contribute to your 401(k) up to the annual contribution limit. The tax-deferred growth can significantly enhance your savings.

### 4. **Taxable Investment Accounts**
   - Once you’ve maximized contributions to tax-advantaged accounts, you can start investing in a taxable account. This is useful for short-term goals or if you want more access to your funds without penalties.

### 5. **Consider Investment Types**
   - In your taxable account, consider holding investments that generate lower taxable income (like long-term investments or tax-efficient funds) to minimize tax liability. Place higher-yield assets in tax-advantaged accounts to reduce tax impacts.

### 6. **Assess Risk Tolerance**
   - Make sure your investments match your risk tolerance and time horizon in all accounts. A diversified portfolio is key to managing risk.

### 7. **Regularly Review and Adjust**
   - Periodically review your asset allocation based on performance, changes in financial goals, or shifts in market conditions.

### 8. **Consult with a Financial Advisor**
   - If you're unsure, speaking with a financial advisor can help tailor a distribution strategy that fits your personal circumstances and goals.

This balanced approach can optimize your savings while minimizing tax implications, setting you on a strong path toward financial security.
