To effectively distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

### 1. **Understand Contribution Limits and Tax Benefits:**
   - **401(k)**: Typically allows higher contribution limits. Contributions are pre-tax, reducing taxable income.
   - **IRA (Traditional and Roth)**: Lower contribution limits. Traditional IRAs provide tax deductions, while Roth IRAs allow for tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: No contribution limits, but you'll pay taxes on dividends, interest, and capital gains.

### 2. **Prioritize Tax Advantages:**
   - **Max Out Employer Match**: Always contribute enough to your 401(k) to get any employer match—this is essentially free money.
   - **Fund Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: After getting the match, consider fully funding your IRA (especially a Roth if you're eligible, due to its tax-free growth).

### 3. **Consider Investment Goals and Time Horizon:**
   - Allocate more aggressively in your 401(k) and IRA, which are long-term savings vehicles, while keeping a diversified portfolio in taxable accounts for potential shorter-term needs.

### 4. **Keep an Eye on Fees and Investment Choices:**
   - 401(k) plans can have limited investment options and higher fees compared to IRAs and taxable accounts. Review these regularly to ensure you're making the best choices.

### 5. **Assess Your Tax Situation:**
   - If you're in a higher tax bracket now and expect to be in a lower bracket during retirement, consider contributing more to a traditional IRA or 401(k). Conversely, if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, a Roth IRA may be advantageous.

### 6. **Regularly Rebalance and Review Your Strategy:**
   - Periodically review your allocation based on changing financial goals, market conditions, and your age.

### Sample Allocation Strategy:
- 401(k): Max out contributions (up to employer match and higher limits).
- IRA: Fully fund both Traditional (if deductible) or Roth IRAs.
- Taxable: Use for additional savings or investments beyond tax-advantaged accounts.

Ultimately, the best distribution will depend on your unique financial situation, risk tolerance, and long-term goals. Consider consulting with a financial planner for personalized guidance.
