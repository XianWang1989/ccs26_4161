The best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts largely depends on your individual financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), try to contribute at least enough to take full advantage of that match. This is essentially "free money" for your retirement.

2. **401(k) Contributions**: Once you’ve secured your employer match, consider contributing up to the annual limit of your 401(k). This account offers tax-deferred growth, meaning you won’t pay taxes on your earnings until you withdraw funds in retirement.

3. **IRA Contributions**: Next, consider funding a traditional IRA or a Roth IRA, depending on your income level and tax strategy. Traditional IRAs offer tax-deferred growth similar to a 401(k), while Roth IRAs provide tax-free growth if certain conditions are met.

4. **Taxable Accounts**: After maximizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts, you can start investing in taxable accounts. This could include stocks, bonds, or mutual funds. Keep in mind that you’ll need to pay capital gains taxes on any profits when you sell investments in these accounts.

5. **Diversification and Asset Allocation**: Regardless of account type, ensure your investments are well-diversified and aligned with your risk tolerance and time horizon. You may have different asset allocations based on the purpose of each account (e.g., more aggressive in taxable accounts if you anticipate needing those funds later).

6. **Rebalance Periodically**: Regularly review your asset allocation and rebalance your accounts as needed. Changes in the market or your financial situation may require adjustments to keep your investments in line with your goals.

7. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: Consider seeking personalized advice from a financial advisor who can help you tailor a strategy based on your unique circumstances.

Ultimately, the best approach to distributing your money will depend on your individual financial situation and long-term financial goals.
