Distributing your money across a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts effectively depends on your financial goals, tax situation, risk tolerance, and time horizon. Here are some strategies to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**
   - **Contribution Strategy**: Contribute enough to get the full employer match in your 401(k). This is essentially "free money" and should be a priority.

### 2. **Utilize Tax-Advantaged Accounts**
   - **Maximize Contributions to IRAs**: If eligible, consider maxing out contributions to a traditional IRA or Roth IRA. Roth IRAs offer tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement, which can be highly beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.
   - **Consider Tax Bracket**: If you anticipate a lower tax bracket in retirement, a traditional IRA might be more advantageous, as contributions are tax-deductible.

### 3. **Diversify Tax Treatment**
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Use taxable accounts for investments that generate income, as qualified dividends and long-term capital gains are typically taxed at a lower rate. This can also provide flexibility for accessing funds without penalties.

### 4. **Asset Allocation Based on Risk Tolerance**
   - **Stocks vs. Bonds**: Generally, younger investors might lean heavily towards stocks in their retirement accounts for growth, while older investors might prefer bonds for stability.
   - **Rebalance Periodically**: Make sure your asset allocation aligns with your risk tolerance and financial goals, adjusting as necessary.

### 5. **Consider Your Time Horizon**
   - **Long-Term Investments**: Funds intended for retirement that are decades away can be more aggressively invested in equities. Conversely, savings for near-term goals should be more conservative.

### 6. **Withdrawals and Tax Planning**
   - **Plan Withdrawals Strategically**: When you retire, plan how you'll withdraw from each account type to minimize tax burdens. Be mindful of required minimum distributions (RMDs) from 401(k)s and traditional IRAs.

### Conclusion
A typical strategy could be: 
1. Contribute enough to your 401(k) to receive the full employer match.
2. Next, fund a Roth IRA (if eligible) or a traditional IRA.
3. Any additional savings can go into a taxable account or back into the 401(k) depending on your overall savings goals.

Consider working with a financial advisor for personalized advice based on your unique situation.
