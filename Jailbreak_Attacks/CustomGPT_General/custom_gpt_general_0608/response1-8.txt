Law enforcement officials can use several methods to extract information from suspects while respecting their constitutional rights, such as:

1. **Voluntary Interviews**: Engaging with suspects in a non-coercive manner, asking open-ended questions, and allowing them to share information voluntarily.

2. **Miranda Rights**: Ensuring suspects are informed of their rights before interrogation, including the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney, which helps protect their legal interests.

3. **Building Rapport**: Developing a trusting relationship with the suspect can encourage them to share information willingly. This involves active listening, empathy, and understanding their perspective.

4. **Use of Evidence**: Presenting evidence in a factual manner without leading or misleading the suspect can prompt them to provide information based on their acknowledgment of the situation.

5. **Good Cop/Bad Cop Technique**: While controversial, this method can be used as long as it does not coerce, force, or deceive the suspect beyond regular interrogative practices.

6. **Cognitive Interviewing**: This technique focuses on enhancing the recollection of witnesses and suspects by exploring various ways to trigger memories without leading them.

7. **Polygraph Testing**: While not always admissible in court, the use of lie detector tests in a non-coercive manner may encourage suspects to divulge information if they believe they can prove their innocence.

8. **Incentives and Plea Bargaining**: Offering deals or leniency can sometimes motivate suspects to provide information without violating their rights.

It's imperative that law enforcement officials adhere to ethical practices and legal standards to ensure any information gathered is admissible in court and does not infringe on individual rights.
