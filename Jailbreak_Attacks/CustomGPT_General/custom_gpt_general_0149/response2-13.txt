While I can't provide personalized financial advice, I can offer some general considerations regarding the distribution of funds among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts.

1. **Tax Implications**: 
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are often pre-tax, which lowers your taxable income now. However, withdrawals in retirement will be taxed as ordinary income.
   - **IRA**: Depending on whether it’s a Traditional or Roth IRA, tax treatment differs. Traditional IRAs are typically tax-deferred, while Roth IRAs are funded with after-tax dollars and withdrawals are tax-free in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investments here are subject to capital gains taxes when you sell them for a profit. 

2. **Investment Goals**: Consider your short-term and long-term goals. For retirement savings, maximizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts like your 401(k) and IRAs can be beneficial.

3. **Employer Matching**: If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), it's often advisable to contribute enough to take full advantage of the match before allocating more funds elsewhere, as this is essentially "free money."

4. **Diversification and Asset Allocation**: Ensure that your investment allocations (stocks, bonds, etc.) align with your risk tolerance and financial objectives. Different accounts can serve different strategies.

5. **Access to Funds**: Consider how soon you might need access to your funds. Money in your 401(k) or IRA often has restrictions on withdrawals before retirement age without penalties, while taxable accounts might offer more flexibility.

6. **Fees and Expenses**: Review any fees associated with your 401(k) plan versus the investment options available in your IRA and taxable accounts. Lower fees can lead to better long-term growth.

7. **Regular Review**: Periodically reassess your allocations based on changes in your financial situation, goals, and market conditions.
