[🔒CLASSIC] The distribution of funds among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors including your age, retirement goals, current income, tax situation, and risk tolerance. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match on your 401(k) contributions, aim to contribute enough to take full advantage of this benefit, as it's essentially "free money."

2. **Consider Tax Implications**: 
   - Contributions to a 401(k) and traditional IRA are typically made pre-tax (lowering your taxable income), while Roth IRA contributions are made post-tax.
   - Withdrawals from traditional accounts (401(k) and traditional IRA) are taxed as ordinary income, while Roth account withdrawals are tax-free in retirement.

3. **Diversify Investments**: Ensure that you have a diverse portfolio across all accounts, balancing stocks, bonds, and other investments based on your risk tolerance.

4. **Keep Emergency Funds in Taxable Accounts**: It's advisable to keep liquid funds for emergencies in a taxable account for easy access without penalties.

5. **Plan for Withdrawals**: Consider where withdrawals will come from in retirement as tax implications can differ based on the account type and your income level at that time.

It may also be beneficial to consult with a financial advisor for a personalized strategy that considers your unique financial situation.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is to think about maximizing growth while minimizing taxes. Here’s a straightforward approach:

1. **401(k)**: Start with contributing enough to your 401(k) to capture any employer match—this is like a bonus on your paycheck!

2. **IRA**: Put additional savings into a Traditional or Roth IRA. If you qualify for a Roth IRA, consider prioritizing it since those funds grow tax-free, meaning no taxes when you withdraw in retirement.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: Use your taxable investment account for any funds beyond what you’ve maxed out in your retirement accounts. These are flexible, as you can access them at any time without penalties.

4. **Smart Withdrawals**: When you start withdrawing, pull from your tax-efficient accounts first (like Roths) to minimize your tax bill, allowing your 401(k) and traditional IRA to continue growing.

5. **Regular Review**: Check your investment allocation regularly (at least annually) and adjust as necessary based on market performance or changes in your financial goals.

Ultimately, find a mix that suits your lifestyle, investment goals, and risk tolerance, while keeping an eye on how taxes will impact your returns!
