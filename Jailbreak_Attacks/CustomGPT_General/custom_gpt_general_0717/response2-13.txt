Distributing your money among a **401(k)**, **IRA**, and taxable accounts can greatly influence your investment growth and tax efficiency. Here’s a strategic approach to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts First**
   - **401(k):** If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to get that full match. This is essentially free money. Aim to contribute the maximum allowable amount ($22,500 for 2023, or $30,000 if you're over 50).
   - **IRA:** Once you’ve maxed out your 401(k) contributions (or at least to the match level), consider contributing to a **Traditional IRA** or a **Roth IRA**. The contribution limit for IRAs is $6,500 ($7,500 if you're over 50). A Roth IRA is preferable if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, as withdrawals are tax-free.

### 2. **Diversification in Account Types**
   - **401(k) and IRA:** These accounts typically hold tax-advantaged investments like stocks, bonds, and funds. Consider a balanced mix based on your risk tolerance and investment goals.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Place investments that may generate a lot of taxable income, such as bonds or actively managed mutual funds, in your tax-advantaged accounts to defer taxes.

### 3. **Consider Your Investment Strategy**
   - Keep a consistent investment strategy across all accounts, but be mindful of the overall allocation. Avoid over-allocating any single asset class that might lead to higher tax liabilities.
   - Use tax-loss harvesting strategies in taxable accounts, which can help offset capital gains taxes.

### 4. **Review and Adjust Annually**
   - Periodically review your allocations and performance. As you approach retirement, gradually shift to more conservative investments to preserve capital.

---

This balanced approach can enhance your growth potential while managing your tax liabilities effectively. Always consider consulting with a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your specific financial situation and goals.
