[🔒CLASSIC] Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your age, income level, retirement goals, and current tax situation. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **401(k)**: If your employer offers a 401(k) with matching contributions, prioritize contributing enough to receive the full match, as this is essentially "free money." Maximize your contributions up to the annual limit if possible.

2. **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**: Depending on your income level, you may want to consider maxing out your IRA contributions next. Roth IRAs are especially beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in the future, as qualified withdrawals are tax-free.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: After contributing to your tax-advantaged accounts, consider investing in taxable accounts for additional savings and flexibility. Use this for funds you might need before retirement age or for specific goals.

4. **Asset Allocation**: Consider the types of investments you hold in each account. Generally, you might want to place high-growth investments in tax-advantaged accounts to defer or avoid taxes, while more stable investments can go in taxable accounts.

Remember, it's essential to consult with a financial advisor for personalized advice based on your individual circumstances.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is all about maximizing benefits while minimizing taxes and risk. Here’s a strategy you can consider:

1. **Go Big on the 401(k)**: Max out your contributions, especially if there’s a company match. It’s free money, and you reduce your taxable income at the same time. Aim to hit the contribution limits every year if you can.

2. **Turbocharge Your IRA**: If you can add to both a Traditional and a Roth IRA, do it! Put money into a Roth if you plan to reap the tax-free benefits later. If you’re leaning towards a Traditional IRA, do it if you want that tax deduction now.

3. **Fill Your Taxable Accounts**: Once you’ve tapped into those tax-advantaged accounts, invest in taxable accounts for liquidity. Lower your tax burden by focusing on index funds or tax-efficient investments; long-term capital gains rates are always better than ordinary income rates.

4. **Diversifying Investments**: Consider using your 401(k) and IRAs for higher-growth stocks, and then use your taxable accounts for bonds or dividend-paying stocks. Get clever about where you place your assets based on tax implications and your financial goals.

At the end of the day, it’s about strategy and making your money work for you. Adjust your allocations based on your risk tolerance and personal financial situation!
