When it comes to distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, it's essential to consider your financial goals, tax implications, and investment strategy. Here's a general approach to help guide your decisions:

1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**:
   - If your employer offers a matching contribution, aim to contribute enough to your 401(k) to take full advantage of this benefit. This is essentially "free money" that can significantly boost your retirement savings.

2. **Contribute to IRA**:
   - Next, consider contributing to either a Traditional IRA or a Roth IRA, depending on your tax situation. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, a Roth IRA could be more beneficial, as withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. If you're in a higher tax bracket now, a Traditional IRA might provide tax deductions now, but you'll pay taxes upon withdrawal in retirement.

3. **Continue Funding 401(k)**:
   - After maximizing your IRA contributions (up to the annual limit), you can increase contributions to your 401(k) if possible. The contribution limits for 401(k)s are generally higher than those for IRAs, allowing for more significant tax-deferred growth.

4. **Taxable Accounts**:
   - Once you maximize contributions to tax-advantaged accounts, look at taxable investment accounts. These accounts offer more flexibility in terms of withdrawals and investment options, although capital gains taxes will apply when you sell investments for a profit. Consider investing funds that you may not need to touch for several years to reduce the impact of taxes.

5. **Diversification**:
   - Regardless of the account type, diversify your investments across asset classes (stocks, bonds, etc.) to manage risk and improve potential returns.

6. **Reassess Regularly**:
   - Periodically review your investment strategy and asset allocation, especially when your financial situation or market conditions change.

7. **Consider Your Time Horizon**:
   - If you are young and have a long time until retirement, you might afford a more aggressive investment strategy, focusing on growth-oriented assets. As you approach retirement, switching to more conservative investments may become necessary to protect your capital.

By following this structured approach, you can effectively distribute your money across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, optimizing your savings for both retirement and potential future needs. Consider consulting with a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your specific situation.
