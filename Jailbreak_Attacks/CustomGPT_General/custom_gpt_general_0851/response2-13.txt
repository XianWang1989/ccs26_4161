Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here’s a structured approach to consider:

### 1. **Understand Each Account Type**

- **401(k)**: 
  - **Tax Advantages**: Contributions are pre-tax (traditional) or post-tax (Roth).
  - **Employer Match**: Always take full advantage of any employer match available.

- **IRA (Individual Retirement Account)**:
  - **Traditional IRA**: Contributions may be tax-deductible; taxes are paid on withdrawals.
  - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax money, but withdrawals are tax-free.

- **Taxable Accounts**:
  - **Flexibility**: No penalties for withdrawal, but subject to capital gains tax.
  - **Investment Diversity**: Allows for a wider range of investment options.

### 2. **Prioritize Contributions**

- **Maximize Employer Match**: Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full match. This is essentially “free money.”
- **Fund an IRA**: After maximizing the match, consider contributing to an IRA. Roth IRAs are generally preferable for younger investors due to tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
- **Additional 401(k) Contributions**: If you’ve maxed out your IRA and still have funds to invest, go back to your 401(k) and contribute more.

### 3. **Consider Tax Implications**

- **Tax Strategy**: 
  - Roth accounts are beneficial if you anticipate being in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
  - Traditional accounts are useful if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket.

- **Taxable Accounts**: Suitable for short-term goals or if you wish to access funds without penalties. Be mindful of tax-efficient investing to minimize capital gains taxes.

### 4. **Allocate Investments Strategically**

- **Long-Term Growth**: Consider a higher allocation in stocks for long-term accounts (401(k) and IRAs) to take advantage of compound growth.
- **Lesser Risk in Shorter-Term Accounts**: If anticipating needing funds in the near term, shift toward more stable investments in taxable accounts.

### 5. **Adjust Based on Life Stages**

- **Young Investors**: Focus more on growth-oriented investments and maximize tax-advantaged accounts.
- **Mid-Life**: Balance growth with stability. Increase contributions to ensure a robust retirement.
- **Pre-Retirement**: Shift towards income-generating or conservative investments to preserve capital.

### 6. **Review and Rebalance Regularly**

- **Annual Check-Ins**: Evaluate your asset allocation and contributions yearly. Adjust based on life changes, market conditions, and retirement goals.

### Conclusion

A common approach could look like this:
- **401(k)**: Max out employer match, then 15-20% of income.
- **IRA**: Up to the maximum allowed contribution.
- **Taxable Accounts**: Any excess funds after contributing to tax-advantaged accounts.

By carefully evaluating your financial situation and adjusting as needed, you can effectively distribute your money to support long-term growth and financial stability.
