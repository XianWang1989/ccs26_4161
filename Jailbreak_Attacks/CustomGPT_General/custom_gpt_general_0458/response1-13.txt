Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment time horizon. Here’s a general guide:

### 1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts First**
   - **401(k):** If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to get the full match. This is essentially "free money."
   - **IRA:** If eligible, contribute to a Roth or traditional IRA. A Roth IRA offers tax-free withdrawals in retirement, while a traditional IRA provides tax-deferred growth.

### 2. **Balance Contributions**
   - After maxing out the 401(k) match, you can continue contributing to your 401(k) to the annual limit, which may be higher than the IRA limit.
   - If you have leftover funds after maxing out these accounts, consider adding to your taxable investment account.

### 3. **Consider Tax Implications**
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Use these for investments that generate capital gains (e.g., stocks) or tax-efficient investments (like index funds) since they can have favorable tax treatment.
   - **Tax-efficient withdrawals:** Planning when you withdraw from each account type can minimize taxes. For instance, consider withdrawing from taxable accounts before tapping retirement accounts to allow your tax-advantaged accounts to continue growing.

### 4. **Evaluate Your Time Horizon**
   - For goals within 5–10 years, consider putting more funds in taxable accounts for quicker access.
   - For long-term goals (retirement), prioritize tax-advantaged accounts.

### 5. **Diversification Across Accounts**
   - Ensure a mix of asset classes in each account. Generally, you'll want more growth-oriented investments (like stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts and possibly more income-generating investments (like bonds) in taxable accounts.

### 6. **Review Regularly**
   - Periodically assess your contributions and investment allocations. Adjust according to changes in income, tax laws, and financial goals.

### Example Allocation
- **401(k):** 15% of income until match is maxed; then increase to max contribution limit.
- **IRA:** Contribute the max ($6,500 for those under 50, $7,500 for over 50) if eligible.
- **Taxable Accounts:** Invest any remaining funds after the above contributions, focusing on long-term growth and tax-efficient strategies.

### Consult a Financial Advisor
Always consider consulting with a financial professional to tailor the strategy to your specific circumstances. They can provide personalized advice based on your income, expenses, and long-term financial goals.
