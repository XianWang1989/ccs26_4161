Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can depend on your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here’s a general approach to consider:

### 1. **401(k) Contribution:**
   - **Max Out Employer Match:** Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match if available. This is effectively "free money."
   - **Consider Contribution Limits:** For 2023, you can contribute up to $22,500 (or $30,000 if age 50+). Prioritize reaching these limits if your employer offers a match.

### 2. **IRA Contributions:**
   - **Traditional vs. Roth IRA:** Decide whether a Traditional IRA (tax-deductible contributions, taxes on withdrawals) or a Roth IRA (after-tax contributions, tax-free withdrawals) is best for you based on your current and expected future tax bracket.
   - **Annual Limits:** For 2023, you can contribute up to $6,500 (or $7,500 if age 50+). Maximize contributions if your situation allows.

### 3. **Taxable Accounts:**
   - **Invest After Tax-Advantaged Accounts:** After maximizing your 401(k) and IRA contributions, consider using taxable accounts for additional investments.
   - **Tax Efficiency:** Focus on tax-efficient investments, such as index funds or ETFs, which generate fewer taxable events.

### 4. **Diversification and Risk Tolerance:**
   - **Asset Allocation:** Ensure your investments are diversified based on your risk tolerance and time horizon. For long-term goals, you might lean towards growth-focused investments.
   - **Rebalance Periodically:** Regularly review and adjust your portfolio to maintain your desired asset allocation.

### 5. **Emergency Fund:**
   - Ensure you have an emergency fund equal to 3-6 months' worth of expenses. This should be kept in a high-yield savings account, separate from your investment accounts.

### Final Thoughts:
Consulting with a financial advisor can provide tailored strategies based on your situation, especially considering factors like tax implications and retirement goals.
