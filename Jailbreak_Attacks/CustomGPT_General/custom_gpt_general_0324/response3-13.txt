Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts effectively depends on your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some considerations:

### 1. **401(k)**
   - **Advantages**: Often offers employer matching contributions; tax-deferred growth until withdrawal.
   - **Strategy**: Maximize contributions to take advantage of any employer match first. 

### 2. **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**
   - **Traditional IRA**: Contributions may be tax-deductible, tax-deferred growth, taxes due on withdrawal.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are post-tax; withdrawals are tax-free in retirement.
   - **Strategy**: If eligible, consider contributing to a Roth IRA for tax-free growth. Balance contributions based on your tax bracket now versus expected retirement taxes.

### 3. **Taxable Accounts**
   - **Advantages**: Flexibility with withdrawals; potential for long-term capital gains tax rates (lower than ordinary income tax rates).
   - **Strategy**: Use these for investments that generate capital gains or dividends, which may be taxed at favorable rates compared to ordinary income.

### General Guidelines
- **Emergency Fund**: Maintain an emergency fund in a savings account before investing.
- **Risk Tolerance**: Adjust allocation based on your risk tolerance and time horizon.
- **Diversification**: Spread investments across different asset classes (stocks, bonds) for better risk management.

### Sample Allocation
- **401(k)**: 50% (due to employer match and tax deferral)
- **IRA**: 30% (split between Traditional and Roth based on tax strategy)
- **Taxable Accounts**: 20% (for liquidity and flexibility)

### Regular Review
Revisit your allocation annually or when your financial situation changes to ensure it aligns with your goals.

#### Final Thoughts
Consult with a financial advisor to tailor a strategy based on your specific circumstances.
