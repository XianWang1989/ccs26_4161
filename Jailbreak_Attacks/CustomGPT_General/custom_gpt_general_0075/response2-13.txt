Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon. Here are some strategies to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match in your 401(k), contribute enough to take full advantage of it. This is essentially free money.

2. **Tax Efficiency**:
   - **401(k) and IRA**: These are tax-advantaged accounts. Contributions may be tax-deductible (traditional accounts), and the investments grow tax-deferred (you pay taxes on withdrawals).
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Use these for investments you might need to access before retirement age, as they don’t have the same tax penalties. They are also beneficial for investments that generate qualified dividends or long-term capital gains, which are taxed at lower rates.

3. **Asset Allocation**:
   - **Higher-risk investments**: Consider placing stocks in your taxable account, where long-term capital gains taxes can apply.
   - **Fixed-income investments**: Bonds might be better in tax-advantaged accounts since they generate interest taxed as ordinary income.

4. **Contribution Limits**: Be mindful of the annual contribution limits for 401(k) and IRA accounts. For 2023, the limit for 401(k) contributions is $22,500 (plus a $7,500 catch-up for those over 50), and for IRAs, it’s $6,500 (plus a $1,000 catch-up).

5. **Withdrawal Strategy**: Plan your withdrawals strategically in retirement to minimize taxes. Withdraw from taxable accounts first, followed by tax-deferred accounts, using tax-free accounts (like Roth IRAs) last.

6. **Diversification**: Ensure your investments are diversified across asset classes to minimize risk.

7. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: Given the complexity of tax rules and personal circumstances, a financial advisor can tailor a strategy specific to your needs.

Consider your unique situation and goals, and adjust your contributions accordingly.
