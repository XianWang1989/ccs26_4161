Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment horizon. Here’s a breakdown of how to approach this:

### 1. **Understand Account Types**

- **401(k):** 
  - **Pros:** Employer match, tax-deferred growth, higher contribution limits.
  - **Cons:** Limited investment options, early withdrawal penalties.

- **IRA (Traditional or Roth):**
  - **Traditional IRA:**
    - **Pros:** Tax-deferred growth, potential for tax-deductible contributions.
    - **Cons:** Taxes on withdrawals in retirement.
  - **Roth IRA:**
    - **Pros:** Tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement.
    - **Cons:** Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, lower contribution limits.

- **Taxable Accounts:**
  - **Pros:** Flexibility in withdrawals, no contribution limits, variety of investment options.
  - **Cons:** Capital gains taxes on profits.

### 2. **Prioritize Contributions**

1. **Maximize Employer Match:**
   - Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match, as this is essentially free money.

2. **Contribute to an IRA:**
   - Consider contributing to an IRA after maximizing your 401(k) match. If you’re eligible, a Roth IRA can provide significant tax advantages.

3. **Maximize 401(k) Contributions:**
   - After funding your IRA, continue to max out your 401(k) contributions up to the annual limit.

4. **Invest in Taxable Accounts:**
   - Use taxable accounts for any additional savings and investments after hitting your retirement account limits, especially if you might need access to funds before retirement.

### 3. **Consider Tax Implications**

- **Tax Rate Strategy:** If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, a Roth IRA can be beneficial. If you anticipate lower taxes, a traditional IRA may be better.

- **Capital Gains Management:** In taxable accounts, be mindful of capital gains taxes. Holding investments for over a year typically qualifies for lower long-term capital gains rates.

### 4. **Evaluate Your Risk Tolerance and Time Horizon**

- **Long-term Goals:** Focus on growth-oriented investments in retirement accounts.
- **Short-term Needs:** Consider more liquid and stable investments in taxable accounts.

### 5. **Regularly Review and Adjust**

- Rebalance your portfolio as needed to maintain your desired asset allocation and account performance.

### Example Distribution Strategy

- **401(k):** 15% of income (enough to get the employer match)
- **IRA (Roth or Traditional):** $6,000/year (or max if eligible)
- **Taxable Account:** Any excess funds after maxing the above.

### Final Thoughts

Tailor your approach based on your specific circumstances and consult a financial advisor for personalized strategies. This diversification can help you optimize growth while managing taxes effectively.
