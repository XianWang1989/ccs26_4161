Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors such as your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon. Here’s a general approach to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Contributions**: If your employer offers a 401(k) match, contribute at least enough to receive the full match. This is essentially free money and a great starting point.

2. **Consider Tax Advantages**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are typically made pre-tax, which can lower your taxable income. Additionally, many 401(k) plans offer a Roth option for after-tax contributions, providing tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
   - **IRA**: Traditional IRAs may provide tax-deductible contributions, while Roth IRAs offer tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement, assuming certain conditions are met.

3. **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have sufficient liquidity for emergencies before heavily investing. Typically, this means 3 to 6 months' worth of living expenses in a liquid savings account.

4. **Taxable Accounts**: After maximizing tax-advantaged accounts, consider investing in taxable accounts. This can give you flexibility in terms of access to funds and can be a good place for investments that might generate capital gains or dividends taxed at lower rates than ordinary income.

5. **Investment Strategy**: Diversify your investments based on your risk tolerance and time horizon. Consider factors like age, income, and retirement goals. Generally:
   - Younger investors might favor aggressive growth-oriented investments (more stocks).
   - As retirement approaches, shift to more conservative investments (bonds, cash).

6. **Tax Implications**: Understand the tax consequences of each account type. For instance, investments that produce capital gains can be held in taxable accounts, whereas interest-generating investments (which are taxed as ordinary income) are better suited for tax-advantaged accounts.

7. **Seek Advice**: Consider consulting a financial advisor to tailor your asset allocation based on your individual circumstances and financial goals.

In summary, start with maximizing employer benefits, then prioritize tax-advantaged accounts based on your tax situation, and finally invest in taxable accounts for flexibility. Adjust your portfolio based on your risk tolerance and investment timeframe.
