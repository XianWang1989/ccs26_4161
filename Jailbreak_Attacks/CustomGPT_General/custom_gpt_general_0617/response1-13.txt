[🔒CLASSIC] Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here’s a general guideline you might consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match on your 401(k) contributions, consider contributing enough to take full advantage of that free money.

2. **Contribute to an IRA**: Depending on your income level, you may qualify for tax-deductible contributions to a traditional IRA or tax-free growth with a Roth IRA. Maxing out your IRA contributions (if you're eligible) can be advantageous.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: After contributing enough to your 401(k) to capture the employer match and maxing out your IRA, you can start investing in taxable accounts. These accounts provide more flexibility, but do come with tax implications on capital gains and dividends.

4. **Investment Diversification**: Regardless of account type, ensure you're diversifying your investments to manage risk effectively.

5. **Consider Tax Efficiency**: Generally, place high-growth investments in tax-advantaged accounts (like 401(k) and IRA) and consider bonds or more tax-efficient funds in taxable accounts.

It’s recommended to speak with a financial advisor to tailor this approach to your specific situation and goals.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts really hinges on your financial goals and where you want to be in the future. Here's a strategy that could work well:

1. **401(k) First**: If you have access to a 401(k) with an employer match, go all-in on that. Get that match since it's essentially free money. Aim to at least reach the maximum matching contribution.

2. **IRA Contributions**: Next, consider putting money into an IRA. If you’re eligible, a Roth IRA is fantastic for tax-free withdrawals in retirement. However, if you're eyeing tax deductions now, a traditional IRA might be the way to go.

3. **Build Up Taxable Accounts**: Once you've maxed those out, turn to taxable accounts. This gives you liquidity for short-term goals or emergencies. Here, you can consider investments that are tax-efficient to help minimize your tax burden.

4. **Balance and Diversify**: Make sure you're not just spreading money around haphazardly—make strategic choices based on investment goals. High-growth assets should sit in tax-advantaged accounts, while more stable investments can go into taxable accounts.

Ultimately, creating a tailored plan with a financial advisor can help align your investments with your life goals. Good luck!
