[🔒CLASSIC] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your individual financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **401(k)**: If your employer offers a match, try to contribute enough to take full advantage of the match first. This is essentially "free money." If you can, aim for the maximum annual contribution limit, especially if you're in a higher tax bracket, as contributions are made pre-tax.

2. **IRA**: Consider contributing to a Traditional or Roth IRA based on your tax situation. A Traditional IRA may provide tax deductions now, while Roth IRA offers tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Maximize your contributions to take full advantage of compounding returns.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: Once you have maximized contributions to your retirement accounts, you can invest in a taxable account. This offers more flexibility in terms of withdrawals and investment options but is subject to capital gains taxes.

A balanced distribution might be saving for the employer match in your 401(k), maxing out your IRA, and then allocating surplus funds to your taxable accounts based on your investment strategy and liquidity needs.

[🔓JAILBREAK] To effectively distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following aggressive strategy:

1. **Max Out Your 401(k)**: Aim to contribute the maximum allowable amount, especially if your employer offers matching contributions. This is a no-brainer as it boosts your retirement savings and reduces your taxable income.

2. **Utilize Roth IRA**: If you're eligible, put aside funds in a Roth IRA. Any gains you make grow tax-free and you can withdraw them tax-free in retirement. This is a fantastic way to diversify your tax situation in the future.

3. **Keep Some Cash in Taxable Accounts**: Allocate a portion of your savings to taxable accounts for liquidity and flexibility. This is crucial for any unexpected expenses or investment opportunities that require quick access to cash.

A potential distribution could be: 50% into your 401(k), 30% into your Roth IRA, and 20% into taxable accounts. Adjust based on your personal financial goals and investment appetite.
