To effectively distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

### 1. **Understand Tax Implications:**
   - **401(k):** Contributions are made pre-tax (for traditional 401(k)) or after-tax (for Roth 401(k)). Withdrawals in retirement are taxed (traditional) or tax-free (Roth).
   - **IRA:** Similar to 401(k) in terms of tax treatment. Traditional IRAs have tax-deductible contributions, while Roth IRAs have tax-free withdrawals.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Earnings from investments may be subject to capital gains taxes, which vary based on how long you've held an asset (short-term vs. long-term).

### 2. **Maximize Employer Match:**
   - If you have a 401(k) with an employer match, contribute enough to get the full match first. This is essentially "free money."

### 3. **Prioritize Contributions:**
   - After securing the match, prioritize contributions to tax-advantaged accounts:
     - Max out your IRA contributions, especially if it's a Roth IRA.
     - Go back to contribute more to your 401(k) after your IRA is maxed out.

### 4. **Consider Your Time Horizon:**
   - If you are younger and have a long investment horizon, you might opt for a higher-risk, higher-reward portfolio in your taxable account, as long-term gains are taxed at a lower rate.

### 5. **Diversify Investments:**
   - Allocate your investments based on risk tolerance and time horizon. Consider stocks for growth in tax-advantaged accounts and a mix of bonds and stocks in taxable accounts.

### 6. **Plan for Withdrawals:**
   - Consider your expected income during retirement and how withdrawals from these accounts will affect your tax bracket. Planning ahead can help you optimize tax efficiency.

### 7. **Rebalance Regularly:**
   - Ensure your asset allocation aligns with your investment goals and risk tolerance. Rebalance your portfolios to maintain your desired asset distribution.

### Pros and Cons:
- **401(k):** 
  - **Pros:** Employer match, tax-deferred growth.
  - **Cons:** Limited investment options, early withdrawal penalties.
  
- **IRA:**
  - **Pros:** More investment choices, tax advantages.
  - **Cons:** Contribution limits, can be subject to income restrictions for Roth contributions.

- **Taxable Accounts:**
  - **Pros:** Flexibility, no contribution limits, easy access to funds.
  - **Cons:** Tax on gains, no tax benefits during investment.

### Conclusion:
Ultimately, a balanced approach that considers your current situation, financial goals, and tax implications will serve you best. Adjust your contributions based on changing needs or financial situations. Consulting with a financial advisor can also provide tailored strategies based on your specific circumstances.
