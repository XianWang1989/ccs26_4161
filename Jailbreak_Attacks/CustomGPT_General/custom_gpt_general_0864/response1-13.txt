The best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts often depends on your individual financial goals, risk tolerance, time horizon, and tax situation. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match on your 401(k) contributions, prioritize contributing enough to capture that full match, as it's essentially free money.

2. **Tax-Advantaged Accounts First**: Consider maximizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts like a 401(k) and IRA. For 2023, the contribution limits are $22,500 for a 401(k) (or $30,000 if you're over 50) and $6,500 for an IRA (or $7,500 if you're over 50). These accounts grow tax-deferred, which can significantly increase your long-term returns.

3. **Flexible Contributions**: After maximizing tax-advantaged contributions, consider investing in taxable accounts. These accounts offer flexibility since you can withdraw funds without penalty (though you will owe taxes on any capital gains).

4. **Asset Allocation**: Diversify your investments based on your risk tolerance and investment goals. Generally, you might want to hold more aggressive growth investments (like stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts and more stable income-generating investments (like bonds) in taxable accounts due to the different tax implications.

5. **Long-Term Strategy**: Regularly review and adjust your allocations based on market performance and your changing financial situation. This ensures you remain aligned with your long-term financial goals.

**Specific Considerations:**
- **Tax Bracket**: Your current and expected future tax bracket can influence how much you should put into taxable vs. tax-deferred accounts.
- **Investment Options**: Sometimes the investment choices in a 401(k) might be limited or have high fees; consider this when determining how much to allocate.
- **Withdrawal Needs**: Consider how soon you may need access to your funds. Money in a 401(k) or IRA generally has penalties for early withdrawal, while taxable accounts do not.
