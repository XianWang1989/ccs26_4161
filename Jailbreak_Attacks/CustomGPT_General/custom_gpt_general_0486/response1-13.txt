The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, risk tolerance, tax situation, and the options available in each account. Here's a general approach:

1. **401(k)**: If your employer offers a 401(k) match, prioritize contributing enough to take full advantage of that match, as it's essentially free money. Beyond that, consider contributing to the 401(k) up to the maximum limit, especially if you are in a higher tax bracket since contributions are made pre-tax.

2. **IRA**: If you're eligible, consider contributing to a traditional IRA or a Roth IRA, depending on your tax situation and whether you want to pay taxes now (Roth) or later (traditional). Roth IRAs offer tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement, which can be beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: Once you've maximized contributions to your 401(k) and IRA, you can invest in taxable accounts. These accounts offer flexibility and liquidity, but you will need to pay taxes on any dividends, interest, and capital gains. Consider holding tax-efficient investments like index funds or ETFs here, as they tend to distribute fewer taxable events.

4. **Asset Allocation**: Regardless of the account type, your allocation between stocks, bonds, and cash should align with your retirement timeline and risk tolerance. Typically, younger investors may lean more towards stocks for growth, while those closer to retirement may prioritize stability and income.

5. **Rebalancing and Tax Considerations**: Regularly review and rebalance your portfolio to maintain your desired asset allocation. Be mindful of tax implications when selling investments in taxable accounts, and consider tax-loss harvesting when applicable.

It’s advisable to consult with a financial advisor to create a personalized strategy that takes into account your unique situation.
