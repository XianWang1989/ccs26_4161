Distributing your money effectively among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is key to optimizing your investment strategy and maximizing tax efficiency. Here’s a general guideline to consider:

1. **Prioritize Contributions**:
   - **401(k)**: If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to maximize that match first. This is essentially "free money" and offers immediate returns.
   - **IRA**: After maximizing your 401(k) match, consider contributing to a Roth or traditional IRA. Roth IRAs are beneficial for tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement, but traditional IRAs can allow for tax-deductible contributions if you qualify.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These come into play after you've maximized contributions to your tax-advantaged accounts. They're useful for financial goals that may arise before retirement or for additional, flexible investing.

2. **Asset Allocation**:
   - Within your tax-advantaged accounts (like the 401(k) and IRA), you can take a more aggressive approach to investing since they are designed for long-term growth. Consider higher-risk investments like stocks.
   - In your taxable account, it's wise to include investments that generate less taxable income (like tax-efficient index funds) to minimize your tax burden.

3. **Consider Tax Implications**:
   - Distributions from traditional 401(k) and IRA accounts are taxed as ordinary income, while qualified withdrawals from a Roth IRA are tax-free. In taxable accounts, you'll need to manage capital gains taxes on any assets sold.
   - Optimize your tax strategy by considering the tax impact of where your investments are held.

4. **Review and Rebalance**:
   - Regularly review your investment performance and goals. Adjust your allocations across your accounts at least annually or when your investment goals change.

5. **Consult a Financial Advisor**:
   - Every financial situation is unique, and the best strategies may vary based on your age, income, financial goals, and risk tolerance. A professional can provide personalized guidance.

By following these guidelines, you can create a balanced and tax-efficient investment strategy across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts.
