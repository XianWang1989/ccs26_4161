Distributing your money amongst your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts requires careful consideration of your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines to help you decide on the best approach:

### 1. **Understand Account Types**:
   - **401(k)**: Offers pre-tax contributions (traditional) or after-tax (Roth), often with employer matching, which is essentially free money.
   - **IRA**: Can be traditional (tax-deferred) or Roth (after-tax). Contributions to traditional IRAs may provide tax deductions, while Roth IRAs grow tax-free.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Subject to capital gains tax on profits and income tax on dividends, but offer more flexibility in terms of withdrawals.

### 2. **Prioritize Contributions**:
   - **Employer Match**: Maximize your 401(k) contributions to at least the point where you get the full employer match; it's part of your compensation.
   - **IRA Contributions**: After maximally funding your 401(k) with any employer match, consider contributing to an IRA. If you qualify for Roth IRA contributions, they can provide substantial tax benefits in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Use these for any additional savings or investment after maximizing tax-advantaged accounts, especially for goals with a shorter time horizon or more liquidity needs.

### 3. **Consider Tax Implications**:
   - **Tax Brackets**: If you're in a high tax bracket now but expect to be in a lower bracket during retirement, prioritize traditional accounts. Conversely, if you believe you'll be in a higher bracket later, consider contributing to Roth accounts.
   - **Withdrawal Timing**: Understand the implications of withdrawing from each account type, especially regarding required minimum distributions and penalties.

### 4. **Asset Allocation**:
   - **Investment Strategy**: Use different accounts for different investment strategies. For example, consider holding more aggressive investments in your tax-deferred accounts where gains are taxed at withdrawal, and more stable investments in your taxable accounts where dividends might have preferential tax treatment.

### 5. **Rebalance Regularly**:
   - Ensure your asset allocation aligns with your risk tolerance and investment goals. Regularly rebalance across accounts to maintain your desired mix of stocks and bonds, adjusting for performance and contributions.

### Pros and Cons:
- **401(k) Pros**: Tax advantages and often employer matching; Cons: Limited investment options.
- **IRA Pros**: Greater flexibility in investment choices; Cons: Contribution limits may restrict maximum investment.
- **Taxable Account Pros**: Greater access and flexibility with funds; Cons: Tax liabilities on gains can reduce overall returns.

### Conclusion:
The best distribution approach may differ based on personal circumstances, goals, and market conditions. Consult with a financial advisor to devise a tailored strategy that aligns with your specific needs and maximizes your investment potential.

Happy investing!
