Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your tax situation, retirement goals, and investment horizon. Here are some guidelines:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**:
   - Contribute enough to get the full employer match, as this is essentially free money.

### 2. **Consider Tax Advantages**:
   - **401(k)**: Pre-tax contributions lower your taxable income. Good for higher earners expecting to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Traditional IRA**: Similar tax benefits to a 401(k), but contribution limits are lower. Contributions may be tax-deductible based on income.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. This can be beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: No tax advantages, but they provide flexibility with withdrawals and investment choices.

### 3. **Prioritize Accounts**:
   - After maxing out employer matching in your 401(k), consider funding a Roth IRA if eligible, then any additional contributions to your 401(k) or IRA.
   - Use taxable accounts for short-term goals or if you've maxed out tax-advantaged accounts.

### 4. **Asset Allocation**:
   - Align your investments with your risk tolerance and time horizon across all accounts. Consider placing higher-growth assets in tax-advantaged accounts to minimize capital gains taxes.

### 5. **Review Regularly**:
   - Periodically assess your financial goals and adjust your contributions as needed.

### Conclusion:
A common strategy is to get the 401(k) match first, then contribute to a Roth IRA, followed by additional contributions to the 401(k) or traditional IRA, with any remaining funds going into taxable accounts. Always consult with a financial advisor for personalized advice based on your specific situation.
