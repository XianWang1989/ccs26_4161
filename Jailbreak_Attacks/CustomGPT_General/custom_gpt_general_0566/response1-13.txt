To effectively distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

1. **Understand Your Goals**:
   - Identify your short-term, medium-term, and long-term financial goals. This will help you determine how much to allocate to each account type.

2. **Maximize Tax Advantage**:
   - **401(k)**: Contribute enough to get any employer match, as it's essentially free money. If you can, aim to maximize contributions (the limit for 2023 is $22,500, or $30,000 if you’re over 50).
   - **IRA**: Contribute to a Traditional or Roth IRA based on your income and tax situation. The limit for IRAs in 2023 is $6,500 (or $7,500 if over 50). A Roth IRA offers tax-free withdrawals in retirement, while a Traditional IRA may offer immediate tax benefits.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Use these for investments that may generate income or gains, considering the taxable implications.

3. **Consider Investment Horizon**:
   - Longer-term investments should be tilted toward retirement accounts which may offer tax benefits.
   - For shorter-term needs, consider keeping investments in taxable accounts for more liquidity.

4. **Diversification and Risk Tolerance**:
   - Diversify investments across asset classes (stocks, bonds, ETFs) based on your risk tolerance. Higher-risk investments may be suitable for tax-advantaged accounts, while stable income-producing assets may fit better in taxable accounts.

5. **Rebalance Regularly**:
   - Periodically review and adjust your portfolio to maintain your desired asset allocation.

6. **Tax Efficiency**:
   - Hold tax-inefficient investments (like bonds or REITs) in tax-advantaged accounts. Keep tax-efficient investments (like index funds) in taxable accounts to minimize tax liabilities.

7. **Consult a Financial Advisor**:
   - A professional can help tailor a strategy to your specific financial situation and goals.

By following these strategies, you can create a balanced investment approach that leverages the strengths of each account type while minimizing your tax burden.
