Distributing your money effectively among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts involves a strategic approach that considers your financial goals, risk tolerance, and tax situation. Here’s a structured guide to help you with the allocation:

### 1. Understand Each Account Type

#### 401(k)
- **Tax Advantage:** Contributions are made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income.
- **Employer Match:** Take advantage of any employer matching contributions as it’s essentially “free money.”

#### IRA (Traditional/Roth)
- **Traditional IRA:** Contributions may be tax-deductible. Taxes are paid upon withdrawal.
- **Roth IRA:** Contributions are made with after-tax dollars. Withdrawals during retirement are tax-free.

#### Taxable Accounts
- **Flexibility:** No restrictions on withdrawals or contributions.
- **Tax Implications:** Capital gains taxes apply when selling investments for a profit.

### 2. Prioritize Contributions
- **Max Out Employer Matched 401(k):** Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match first.
- **Contribute to an IRA:** Depending on your income level, consider contributing to a Roth or Traditional IRA.
- **Max Out 401(k):** After maxing out the IRA, consider contributing more to your 401(k) if possible.
- **Fund Taxable Accounts Last:** Use taxable accounts for additional savings or investments after maxing tax-advantaged accounts.

### 3. Consider Investment Choices
- **401(k):** Generally offers a limited selection of funds, usually mutual funds. Choose a mix of stocks and bonds based on your risk tolerance.
- **IRA:** You have a broader choice of investments (stocks, bonds, ETFs, etc.). Select based on your investment strategy.
- **Taxable Accounts:** Choose tax-efficient investments (like index funds or tax-managed funds) to minimize capital gains taxes.

### 4. Evaluate Tax Implications
- **Short-Term vs. Long-Term:** Aim for long-term investments in your taxable accounts to minimize tax impact. Long-term capital gains are taxed at a lower rate than short-term gains.
- **Withdrawal Strategy:** Plan when and how to withdraw funds during retirement to mitigate tax burdens effectively.

### 5. Regular Review and Adjust
- **Reassess Allocation:** Periodically review your investments and overall allocation according to changes in income, expenses, and market conditions.
- **Adapt to Life Changes:** Major life events (marriage, children, retirement) may necessitate changes to your investment strategy.

### Example Allocation:
- **Age 30-40:** More aggressive (70% stocks, 30% bonds across accounts)
- **Age 50-60:** More balanced (60% stocks, 40% bonds)
- **Age 65+:** Generally more conservative (50% stocks, 50% bonds)

### Conclusion
While specific allocations can vary based on individual circumstances, following this structured guide can help you optimize your investment strategy across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts. Always consider consulting with a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your situation.
