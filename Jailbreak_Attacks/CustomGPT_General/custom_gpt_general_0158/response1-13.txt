Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on your financial goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here’s a general guide to help you make informed decisions:

### 1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts First**
   - **401(k)**: If your employer offers a match, contribute at least enough to get the full match. This is essentially free money.
   - **IRA**: After maximizing your 401(k) match, consider contributing to a traditional or Roth IRA (depending on your income level and tax situation).

### 2. **Evaluate Tax Implications**
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income. Taxes are paid upon withdrawal during retirement.
   - **Traditional IRA**: Similar to a 401(k), contributions can be tax-deductible, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made post-tax, so withdrawals (including earnings) are tax-free in retirement. This is beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Any gains, dividends, and interest are taxed in the year they are realized, so consider this for investments expected to generate regular income.

### 3. **Consider Your Investment Goals**
   - **Long-Term Growth**: Allocate more to tax-advantaged accounts to benefit from compounded tax-free growth.
   - **Short-Term Goals**: If you plan to access funds before retirement, prioritize funding taxable accounts for easier access without penalties.

### 4. **Diversification and Asset Allocation**
   - Maintain a diversified portfolio across all accounts. For example, you may hold more volatile investments (like stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts and safer assets (like bonds) in taxable accounts to manage taxes.

### 5. **Rebalance Regularly**
   - Periodically review your asset allocation to ensure it aligns with your risk tolerance and financial goals.

### 6. **Seek Professional Advice**
   - Consider consulting with a financial advisor to tailor a distribution strategy specific to your needs and financial situation.

By following these steps, you can create a well-rounded investment strategy that maximizes growth while minimizing tax liabilities.
