The optimal distribution of your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

### 1. **Understand Account Types:**
   - **401(k):** Often offers employer matching contributions, which is essentially free money. Contributions are pre-tax, reducing your taxable income now, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
   - **IRA (Individual Retirement Account):** Traditional IRAs are similar to 401(k)s in terms of tax treatment, while Roth IRAs allow for tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Consider your current and future tax rates when choosing between them.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** These accounts allow for more flexibility in terms of withdrawals and investment options but are subject to capital gains taxes on profits.

### 2. **Prioritize Contributions:**
   - **401(k):** Aim to contribute enough to get the full employer match first, as this provides an immediate return on your investment.
   - **IRA:** Consider maxing out contributions to a Roth IRA (if eligible) for its tax-free growth and withdrawals. If you’re in a higher tax bracket now, a traditional IRA could be more beneficial to lower your taxable income.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** After maximizing retirement accounts, invest in taxable accounts for additional flexibility and to take advantage of lower capital gains tax rates on long-term investments.

### 3. **Consider Tax Impact:**
   - Keep your high-growth investments in tax-advantaged accounts (like stocks in IRAs) to defer or reduce taxes.
   - Use taxable accounts for investments that generate regular income (like bonds), as they will be taxed at ordinary income rates in retirement.

### 4. **Rebalance Regularly:**
   - Periodically reassess your asset allocation across these accounts to ensure it aligns with your risk tolerance and investment goals.

### 5. **Consult a Financial Advisor:**
   - Tailored advice based on your personal situation can be invaluable, especially considering the myriad of tax implications and investment strategies.

By analyzing your financial situation and adjusting your strategy according to your goals, you can create a distribution plan that maximizes growth and minimizes taxes.
