Finding the best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here are some general guidelines:

### 1. **401(k) Contributions:**
   - **Employer Match:** Max out contributions to your 401(k) at least to the level of any employer match, as it’s essentially free money.
   - **Contribution Limits:** Be aware of annual contribution limits ($19,500 for 2021, $20,500 for 2022, and $22,500 for 2023, with catch-up contributions for those aged 50 and older).

### 2. **IRA (Traditional or Roth):**
   - **Eligibility:** Choose between a Traditional IRA (tax-deductible contributions) and a Roth IRA (tax-free withdrawals in retirement). Your eligibility may depend on your income and filing status.
   - **Tax Benefits:** If you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement, a Traditional IRA might be more beneficial, while a Roth IRA is favorable if you anticipate higher future tax rates.

### 3. **Taxable Accounts:**
   - **Investment Flexibility:** Use taxable accounts for investments you may need to access before retirement (like a home purchase or major expense) or for strategies involving tax-efficient investments (e.g., index funds, ETFs).
   - **Capital Gains:** Be mindful of capital gains taxes when selling investments in a taxable account. Holding investments for over a year can qualify you for lower long-term capital gains tax rates.

### 4. **Diversification and Risk Tolerance:**
   - **Asset Allocation:** Diversify your investments based on your risk tolerance and investment timeline across all account types. Younger investors may lean towards equities, while those closer to retirement might favor bonds.

### 5. **Tax Considerations:**
   - **Withdrawal Strategy:** In retirement, consider the tax implications of withdrawing from these accounts strategically to minimize your overall tax burden.

### Summary:
Evaluate your financial goals and consider meeting with a financial advisor for personalized advice. A well-structured approach could involve maximizing your 401(k) contributions first, then mindful contributions to IRAs, and lastly, investing in taxable accounts, focusing on tax-efficient strategies.
