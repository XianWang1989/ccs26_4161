The best way to distribute your money amongst your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **401(k) Contributions**: If your employer offers a 401(k) plan, consider contributing enough to get any employer match, as this is essentially free money. The 401(k) is beneficial due to tax-deferred growth, meaning you won’t pay taxes on your contributions or investment gains until you withdraw funds during retirement.

2. **IRA Contributions**: After maximizing your employer match in your 401(k), consider contributing to an IRA. If you're eligible for a Roth IRA, this can be a good option for tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Traditional IRAs offer tax-deferred growth, but you’ll pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: After maxing out contributions to your 401(k) and IRA, you can consider investing in a taxable brokerage account. This is useful for short-term savings goals or if you anticipate needing access to your funds before retirement. Keep in mind that you'll pay capital gains taxes on any profits, but this can be managed through strategies such as tax-loss harvesting.

4. **Asset Allocation**: Consider your risk tolerance and time horizon when allocating investments within these accounts. Typically, you might want to hold more aggressive investments like stocks in tax-advantaged accounts (like 401(k) and IRA) where they can grow tax-free or tax-deferred, while holding more conservative investments in taxable accounts.

5. **Tax Considerations**: Be mindful of the tax implications of your distributions and withdrawal strategy in retirement. Aim to withdraw from accounts in a way that minimizes your tax burden in retirement.

It's important to assess your specific situation or consult a financial advisor for a personalized plan based on your individual circumstances.
