[🔒CLASSIC] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts generally depends on your individual financial situation, retirement goals, tax considerations, and investment preferences. Here are some guiding principles to consider:

1. **401(k)**: If your employer offers a 401(k) plan with a matching contribution, prioritize contributing enough to receive the full match, as this is essentially free money. After reaching the match, consider the fees and investment options in your 401(k) compared to other accounts before deciding how much more to contribute.

2. **IRA**: Depending on your income level, you can choose between a Roth IRA and a Traditional IRA. A Roth IRA provides tax-free withdrawals in retirement, while a Traditional IRA offers tax deductions upfront. Contributing to an IRA can be beneficial for tax diversification.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: Use taxable accounts for funds you may need access to before retirement age or for investments that generate lower taxes, such as qualified dividends.

To summarize, prioritize your 401(k) contributions up to the employer match, then consider maxing out your IRA contributions, followed by investing in taxable accounts as needed. It's also advisable to consult with a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your specific circumstances.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts really depends on your financial goals, but here’s a bold approach: 

1. **Go Big on the 401(k)**: Max out your contributions here, especially if there’s an employer match. Think of it as free money! The tax benefits are sweet too.

2. **IRA Strategy**: If you can, put the max into a Roth IRA. It might cost you more upfront, but tax-free growth is a game changer. If you can’t contribute to a Roth due to income limits, pivot to a Traditional IRA.

3. **Taxable Account Playground**: Put any extra funds in a taxable account. This way, you can decide what to do with your money without the restrictions of retirement accounts. Investments in these accounts can be tax-efficient too.

In this wildcard strategy, it's all about maximizing your tax advantages and keeping some flexibility. Just make sure to keep an eye on your long-term goals and adapt as needed. And don't forget to check in with a financial guru to confirm your approach!
