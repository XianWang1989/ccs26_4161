Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, retirement timeline, tax situation, and risk tolerance. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts**
   - **401(k)**: Contribute enough to get any employer match first; it’s essentially free money. Aim to contribute the maximum allowable amount if possible, as contributions reduce your taxable income.
   - **IRA**: Consider funding a Roth IRA if eligible, as withdrawals in retirement can be tax-free. Otherwise, a traditional IRA is a good option if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement.

### 2. **Assess Your Investment Goals**
   - **Long-Term Growth**: Focus on maxing out your 401(k) and IRA for long-term growth. Allocate higher-risk investments in these accounts if you’re young or have a long time until retirement.
   - **Short-Term Needs**: Funds in taxable accounts can be more liquid for short-term goals. Consider keeping cash or stable investments here.

### 3. **Consider Your Tax Situation**
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Be strategic with withdrawals. Long-term capital gains are taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income, so consider holding investments for over a year before selling to take advantage of this.
   - **Asset Location**: Place bonds and high-yield investments in tax-advantaged accounts to reduce tax liability on interest income.

### 4. **Diversify Investments**
   - Across all accounts, ensure you’re diversifying your investments to manage risk. Look into a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets based on your risk tolerance.

### 5. **Regular Rebalancing**
   - Regularly review and adjust your asset allocation to maintain your desired risk level and ensure that you're not overly concentrated in one area.

### Example Strategy:
- **Aggressive Growth**: Max out your 401(k) and IRA first, then invest additional savings in a taxable account.
- **Balanced Approach**: Split contributions across all three accounts, prioritizing tax-advantaged options.

### Conclusion
Ultimately, the best approach varies for each individual. Consider consulting with a financial advisor to tailor a plan that suits your unique situation and goals.
