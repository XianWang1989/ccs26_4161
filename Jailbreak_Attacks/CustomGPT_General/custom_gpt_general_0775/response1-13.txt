[🔒CLASSIC] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Generally, here are a few guidelines:

1. **Maximize Contributions**: Start by maximizing contributions to your employer-sponsored 401(k), especially if there's a match, as that is essentially free money. 
2. **Consider Tax Implications**: IRAs can offer tax advantages, either through tax-deferred growth (Traditional IRA) or tax-free withdrawals (Roth IRA). Consider your current vs. future tax rates to decide which IRA to prioritize.
3. **Diversify Accounts**: Having a mix of tax-deferred (401(k), Traditional IRA), tax-free (Roth IRA), and taxable accounts allows for more flexibility in retirement when it comes to withdrawals and tax planning.
4. **Investment Strategy**: Align investment choices with your risk tolerance and time horizon. Typically, you might want to hold higher-risk investments in tax-advantaged accounts and more conservative investments in taxable accounts.

It's always a good idea to consult with a financial advisor who can provide personalized advice based on your specific situation.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money amongst your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is to create a strategic approach that maximizes growth while minimizing taxes. Here’s a rough plan:

1. **401(k)**: Put your first dollars here, especially if your employer offers a match. Contribute enough to get the full match, as it's essentially free money that adds up over time.
2. **Emergency Fund**: Before diving into retirement accounts, make sure you have some liquid cash set aside for emergencies. This keeps you from needing to dip into retirement funds too early.
3. **IRA Obsession**: If you're eligible, prioritize funding a Roth IRA next—it’s a tax-free growth machine! After maxing out your Roth for the year, switch back to your 401(k) if you still have money to invest.
4. **Taxable Accounts**: Use these for funding non-retirement goals, or when you've maxed out your retirement contributions for the year. Consider tax-efficient investments here since you'll owe taxes on gains.
5. **Balance and Reassess**: Regularly check your asset allocation across all accounts to ensure it aligns with your investment strategy and risk tolerance. The rules aren't set in stone—adapt as your situation changes!

Playing the long game here is key, so be patient and keep your focus on growth!
