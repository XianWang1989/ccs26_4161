[🔒CLASSIC] Distributing your money across a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **Maximize Contributions**: Start by maximizing contributions to your 401(k) if your employer offers a match. This can provide immediate returns through the match.
   
2. **Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: After maximizing your 401(k), consider contributing to an IRA (Traditional or Roth) based on which is more tax-beneficial for you. A Traditional IRA may provide a tax deduction, while a Roth IRA allows for tax-free withdrawals in retirement.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: After maxing out tax-advantaged accounts, you can invest in taxable accounts. This gives you more flexibility and liquidity, although you will have to pay taxes on dividends and capital gains.

4. **Consider Asset Allocation**: Different types of accounts may be suited for different investments. For example, consider placing more tax-efficient investments (like index funds) in taxable accounts and holding bonds (which are less tax-efficient) in tax-advantaged accounts.

5. **Periodic Reviews**: Regularly review your investments and rebalance as necessary to align with your financial goals and market conditions.

Consulting with a financial advisor for personalized advice is also a good idea.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money really depends on your personal circumstances and investment goals, but here's a strategy that can work for many:

1. **Go for the Employer Match**: If your 401(k) has an employer match, that's free money! Contribute at least enough to get the full match before you do anything else.

2. **Fund the Roth IRA**: If you're eligible, consider Roth IRA contributions next. They grow tax-free and can be withdrawn tax-free in retirement, making them a fantastic way to save for the future.

3. **Max Out the 401(k)**: Once the Roth IRA is funded, pump up your 401(k) contributions to the maximum limit. This helps reduce your taxable income and grows your nest egg.

4. **Invest in Taxable Accounts**: After you've hit the 401(k) max, consider taxable accounts for added flexibility. Here, you can invest in stocks or funds, but be mindful of capital gains taxes.

5. **Diversify and Optimize for Taxes**: Put tax-inefficient investments like bonds in your 401(k) or IRA, while putting tax-efficient investments like index funds in taxable accounts.

6. **Keep an Emergency Fund**: Don’t forget to keep some cash set aside for emergencies. This can help you avoid withdrawing early from your retirement accounts.

Ultimately, keep assessing your strategy as life changes. Having a mix lets you take advantage of different benefits while staying flexible!
