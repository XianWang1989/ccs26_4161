To effectively distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

1. **Understand the Account Types**:
   - **401(k)**: Typically offers higher contribution limits and potential employer matching. Contributions are made pre-tax, lowering your taxable income.
   - **IRA (Traditional and Roth)**: IRAs have lower contribution limits compared to 401(k)s but can provide tax advantages. Traditional IRAs offer tax deductions; Roth IRAs provide tax-free withdrawals if certain conditions are met.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These accounts don’t have tax advantages but allow for more flexible withdrawals. Ideal for investments with lower tax burdens.

2. **Maximize Employer Match**: Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match, if available. This is essentially free money.

3. **Prioritize Tax-Efficiency**:
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Use them for investments that produce lower taxable income, such as index funds or long-term capital gains.
   - **Roth IRA**: Best for higher-growth investments as withdrawals are tax-free in retirement. Consider using it for riskier assets since growth isn’t taxed.
   - **Traditional IRA**: Use for conservative investments, as taxes are deferred until withdrawal.

4. **Diversification Across Accounts**: Maintain a diversified investment strategy across all accounts while considering the tax implications of each investment type.

5. **Focus on Long-Term Goals**: Align your contributions with your retirement goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon. Regularly review and adjust your allocations based on life changes or market conditions.

6. **Regular Contributions**: Make consistent contributions to all accounts, taking advantage of dollar-cost averaging to mitigate market volatility.

7. **Seek Professional Advice**: If unsure about the best allocation strategy, consider consulting a financial advisor to tailor a plan based on your unique financial situation.

By considering these factors, you can create a balanced distribution strategy that minimizes taxes and maximizes your savings potential for the future.
