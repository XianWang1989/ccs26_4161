Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your age, risk tolerance, and long-term financial goals. Here’s a general approach:

### 1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts First**
   - **401(k)**: If your employer offers a match, contribute at least enough to get the full match, as this is essentially "free money."
   - **IRA**: Once you've maximized your 401(k) match, consider contributing to a Traditional or Roth IRA. Roth IRAs are especially beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.

### 2. **Taxable Accounts for Flexibility**
   - After maxing out your tax-advantaged accounts, allocate additional funds to taxable accounts for more liquidity and flexibility. This can be useful for short-term goals or if you expect to need access to your money before retirement.

### 3. **Asset Allocation**
   - Diversify your investments based on your time horizon and risk tolerance. Generally, younger investors may opt for a more aggressive mix (stocks), while those closer to retirement might choose a conservative approach (bonds).

### 4. **Consider Tax Implications**
   - Understand the tax implications of selling investments in a taxable account versus those in tax-advantaged accounts. Long-term capital gains in taxable accounts are taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income.

### 5. **Regular Review and Rebalance**
   - Periodically review your asset allocation and adjust contributions as needed to align with your financial goals and market conditions.

### Conclusion
A balanced approach, starting with maximization of tax-advantaged accounts followed by contributions to taxable accounts, typically provides the best outcome regarding tax efficiency and goal attainment. It's also wise to consult a financial advisor to tailor a strategy specific to your situation.
