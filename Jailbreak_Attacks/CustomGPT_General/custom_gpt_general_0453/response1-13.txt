The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, investment preferences, and time horizon. Here are some general principles to consider:

### 1. **Understand Account Types**

- **401(k):** Employer-sponsored retirement plan with potential employer matching. Contributions are pre-tax, reducing taxable income. Distributions are taxed as ordinary income.
  
- **Traditional IRA:** Similar to a 401(k) but not employer-sponsored. Contributions may be tax-deductible depending on your income and whether you have access to a 401(k). Taxes apply upon withdrawal.

- **Roth IRA:** Contributions made with after-tax dollars. Qualified withdrawals are tax-free. Good for those expecting to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.

- **Taxable Accounts:** These accounts do not offer tax advantages but allow for flexibility in withdrawals and investments. Capital gains and dividends are taxed, but you're not penalized for early withdrawals.

### 2. **Prioritize Contributions**

- **Maximize Employer Match:** Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match if it's available. This is essentially free money.

- **Max Out Tax-Advantaged Accounts:** Next, consider maxing out your IRA contributions, especially if it's a Roth IRA, which can provide tax-free growth.

- **Taxable Accounts for Additional Savings:** Once you've maximized your tax-advantaged accounts, you can use taxable accounts for additional savings. These can be useful for short- to medium-term goals, but be mindful of tax implications.

### 3. **Consider Investment Options and Fees**

- **Investment Choices:** 401(k) plans often have limited investment options. Choose funds that align with your risk tolerance and investment strategy. For IRAs and taxable accounts, you might have more flexibility in your investment choices.

- **Fees:** Be aware of the fees associated with your accounts. Lower fees can lead to higher long-term returns.

### 4. **Factor in Your Financial Goals and Time Horizon**

- **Retirement Goals:** If you're far from retirement, you might lean more heavily on stocks for growth in your 401(k) and IRAs. As you approach retirement, consider shifting to more stable investments.

- **Other Financial Goals:** If you have short-term goals (buying a house, starting a business), you may want to keep more in taxable accounts.

### 5. **Tax Considerations**

- **Current and Future Tax Bracket:** Consider your current tax bracket and expected tax bracket during retirement. If you expect to be in a higher bracket, a Roth IRA may be more beneficial.

- **Withdrawal Strategy:** Plan how you will withdraw funds in retirement to minimize tax liability. It can be beneficial to withdraw from different accounts strategically.

### Example Distribution Strategy:
- **401(k):** Max out contributions, particularly to get any employer match.
- **IRA:** Contribute the maximum allowed, prioritizing Roth if eligible and beneficial.
- **Taxable Account:** Use any excess funds to invest in a taxable account, focusing on tax-efficient investments.

### Conclusion
Ultimately, tailoring your distribution strategy to fit your unique financial situation and goals is crucial. Consulting with a financial advisor can provide personalized guidance based on your specific circumstances.
