To determine the best way to distribute your money amongst your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following factors:

1. **Tax Implications**: 
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are typically made pre-tax, which can reduce your taxable income for the year. However, you will pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement.
   - **IRA**: The traditional IRA also offers tax-deferred growth, but contributions may be limited based on income. Roth IRAs allow for tax-free withdrawals, but contributions are made with after-tax dollars.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Income generated (like dividends or capital gains) is taxed in the year it’s realized, so this account should typically hold investments with lower turnover and qualified dividends.

2. **Investment Goals**: Determine your investment goals (retirement, short-term savings, etc.) as this influences account allocation. Long-term growth is more suitable for retirement accounts due to their tax advantages.

3. **Contribution Limits**: Be aware of the annual contribution limits for each account type. For example, 401(k) limits may differ from those of IRAs.

4. **Emergency Funds**: Keep sufficient liquidity in taxable accounts for emergencies, as accessing funds in retirement accounts before a certain age may incur penalties.

5. **Diversification**: Ensure that your asset allocation is diversified across these accounts; avoid over-concentrating your investments.

6. **Employer match**: If your employer offers a match on your 401(k) contributions, consider maximizing this benefit first, as it's essentially "free money."

By assessing these factors, you can tailor a distribution strategy that aligns with your financial situation and goals. Consulting with a financial advisor may also provide personalized guidance.
