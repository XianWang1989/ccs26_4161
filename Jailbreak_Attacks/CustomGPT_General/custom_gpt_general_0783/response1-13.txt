When considering how to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, here are some key factors to keep in mind:

### 1. **Tax Benefits:**
   - **401(k):** Contributions are often pre-tax, reducing your taxable income. Some plans even offer employer matching, which is essentially free money. Maximize this before moving to other accounts.
   - **IRA:** Traditional IRAs may offer tax deductions, while Roth IRAs provide tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** You pay taxes on dividends and capital gains, but these accounts offer greater flexibility for withdrawals without penalties.

### 2. **Contribution Limits:**
   - Be aware of the annual contribution limits for each account type. For 2023, the limits are:
     - **401(k):** $22,500 (or $30,000 if you're over 50)
     - **IRA:** $6,500 (or $7,500 if you're over 50)
   - Prioritize contributing enough to your 401(k) to get any employer match, then consider IRAs as they often have more investment options.

### 3. **Investment Goals:**
   - Determine your short-term vs. long-term goals. Taxable accounts might be better for short-term needs, while retirement accounts are suitable for long-term growth.

### 4. **Withdrawal Flexibility:**
   - 401(k) and IRAs have restrictions on withdrawals, especially before age 59½. Taxable accounts provide more flexibility for accessing funds.

### 5. **Investment Strategy:**
   - Consider how each type of account fits into your overall investment strategy. For example, you might choose to hold more aggressive investments in your tax-advantaged accounts, while favoring more conservative investments in taxable accounts to minimize tax impacts.

### 6. **Diversification:**
   - Ensure your overall portfolio is well-diversified across all accounts to mitigate risk.

### Suggested Strategy:
1. **Max out employer match on your 401(k).**
2. **Contribute to an IRA (consider a Roth if you qualify).**
3. **Continue to invest in your 401(k) up to the limit if possible.**
4. **Use taxable accounts for additional savings or investments after maximizing retirement accounts.**

### Conclusion:
The best distribution strategy often involves maximizing tax-advantaged accounts first, while making sure to meet your personal financial goals and maintaining liquidity as needed. It's beneficial to consult with a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your specific situation.
