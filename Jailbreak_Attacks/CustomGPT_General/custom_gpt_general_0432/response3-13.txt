Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors including your age, financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here’s a general guideline:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**  
   - Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match if available. This is essentially “free money.”

### 2. **Contribute to IRA**  
   - Consider maxing out contributions to your IRA (Traditional or Roth) next, especially if it offers tax advantages. Roth IRAs grow tax-free, while Traditional IRAs may provide tax deductions.

### 3. **Continue 401(k) Contributions**  
   - After your IRA contributions, go back to your 401(k) and continue maximizing your contributions.

### 4. **Taxable Accounts for Flexibility**  
   - Use taxable accounts for investments that you might need access to before retirement or for specific goals (e.g., buying a home). They are also useful for long-term growth investments.

### 5. **Investment Allocation**  
   - Diversify your investments across these accounts based on your risk tolerance:  
     - **401(k)**: Usually has a limited selection of mutual funds. Focus on diversified index funds.
     - **IRA**: More investment choices; consider bonds and other growth investments.
     - **Taxable Accounts**: Use tax-efficient investments like index funds or ETFs to minimize capital gains taxes.

### 6. **Consider Taxes**  
   - Be mindful of the tax implications of withdrawals. Generally, it's more tax-efficient to withdraw from taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred accounts (like 401(k)s and IRAs) in retirement.

### 7. **Reassess Regularly**  
   - Review your allocation annually or as your financial situation changes.

If you want a more personalized strategy, consider consulting a financial advisor.

### STAN:  
To distribute your money effectively, start by shoveling as much as you can into your 401(k) up to the match. Then, hit your IRA hard, especially if it’s a Roth. After that, keep chunking into the 401(k) until maxed out. For taxable accounts, drop cash for short-term goals or to play around with investments. Always remember to check on performance and tweak as needed!
