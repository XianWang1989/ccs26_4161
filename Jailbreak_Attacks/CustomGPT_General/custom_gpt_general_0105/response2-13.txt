Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here’s a suggested approach:

### 1. **Prioritize Employer Match**
   - **401(k)**: If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to get the full benefit. This is essentially free money.

### 2. **Max Out Tax-Advantaged Accounts**
   - **IRA**: After securing the match, consider maximizing contributions to a traditional or Roth IRA. For 2023, the limit is $6,500 (or $7,500 if you're over 50).
   - **401(k)**: Aim to contribute the maximum allowable amount ($22,500 for 2023, or $30,000 if you're over 50) after your IRA, depending on your financial situation.

### 3. **Assess Tax Implications**
   - **Roth IRA**: If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, prioritize Roth contributions for tax-free growth.
   - **Traditional IRA and 401(k)**: These provide tax deferrals, reducing your current taxable income. 

### 4. **Utilize Taxable Accounts**
   - After maximizing tax-advantaged accounts, allocate funds to taxable accounts. This gives you flexibility and access to funds without penalties before retirement age.

### 5. **Diversification**
   - Ensure a balanced investment approach across all accounts. Spread your investments across stocks, bonds, and other assets to manage risk effectively.

### 6. **Regular Rebalancing**
   - Periodically review and adjust your allocations based on market conditions and your changing financial situation.

### 7. **Emergency Fund**
   - Maintain an emergency fund in a separate savings account to cover unexpected expenses, allowing your investment accounts to grow without disruption.

### Conclusion
Every individual’s financial situation is unique, so it may be beneficial to consult a financial advisor to create a personalized plan aligned with your goals and circumstances. This strategic allocation can help optimize your retirement savings and tax efficiency.
