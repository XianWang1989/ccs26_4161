Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment time horizon. Here are some considerations for each type of account:

### 1. **401(k)**
   - **Benefits**: Often includes employer matching contributions, which is free money. Contributions are tax-deferred until withdrawal.
   - **Strategy**:
     - **Maximize Contributions**: Start by contributing enough to get the full employer match.
     - **Consider Fees**: Review the investment options and fees associated with your 401(k).

### 2. **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**
   - **Traditional IRA**: Contributions are tax-deductible, but withdrawals are taxed.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made after-tax, and qualified withdrawals are tax-free.
   - **Strategy**:
     - **Roth vs. Traditional**: If you anticipate being in a higher tax bracket in retirement, a Roth IRA might be more advantageous. If you expect a lower bracket, consider a Traditional IRA.
     - **Contribution Limits**: Be mindful of annual contribution limits for both types of IRAs ($6,500 in 2023; $7,500 if over 50).

### 3. **Taxable Accounts**
   - **Benefits**: More flexibility and liquidity; no contribution limits or penalties for withdrawals.
   - **Strategy**:
     - **Invest in Tax-Efficient Funds**: Consider index funds or ETFs to minimize taxable events from capital gains.
     - **Use for Short-Term Goals**: Ideal for goals within the next 5-10 years or for emergency funds.

### **General Tips for Distribution**
- **Assess Your Risk Tolerance**: Allocate investments based on your comfort with risk. Younger investors might favor stocks, while those closer to retirement may prefer bonds.
- **Diversification**: Spread investments across various asset classes to reduce volatility.
- **Tax Considerations**: Be strategic about where to place certain types of investments. For instance, put high-growth stocks in tax-advantaged accounts to defer taxes on gains.

### **Sample Allocation Strategy**
- **If you're under 50**:
  - 401(k): 15-20%
  - IRA (Traditional/Roth): 10-15%
  - Taxable account: 20-30%
- **If you're 50 or older** (considering retirement soon):
  - 401(k): 15-25%
  - IRA (Traditional/Roth): 15-20%
  - Taxable account: 10-20%

### **Conclusion**
The best approach is personalized. Regularly review your allocations to adapt to changes in income, tax law, or financial goals. Consulting with a financial advisor can also help tailor a strategy suited to your specific situation.
