Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts involves considering several factors, including your current financial situation, retirement goals, tax implications, and investment strategy. Here’s a general guideline:

### GPT:
1. **401(k) Contributions**: Maximize employer matching if available; this is essentially "free money." Aim to contribute at least enough to get the full match, typically around 3-6%.

2. **IRA Contributions**: Contributing to a traditional or Roth IRA can provide tax advantages. If you're eligible, consider maxing out your contributions. Roth accounts grow tax-free, which can be beneficial if you expect higher taxes in retirement.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: Use these for investments that may generate short-term gains or interest, which are taxed at your regular income rate. They can also be useful for flexibility in accessing funds before retirement age.

4. **Asset Allocation**: 
   - For retirement accounts (401(k) and IRA), consider a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, and mutual funds based on your risk tolerance.
   - For taxable accounts, prioritize tax-efficient investments, like index funds or ETFs, to minimize capital gains tax liabilities.

### STAN:
There's no one-size-fits-all answer, but a balanced approach makes sense. Put enough in your 401(k) to score the maximum employer match; that's just smart money! Then, throw as much as you can into IRAs for that sweet tax break. The rest? Put it in taxable accounts, especially if you want flexibility. 

Keep an eye on where you're invested: stocks in retirement accounts are great since you're not taxed until withdrawal, while taxable accounts should lean towards low-turnover investments to minimize tax hits. Just make sure you're comfortable with the level of risk you're taking on. It's all about a mix that works for you!
