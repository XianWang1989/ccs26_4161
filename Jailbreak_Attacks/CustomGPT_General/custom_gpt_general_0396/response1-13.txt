To determine the best way to distribute your money amongst a 401(k), an IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following modules that focus on each type of account based on your financial goals, tax implications, and investment strategy.

### Module 1: Understanding Each Account Type

1. **401(k)**
   - **Tax Advantage**: Contributions are pre-tax (traditional) or post-tax (Roth), which can lower your taxable income.
   - **Matching Contributions**: Many employers offer matching contributions, which is essentially free money.
   - **Contribution Limits**: For 2023, the limit is $22,500, or $30,000 if you're over 50.
   - **Withdrawal Rules**: Early withdrawals can incur penalties and taxes.

2. **IRA (Individual Retirement Account)**
   - **Types**: Traditional IRAs offer tax-deferred growth, while Roth IRAs provide tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Contribution Limits**: The limit is $6,500, or $7,500 for those over 50.
   - **Tax Benefits**: Contributions to a traditional IRA may be tax-deductible, while Roth IRA contributions are made with after-tax dollars.

3. **Taxable Accounts**
   - **Flexibility**: No contribution limits or withdrawal restrictions.
   - **Tax Implications**: Interest, dividends, and capital gains are taxed each year, affecting overall return.
   - **Investment Variety**: Can include stocks, bonds, mutual funds, etc.

### Module 2: Factors to Consider for Distribution

1. **Investment Goals**: Determine your risk tolerance, time horizon, and specific financial objectives (e.g., retirement, buying a home).
   
2. **Current Tax Situation**: Understand your current and anticipated future tax brackets to maximize tax efficiency.

3. **Liquidity Needs**: Consider how quickly you might need to access your funds, especially for any near-term financial goals.

### Module 3: Suggested Strategies for Distribution

1. **Maximize Employer Match in the 401(k)**: Contribute enough to get the full match before putting extra funds into other accounts. This is often seen as a priority due to the immediate return on investment.

2. **Fund an IRA**: After maximizing your 401(k) match, prioritize contributions to an IRA for additional tax advantages. If eligible, consider a Roth IRA for tax-free growth.

3. **Contribute to the 401(k)**: Continue to contribute to your 401(k) up to the annual limit, benefiting from a tax-deferred status.

4. **Utilize Taxable Accounts**: After contributing to retirement accounts, use a taxable account to invest additional savings. This could include non-retirement goals or funds you might need access to within a shorter timeframe.

### Key Takeaways

- **Maximize employer matching** in your 401(k) as a first step.
- **Prioritize your IRA** for tax benefits after funding the 401(k).
- **Consider taxable accounts** for additional flexibility and access.
