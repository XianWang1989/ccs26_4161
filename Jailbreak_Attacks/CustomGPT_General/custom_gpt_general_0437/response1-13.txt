Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon. Here’s a general approach to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match:**
   - **401(k):** If your employer offers a matching contribution, contribute enough to take full advantage of this benefit. It's essentially "free money."

### 2. **Consider Tax Implications:**
   - **Traditional IRA/401(k):** Contributions are often tax-deductible, reducing your taxable income in the year of contribution. However, withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
   - **Roth IRA:** Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free, making this account attractive if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Interest, dividends, and capital gains are subject to annual taxation. These accounts are more flexible but less tax-efficient for long-term savings.

### 3. **Determine Your Goals and Time Horizon:**
   - If you’re saving for retirement, prioritize tax-advantaged accounts like 401(k)s and IRAs. Allocate funds based on your expected retirement age.
   - If you need access to funds sooner (e.g., buying a house), a taxable account may be more appropriate.

### 4. **Diversify Investments:**
   - Within each account, diversify your investments to manage risk. Consider a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets according to your risk tolerance.

### 5. **Review Contribution Limits:**
   - Stay informed about annual contribution limits for each account type to optimize your funding strategy.

### 6. **Regularly Reassess Your Strategy:**
   - Monitor your financial situation and goals regularly. Adjust your contributions and investment allocations as necessary.

Consulting with a financial advisor can provide personalized advice tailored to your specific circumstances.
