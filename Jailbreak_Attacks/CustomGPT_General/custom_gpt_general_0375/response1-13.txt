[🔒CLASSIC] Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **Maximize Employer Match:** If your employer offers a matching contribution to your 401(k), contribute enough to capture the full match before diverting funds elsewhere.

2. **Consider Contribution Limits:** Be aware of the contribution limits for both 401(k) and IRAs. For 2023, the limit is $22,500 for 401(k)s ($30,000 if you're over 50) and $6,500 for IRAs ($7,500 if you're over 50).

3. **Tax Implications:** Traditional 401(k) and IRA contributions are tax-deferred, meaning you'll pay taxes upon withdrawal in retirement. Roth IRAs, however, are funded with after-tax money, allowing tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Taxable accounts do not offer tax advantages like these but provide more flexibility for withdrawals.

4. **Diversification:** Aim to diversify your investments across different account types to balance tax advantages strategically. 

5. **Retirement Timeline:** If you’re close to retirement, prioritize tax-deferred accounts for their immediate benefits. If you're younger, consider focusing on Roth accounts for long-term tax-free growth.

6. **Consult a Professional:** Consider speaking with a financial advisor for personalized advice based on your situation.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts really depends on your specific financial situation, but here’s a bold strategy you might consider:

1. **First, Take Full Advantage of Your 401(k) Match:** If your employer matches your contributions, throw enough in there to get the maximum match—it's basically free money!

2. **Next, Prioritize Roth Accounts (if you qualify):** If you can afford it, contribute to a Roth IRA. Since you pay taxes on that money now, all the growth in the account can be withdrawn tax-free in retirement, which is fantastic.

3. **Balance with Taxable Accounts:** Once you’ve maxed out your 401(k) contributions and contributed to a Roth IRA (or a traditional IRA depending on your tax situation), any extra savings can go into a taxable account. It's more flexible, and while you’ll pay taxes on dividends and capital gains, it allows access without penalties before retirement age.

4. **Consider Your Timeline and Goals:** If you're aiming for early retirement or have medium-term goals, you might want to keep a larger portion in taxable accounts. For long-term thinkers, lean more on tax-advantaged accounts.

5. **Reassess Regularly:** Make it a habit to review and adjust your strategy as your financial situation and tax laws change. 

6. **Hit Up Financial Tools:** Leverage investment platforms that help you strategize and automate investments based on your risk tolerance and goals.

Ultimately, keep your eyes on the prize—aim for a blend of maximizing retirement savings while ensuring you have accessible funds for unexpected needs!
