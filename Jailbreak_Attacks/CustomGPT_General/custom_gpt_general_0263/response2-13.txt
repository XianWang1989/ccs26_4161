When distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match on your 401(k), contribute enough to maximize this benefit. It's essentially free money.

2. **Tax Efficiency**:
   - **401(k)**: This account is often pre-tax, meaning contributions lower your taxable income. It’s great for long-term growth with tax-deferred compounding.
   - **IRA**: Depending on whether it’s a Traditional or Roth IRA, you may benefit from tax deductions now (Traditional) or tax-free withdrawals in retirement (Roth).
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These are less tax-efficient for long-term investments but offer flexibility for withdrawals without penalties.

3. **Investment Goals**: Align your investments with your goals and time horizon. If you're younger, you might lean more heavily into growth-oriented assets (stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts. As you approach retirement, consider shifting to more conservative investments.

4. **Consider Fees and Expenses**: Compare the fees associated with each account type. Sometimes, 401(k) plans might have higher fees than IRAs or taxable accounts.

5. **Diversification**: Keep your asset allocation in mind across all accounts to ensure a balanced risk profile. 

6. **Withdrawal Strategy**: Think about how you'll withdraw during retirement. Taxable accounts can provide immediate cash without penalties, while tax-advantaged accounts may have tax implications upon withdrawal.

### Suggested Allocation Example
- **401(k)**: 10-15% (enough to get the full company match)
- **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**: 10-15%
- **Taxable Accounts**: Remainder (consider maxing out tax-advantaged accounts first)

Consider consulting a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your specific situation.
