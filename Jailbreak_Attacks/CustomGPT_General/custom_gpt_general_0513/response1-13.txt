To tailor a strategy for distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following factors and steps:

1. **Tax Considerations**:
   - **401(k) and IRA**: Contributions to these accounts may be tax-deferred (traditional) or tax-free (Roth). If you're in a lower tax bracket now, you might prefer Roth accounts.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Dividends and capital gains are taxable in the year they are realized, so consider how active your investment strategy will be.

2. **Contribution Limits**:
   - Make sure you're aware of annual contribution limits for your 401(k) and IRAs. For 2023, the limit is $22,500 for 401(k) plans ($30,000 if over 50), and $6,500 for IRAs ($7,500 if over 50).

3. **Emergency Fund**:
   - Before investing, ensure you have an emergency fund in place, typically covering 3 to 6 months of living expenses.

4. **Investment Goals and Timeline**:
   - Align your investment strategy with your goals (retirement, purchasing a home, etc.) and your time horizon. Longer timelines can generally handle more risk.

5. **Diversification**:
   - Use your accounts to diversify investments. For example, consider holding more volatile assets in your retirement accounts (where you won’t need to access funds soon), while keeping more conservative investments in taxable accounts.

6. **Rebalance Regularly**:
   - Periodically review and adjust your allocation based on changes in your financial situation and market conditions.

Here’s a sample distribution strategy:
- **401(k)**: Max out contributions to take advantage of employer matching (if available).
- **Traditional IRA**: If you are eligible for tax-deductible contributions, consider contributing up to the limit.
- **Roth IRA**: If eligible, contribute to a Roth IRA for tax-free growth.
- **Taxable Accounts**: Use these for more liquid investments or to invest any leftover funds after maximizing retirement contributions.

Consult with a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your specific financial situation, including your risk tolerance and investment preferences. They can also help assess the impact of fees and investment choices in each account type.
