Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can depend on several factors, including your age, investment goals, current financial situation, and tax considerations. Here’s a general approach to consider:

### 1. **Identify Your Goals**
   - **Retirement**: Focus on tax-advantaged accounts like 401(k) and IRA for long-term growth.
   - **Short-term Needs**: Use taxable accounts for easier access without penalties.

### 2. **Maximize Employer Matches**
   - If your employer offers a match on your 401(k), contribute at least enough to get the full match first, as this is essentially free money.

### 3. **Understand Contribution Limits**
   - **401(k)**: For 2023, the limit is $22,500 (or $30,000 if you’re over 50).
   - **IRA**: The limit is $6,500 (or $7,500 for those over 50).

### 4. **Consider Tax Implications**
   - **Traditional Accounts**: Contributions are often tax-deductible, but withdrawals are taxed as income.
   - **Roth Accounts**: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, allowing tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Taxes are owed on any capital gains or income earned.

### 5. **Diversification**
   - Ensure that your investments across all accounts are diversified and align with your risk tolerance and time horizon.

### 6. **Periodic Review**
   - Regularly assess your allocations based on changes in your financial situation or market conditions.

### Sample Allocation Strategy:
- **Younger Investors (20s-30s)**:
  - 401(k): 15-20%
  - IRA: 10-15%
  - Taxable: 5-10%
  
- **Mid-Age Investors (40s-50s)**:
  - 401(k): 20-30%
  - IRA: 15-20%
  - Taxable: 10-15%
  
- **Near Retirement (60s)**:
  - 401(k): 25-35%
  - IRA: 20-25%
  - Taxable: 15-20%

### Conclusion
It's important to tailor your strategy to your unique situation and consider consulting a financial advisor for personalized advice. This strategy can help you balance growth and liquidity in a tax-efficient manner.
