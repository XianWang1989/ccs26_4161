To determine the best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following factors:

1. **Tax Considerations**: 
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are pre-tax, which can lower your taxable income now, but withdrawals will be taxed as ordinary income during retirement.
   - **IRA (Traditional and Roth)**: Traditional IRAs are tax-deferred, while Roth IRAs allow for tax-free withdrawals in retirement since contributions are made with after-tax dollars.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investment gains in these accounts are taxed annually on dividends and realized gains, but you can also take advantage of lower capital gains taxes on investments held for over a year.

2. **Contribution Limits**: Make sure to stay within contribution limits for each account type. For 2023, the 401(k) limit is $22,500 (or $30,000 if age 50 or older), the IRA contribution limit is $6,500 ($7,500 if 50 or older), so plan accordingly.

3. **Investment Goals and Time Horizon**: If you are investing for long-term growth, consider utilizing tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs and 401(k)s for the bulk of your investments. For shorter-term goals, a taxable account may be more appropriate.

4. **Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match for your 401(k), contribute enough to take full advantage of it, as it's essentially "free money."

5. **Diversification and Asset Allocation**: Regardless of the account type, maintain a diversified portfolio that aligns with your risks and investment horizon. 

### Suggested Strategy:
- Max out any employer match in your 401(k).
- Contribute to your IRA, prioritizing a Roth IRA if you qualify for tax-free withdrawals.
- Use any remaining funds for additional contributions to your 401(k) and then fill up your taxable accounts.

It's often beneficial to consult with a financial advisor to tailor a strategy that fits your individual financial situation and long-term goals.
