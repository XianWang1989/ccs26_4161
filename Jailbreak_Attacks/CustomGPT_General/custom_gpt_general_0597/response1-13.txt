Distributing your money efficiently among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, age, and risk tolerance. Here’s a general guide to help you decide:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Contributions**
   - **401(k)**: Start by contributing enough to get any employer match, as this is essentially free money.

### 2. **Consider Tax Implications**
   - **Traditional 401(k) and IRA**: Contributions reduce your taxable income now, but you'll owe taxes on withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax money, but withdrawals are tax-free in retirement, making it ideal if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These are taxed on dividends, interest, and short-term capital gains. They provide flexibility but can lead to a higher tax burden.

### 3. **Prioritize Retirement Accounts**
   - After meeting the employer match, maximize contributions to your IRA or Roth IRA (if eligible), as these accounts typically have more favorable tax treatment.
   - Consider maxing out your 401(k) next, especially if you anticipate being in a lower tax bracket during retirement.

### 4. **Utilize Taxable Accounts Strategically**
   - If you have additional money after maxing out tax-advantaged accounts, consider using taxable accounts for investments with favorable tax treatment, like long-term capital gains and qualified dividends.
   - Use tax-loss harvesting strategies to offset gains.

### 5. **Review Periodically**
   - Your financial situation, tax laws, and market conditions can change. Regularly review and adjust your contributions and investment allocations accordingly.

### 6. **Consult a Financial Advisor**
   - For personalized advice tailored to your unique circumstances, consider working with a financial advisor.

### Example Strategy:
- Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match.
- Max out your IRA/Roth IRA if eligible.
- After those are maxed out, continue funding your 401(k) up to the IRS limits, and allocate any excess funds to a taxable account.

This balanced approach helps optimize tax benefits while ensuring liquidity and growth potential.
