The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, risk tolerance, investment timeline, and tax considerations. Here’s a general approach to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**: If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), aim to contribute at least enough to get the full match, as this is essentially free money.

2. **Contribute to an IRA**: If your income allows, consider contributing to a Traditional IRA or Roth IRA. A Roth IRA can provide tax-free withdrawals in retirement if certain conditions are met, which can be beneficial for long-term tax strategy.

3. **Prioritize Taxable Accounts if Necessary**: Once you’ve maxed out any 401(k) match and contributed to an IRA, consider investing in taxable accounts for additional flexibility. Taxable accounts also offer access to funds without penalties, unlike retirement accounts if you need funds before retirement age.

4. **Consider Asset Location**: When distributing investments across these accounts, think about asset location. Generally, place income-generating investments that produce interest and dividends (like bonds) in tax-advantaged accounts (401(k) and IRA). Place investments that are expected to grow in value (like stocks) in taxable accounts to benefit from lower long-term capital gains rates.

5. **Tax Considerations and Withdrawal Strategy**: Consider your current and expected future tax rates. You may want to withdraw from accounts in an order that minimizes taxes; typically, this means withdrawing from taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred accounts, and finally tax-free accounts.

6. **Adjust Based on Goals and Timeline**: Your distribution may also depend on whether you are saving for short-term goals (home purchase, etc.) or long-term (retirement). Review and adjust your contributions as needed periodically.

In conclusion, it's common to first secure your employer match, contribute to tax-advantaged accounts like an IRA, then allocate extra funds to a taxable account. It's advisable to consult with a financial advisor to tailor your distribution strategy based on your personal circumstances.
