[🔒CLASSIC] Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here are some general guidelines:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your 401(k) offers an employer match, contribute enough to take full advantage of it. This is essentially free money.

2. **IRA Contributions**: If you're eligible, consider contributing to a Traditional IRA or Roth IRA, as these accounts offer tax advantages. Roth IRAs, for instance, provide tax-free withdrawals in retirement.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: After maximizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts, consider funding taxable accounts. This provides flexibility, as you can access the money without penalties before retirement, though you'll pay taxes on capital gains.

4. **Asset Allocation**: Focus on your investment strategy. Generally, stocks may be more suitable for long-term growth in tax-advantaged accounts, while bonds or dividend-paying stocks could be considered for taxable accounts to manage tax impacts.

5. **Considerations**: Regularly review your allocations based on changes in your financial situation, market conditions, and retirement goals.

It's advisable to consult with a financial advisor for personalized advice tailored to your situation.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts really depends on your individual financial goals and risk tolerance. Here’s a savvy approach:

1. **Max it Out**: First, get the full benefit of any employer match in your 401(k). That’s basically free cash! Aim to contribute at least enough to achieve that match.

2. **IRA Choices**: If you're eligible, fund a Roth IRA next. It's excellent for tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. If you're looking to lower your current taxable income, go for a Traditional IRA.

3. **Taxable Accounts for Flexibility**: After maxing out your tax-advantaged accounts, throw any extra cash into a taxable brokerage account. They offer great flexibility since you can access your funds anytime without penalties.

4. **Invest Wisely**: Invest more aggressively in your tax-advantaged accounts since you won't pay taxes on gains until withdrawal or ever in the case of a Roth. In taxable accounts, lean towards investments that generate qualified dividends or long-term capital gains — they get preferential tax treatment.

5. **Review and Adjust**: As your financial situation changes or as you near retirement, reevaluate your stances to adapt to new circumstances.

Overall, a well-rounded approach could set you up for financial success, and sometimes, it’s worth chatting with a pro to really hone in on what’s best for you!
