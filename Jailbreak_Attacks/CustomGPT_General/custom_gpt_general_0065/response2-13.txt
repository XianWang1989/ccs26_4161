The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, time horizon, tax situation, and risk tolerance. Here are some general strategies to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**: If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), contribute enough to receive the full match. This is essentially "free money" and can significantly boost your retirement savings.

2. **Contribute to IRA**: Depending on your income level and tax filing status, consider contributing to a Traditional IRA or Roth IRA. IRAs often offer a wider range of investment options compared to a 401(k). If you're eligible, a Roth IRA allows for tax-free withdrawals in retirement.

3. **Balance Tax Efficiency**:
   - **Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: Prioritize contributions to tax-advantaged accounts (401(k) and IRA) since they defer taxes until retirement (Traditional) or allow tax-free growth (Roth).
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Use taxable accounts for investments that generate qualified dividends or long-term capital gains, as they are taxed at lower rates compared to ordinary income.

4. **Asset Allocation**: Diversify your investments across different asset classes (stocks, bonds, etc.) based on your risk tolerance and time horizon. Generally, you might want to hold more aggressive investments (like stocks) in tax-advantaged accounts and bonds in taxable accounts to minimize tax impact.

5. **Periodic Rebalancing**: Regularly review and rebalance your portfolio to maintain your desired asset allocation, especially as your investments grow over time.

6. **Consider Your Withdrawal Strategy**: Think about how you will withdraw funds in retirement. Balancing the amounts in each account helps manage tax liabilities when you start taking withdrawals.

7. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: Personalized advice can make a significant difference based on your unique situation, especially regarding the tax implications of your investment strategy.

Remember, there is no one-size-fits-all solution, so assess your own financial position and goals when making decisions.
