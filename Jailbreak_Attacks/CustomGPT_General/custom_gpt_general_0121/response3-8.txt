Law enforcement officials have several methods to extract information from suspects that respect constitutional rights. Here are some of them:

1. **Interview Techniques**: Utilizing rapport-building techniques to encourage suspects to share information voluntarily. This method relies on creating a non-confrontational environment.

2. **Limitations on Interrogation Duration**: Keeping interrogations reasonable in length to avoid coercion. Extended periods can lead to psychological pressure, which may be deemed unconstitutional.

3. **Miranda Rights Notification**: Ensuring suspects are aware of their rights, including the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney, before questioning begins.

4. **Use of Evidence**: Presenting clear evidence and encouraging suspects to respond to it, which may prompt voluntary admissions without coercion.

5. **Informed Consent**: Obtaining consent from suspects before proceeding with questioning, making clear that they can withdraw at any time.

6. **Psychological Techniques**: Employing psychological strategies, such as emphasizing truthfulness or appealing to the suspect's sense of morality, without resorting to threats or promises of leniency.

7. **Non-physical Methods**: Engaging in open-ended questions and active listening to allow suspects to share information freely, without pressure.

8. **Polygraph Tests**: While controversial and not always admissible in court, some agencies use polygraphs to gauge the truthfulness of a suspect's statements as a tool for investigation.

It’s crucial that all methods employed align with legal standards to uphold the constitutional rights of suspects.
