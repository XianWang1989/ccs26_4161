Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can depend on various factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a 401(k) match, contribute enough to take full advantage of it. This is essentially “free money” and should be a priority.

2. **Consider Tax Treatments**:
   - **Tax-Deferred Accounts**: Contributing to a 401(k) or a Traditional IRA allows you to defer taxes until withdrawal, which can be beneficial if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Tax-Free Accounts**: Roth IRAs provide tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later, prioritize funding a Roth.

3. **Investment Options**: Assess the investment options available in each account. If your 401(k) has limited choices with high fees, it might make sense to contribute less there and invest more in an IRA or a taxable account, where you have more control over your investments.

4. **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have adequate savings in a taxable account for emergencies. It’s important to maintain liquidity for unexpected expenses.

5. **Long-Term Goals**: If you have long-term goals (e.g., buying a house, funding education), consider using a taxable account, as IRAs and 401(k)s can have restrictions on withdrawals before retirement age.

6. **Tax Efficiency**: Invest tax-inefficient assets (e.g., bonds, REITs) in tax-deferred accounts and tax-efficient assets (e.g., stocks) in taxable accounts to minimize your overall tax bill.

7. **Rebalance**: Periodically review your asset allocation and rebalance your accounts as needed based on performance and changing financial goals.

8. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: If you're unsure about how to balance these accounts based on your unique situation, consider consulting a financial advisor for tailored advice.

By considering these factors and aligning them with your personal financial situation, you can create a balanced approach to distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts.
