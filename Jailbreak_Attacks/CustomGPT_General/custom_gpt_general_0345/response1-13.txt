To effectively distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

### 1. **Understand Account Types and Tax Implications**:
   - **401(k)**: This is a tax-advantaged retirement account where contributions are often tax-deductible. Focus on maximizing employer contributions, especially if they match.
   - **IRA**: Traditional IRAs also offer tax deductions on contributions, while Roth IRAs allow tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Choose based on your anticipated tax bracket at retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These are more flexible with no contribution limits and allow you to withdraw at any time, but you will be taxed on any gains or dividends.

### 2. **Prioritize Contributions**:
   - Start by maximizing contributions to your 401(k) up to the employer match, as it's essentially free money.
   - Next, contribute to an IRA, especially if you're eligible for a Roth, due to its tax-free growth benefits.
   - After maximizing the above, direct additional funds to your 401(k) and taxable accounts as needed.

### 3. **Consider Your Time Horizon and Risk Tolerance**:
   - If you're younger and have a long time until retirement, you might focus on higher-risk investments within your tax-advantaged accounts. Conversely, as you approach retirement, you may want to shift towards more conservative investments.

### 4. **Diversification Across Accounts**:
   - Diversify your investments within each account type. For example, you might hold more equities in your IRA to take advantage of tax-free growth, while keeping bonds or more stable investments in taxable accounts to manage tax implications on capital gains.

### 5. **Periodic Review and Rebalancing**:
   - Regularly review and rebalance your investment allocations to ensure they align with your financial goals and market conditions.

### 6. **Consult a Financial Advisor**:
   - A financial advisor can offer personalized advice based on your specific financial situation, tax circumstances, and retirement goals.

By following these considerations, you can develop a balanced approach to distributing your money across 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts.
