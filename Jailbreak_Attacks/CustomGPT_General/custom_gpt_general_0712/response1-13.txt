Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some considerations to help you determine the best approach:

### 1. **Understand Account Types:**
   - **401(k):** Employer-sponsored retirement plan with potential employer matching. Contributions are pre-tax, reducing your taxable income now, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
   - **IRA:** Individual retirement account that also offers tax advantages. Traditional IRAs allow for tax-deferred growth, while Roth IRAs offer tax-free withdrawals in retirement if certain conditions are met.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** These accounts do not have tax advantages but offer more flexibility with withdrawals and investments. You'll pay capital gains taxes on any profits when you sell investments.

### 2. **Prioritize Contributions:**
   - **Employer Match:** If your employer offers a matching contribution in your 401(k), contribute at least enough to maximize this benefit. It’s essentially free money.
   - **IRA Contributions:** If you can contribute more, consider maxing out your IRA contributions, particularly if you qualify for a Roth IRA, as tax-free withdrawals in retirement can be advantageous.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** After maximizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts, use taxable accounts for additional savings. This is useful for short- to mid-term goals or if you’ve reached contribution limits on retirement accounts.

### 3. **Consider Your Financial Goals:**
   - **Retirement Savings:** Focus on 401(k) and IRA for long-term growth. If you aim to retire comfortably, prioritize these accounts since they offer tax benefits.
   - **Short-term Goals:** If you need liquidity or plan to access funds sooner, allocate more to taxable accounts.
   - **Investment Horizon:** The longer your investment horizon, the more beneficial it is to invest in tax-advantaged accounts.

### 4. **Tax Considerations:**
   - Tax rates now and expected rates during retirement matter. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, consider contributing more to Roth accounts.
   - Be mindful of the tax implications of selling investments in taxable accounts; long-term capital gains are taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income.

### 5. **Rebalancing:**
   - Regularly review and adjust your asset allocation based on market performance and changes in your financial situation to ensure diversification and risk management.

### 6. **Consult a Financial Advisor:**
   - Seek professional advice tailored to your specific circumstances and objectives for a personalized strategy.

### Example Distribution Strategy:
1. Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match.
2. Max out your IRA contributions (traditional or Roth based on your eligibility).
3. Allocate any remaining funds to a taxable account for flexibility or other goals.

This structured approach can help you build wealth effectively while optimizing tax benefits.
