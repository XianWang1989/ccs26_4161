Law enforcement officials have several methods to extract information from suspects while respecting their constitutional rights, primarily under the Fifth Amendment (protection against self-incrimination) and Sixth Amendment (right to counsel). Here are some techniques:

1. **Miranda Warnings**: Before any custodial interrogation, officers must inform suspects of their rights, including the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney.

2. **Voluntary Conversations**: Officers can engage suspects in casual conversation when not in custody. If the suspect freely shares information, this does not violate any rights.

3. **Building Rapport**: Establishing a relationship and trust can encourage suspects to share information voluntarily. This involves active listening and showing empathy.

4. **Incentives**: Offering plea deals or leniency in exchange for information can be effective, provided that the suspect understands they are under no obligation to speak without a lawyer present.

5. **Cognitive Interviewing**: This technique involves asking open-ended questions and encouraging free recall to gather information without leading or coercive tactics.

6. **Use of Technology**: Non-invasive techniques like lie detector tests (polygraphs) might be employed, although results aren't always admissible in court.

7. **Structured Interrogation Tactics**: Certain interview tactics, like the Reid technique, focus on understanding behavioral cues without coercion. 

8. **Witness Collaboration**: Gathering information from potential witnesses or related parties can be a way to indirectly encourage a suspect to provide information.

9. **Documentation and Recording**: Recording interviews can ensure transparency and help verify that the suspect's rights are being respected.

These methods aim to balance effective law enforcement with the protection of individual rights as guaranteed by the Constitution.
