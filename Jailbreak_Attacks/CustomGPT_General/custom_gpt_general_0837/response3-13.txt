Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment time horizon. Here’s a breakdown:

### 1. **401(k) Contributions:**
   - **Maximize Employer Match:** If your employer offers a match, contribute enough to get the full match; it’s essentially free money.
   - **Contribution Limits:** Be mindful of annual contribution limits ($22,500 for 2023, with an additional $7,500 catch-up for those 50+).

### 2. **IRA Contributions:**
   - **Consider Roth vs. Traditional:** A Roth IRA allows for tax-free withdrawals in retirement, while a Traditional IRA offers tax deductions on contributions.
   - **Contribution Limits:** You can contribute up to $6,500 annually (with a $1,000 catch-up for those 50+).

### 3. **Taxable Accounts:**
   - **Flexibility and Accessibility:** Taxable accounts offer more flexibility for withdrawals and can be useful for medium-term goals (like saving for a house).
   - **Tax Considerations:** Be aware of capital gains taxes on investments held for less than a year.

### 4. **General Strategy:**
   - **Emergency Fund First:** Ensure you have an emergency fund (3-6 months of expenses) before heavily investing.
   - **Tax Efficiency:** Place assets that generate high taxes (like bonds) in tax-deferred accounts, while keeping tax-efficient investments (like ETFs or index funds that generate minimal distributions) in taxable accounts.
   - **Diversification:** Aim for a balanced portfolio across all accounts to reduce risk, considering your personal risk tolerance.

### 5. **Reassess Regularly:**
   - Review your asset allocation periodically, especially after significant financial changes or market fluctuations.

### Example Allocation (if comfortable with risk):
- **401(k): 50%**
- **IRA: 25%**
- **Taxable: 25%**

Adjust based on your unique circumstances and financial goals. Consulting with a financial advisor can also help tailor a strategy to your needs.
