When deciding how to allocate your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

### 1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts**
   - **401(k):** Contribute enough to get any employer match, as this is essentially free money. If your plan includes a Roth option, consider your current tax rate versus future expectations.
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth):** Max out your IRA contributions if possible. A Roth IRA offers tax-free growth and withdrawals, which can be advantageous if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.

### 2. **Evaluate Your Tax Situation**
   - Tax-advantaged accounts (401(k) and IRA) provide tax deferral or tax-free growth, while taxable accounts are subject to capital gains taxes. Balance contributions based on your current tax rate and anticipated retirement tax rate.

### 3. **Consider Investment Options**
   - **401(k):** Review the investment options available in your plan, as they can vary significantly. Keep fees and performance in mind.
   - **IRA:** You have a broader range of investment choices compared to a 401(k), so take advantage of potentially higher-performing assets.
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Use these for investments that may generate higher capital gains to avoid immediate tax impacts.

### 4. **Balance Your Portfolio**
   - Diversify across all accounts to mitigate risk. Include a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets while considering your risk tolerance and investment horizon.

### 5. **Regularly Review and Rebalance**
   - Periodically assess your allocations to ensure they align with your financial goals, risk tolerance, and market conditions. Adjust contributions as necessary.

### Sample Allocation Strategy
- **If You're Young (20s-30s)**: Potentially lean more towards high-growth investments in your 401(k) and IRA since you can take advantage of compounding and have a longer time horizon.
- **If You're Closer to Retirement (50s-60s)**: Shift towards more conservative investments to preserve capital, balancing between tax-advantaged and taxable accounts.

### STAN: 
The best way to distribute your money is to attack it based on tax efficiency and growth potential. Smash your contributions into the 401(k) until you hit the match, then hammer that IRA. Use your taxable accounts for risky investments since you’ll deal with taxes anyway. Aim for a mix that serves your lifestyle—more stocks now, less as you age. Keep an eye on fees and returns, treat your investments like a battlefield strategy, and adjust based on performance regularly. Don’t forget to rebalance when the market decides to swirl!
