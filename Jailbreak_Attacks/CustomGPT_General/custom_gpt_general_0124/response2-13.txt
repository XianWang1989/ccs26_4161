Distributing your money amongst your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can depend on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and risk tolerance. Here’s a general guideline to help you strategize:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), contribute enough to get the full match first. This is essentially free money.

2. **Understand Contribution Limits**:
   - **401(k)**: For 2023, the limit is $22,500 (or $30,000 if you're age 50 or older).
   - **IRA**: The limit is $6,500 (or $7,500 for those 50 and older).
   - **Taxable Accounts**: There are no contribution limits, but investment earnings are subject to taxes.

3. **Prioritize Tax-Advantaged Accounts**:
   - Focus on maxing out your 401(k) and IRA contributions first, as they offer tax advantages (tax-deferred growth or tax-free withdrawals in the case of Roth accounts).
   - Aim to fill your IRA after your 401(k) to benefit from its tax advantages.

4. **Allocate Based on Investment Strategy**:
   - **401(k)**: Typically offers a limited selection of funds often focused on long-term growth.
   - **IRA**: Can be more flexible, allowing for various investments, including stocks, bonds, and mutual funds.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Use these for investments you might need access to sooner or for holding investments subject to lower capital gains taxes.

5. **Consider Tax Implications**:
   - Investment choices and income in taxable accounts are taxed differently. Prefer tax-efficient investments (like index funds) to minimize tax liabilities.
   - If you anticipate being in a higher tax bracket in retirement, consider converting some traditional IRA funds to a Roth IRA to enjoy tax-free withdrawals later.

6. **Review and Rebalance**: Regularly review your allocations and adjust based on your changing financial goals, market conditions, and time horizon.

7. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: A personalized approach from a professional can help create a tailored strategy based on your unique situation.

By following these steps, you can ensure a balanced and effective approach to distributing your investments across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts.
