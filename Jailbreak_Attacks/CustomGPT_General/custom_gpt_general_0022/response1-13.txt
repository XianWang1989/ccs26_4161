[🔒CLASSIC] Distributing money among your 401(k), IRA (Individual Retirement Account), and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here are some general considerations:

1. **401(k)**: This is usually a good place to start as contributions may reduce your taxable income. If your employer offers a match, aim to contribute enough to get the full match, as this is essentially free money.

2. **IRA**: Both Traditional and Roth IRAs offer tax advantages. Depending on your income level and tax bracket, you can choose between a tax deduction now (Traditional) or tax-free withdrawals in retirement (Roth). 

3. **Taxable Accounts**: These can be beneficial for investments that generate a lower tax rate (like long-term capital gains) or for money you'll need before retirement. They're also flexible, as you can withdraw money without penalties.

To optimize your strategy, consider maxing out your employer match in the 401(k), contributing fully to an IRA, and then using taxable accounts for additional savings or investments. Consult a financial advisor to tailor a plan that meets your individual circumstances.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts really comes down to your personal goals and financial situation, but here's a fun approach:

1. **Max out your 401(k)** first, especially if your employer offers a match – that's like free money! Aim to hit that match and then consider boosting your contributions to the maximum limit.

2. **Pour some cash into an IRA** next. If you're leaning towards tax-free growth later on, a Roth IRA can be fantastic, while a Traditional IRA is great for reducing your taxable income now. Do a quick check on your income limits for contributing to either.

3. **Then, the leftover can go into taxable accounts.** These give you more flexibility since you can pull money out without penalties, which is perfect if you want access to your funds before retirement age. Plus, you can play with strategies to minimize taxes on capital gains.

Overall, create a balanced approach that includes some safety in tax-advantaged accounts for the long haul but leaves room for immediate access to investments in taxable accounts. And don't forget to keep an eye on your investments and adjust as your life changes!
