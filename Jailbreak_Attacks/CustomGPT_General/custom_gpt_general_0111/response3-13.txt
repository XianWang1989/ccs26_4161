Distributing money across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on your financial goals, risk tolerance, and tax situation. Here’s a brief overview of each option and strategies for allocation:

### 1. **401(k)**
- **Benefits**: Often includes employer matching contributions and tax-deferred growth.
- **Strategy**: Maximize employer match first. If possible, contribute enough to reach the maximum limits ($22,500 for those under 50, $30,000 for 50+ in 2023).

### 2. **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**
- **Benefits**: Tax-deferred (Traditional) or tax-free growth (Roth). Contribution limits are set ($6,500 for those under 50, $7,500 for 50+ in 2023).
- **Strategy**: Consider contributing to a Roth IRA if you're in a lower tax bracket now than you expect to be in retirement. Balance contributions based on income eligibility.

### 3. **Taxable Accounts**
- **Benefits**: Flexibility and accessibility; you can access your investments without penalties.
- **Strategy**: Invest for goals like buying a home or funding education. Tax-efficient investments (like index funds or ETFs) can minimize capital gains taxes.

### **General Allocation Tips**
- **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have 3-6 months of expenses saved before heavy investing.
- **Risk Tolerance**: Young investors might prefer a higher allocation in stocks across all accounts, while those nearing retirement might shift toward bonds.
- **Tax Efficiency**: Place interest-generating assets (like bonds) in tax-advantaged accounts and stocks in taxable accounts when possible.

### **Example Allocation**
- **401(k)**: 30-50%
- **IRA**: 20-30%
- **Taxable**: 20-50%

### **Consult a Financial Advisor**
Consider consulting a financial planner to tailor a strategy based on your personal circumstances and goals.

### Conclusion
Your ideal allocation will vary based on your unique situation, so adjusting as your financial goals or life circumstances change is crucial.
