Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts involves several considerations, including your financial goals, tax implications, and age. Here's an approach to help you evaluate the best allocation:

1. **Understand the Accounts**:
   - **401(k)**: Employer-sponsored retirement plan with potential matching contributions. Contributions are typically pre-tax, reducing your taxable income. There are also Roth 401(k) options for after-tax contributions.
   - **IRA**: Individual Retirement Account with either traditional (pre-tax) or Roth (after-tax) options. Contribution limits apply, and there are tax benefits depending on your income and tax filing status.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: No tax advantages, but you have more flexibility regarding withdrawals. Investment gains are taxed as capital gains, which can be lower than ordinary income tax rates if held long-term.

2. **Establish Your Goals**: 
   - Determine your short-term and long-term financial goals (retirement age, buying a home, etc.).
   - Decide how much liquidity you need now versus later.

3. **Consider Tax Implications**:
   - Maximize contributions to tax-advantaged accounts first. This often means prioritizing your 401(k) up to the employer match and then contributing to an IRA.
   - If eligible, consider maxing out Roth IRAs for long-term tax-free growth, especially if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.

4. **Account Contribution Order**:
   - Start with your **401(k)** to get the full employer match.
   - Next, contribute to your **IRA** (traditional or Roth), balancing contributions based on your tax situation.
   - After maxing out tax-advantaged accounts, contribute additional funds to your **taxable account** for flexibility and additional investment opportunities.

5. **Asset Allocation**:
   - Diversify your investments within each account type. For retirement accounts, consider a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets that align with your risk tolerance and time horizon.
   - In taxable accounts, focus on investments that have favorable tax treatment, such as index funds or holding investments for over a year to benefit from long-term capital gains tax rates.

6. **Review and Adjust**:
   - Regularly review your allocations based on changes in income, expenses, market conditions, and retirement goals.
   - Adjust contributions and investment strategies as needed.

Ultimately, consulting with a financial advisor can provide personalized advice tailored to your unique financial situation.
