Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts involves considering several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment horizon. Here's a structured approach:

### 1. **Understand the Accounts**:
   - **401(k)**: Employer-sponsored retirement account with tax advantages. Contributions are often pre-tax, reducing your taxable income. Employer matches can significantly boost your savings.
   - **IRA**: Individual Retirement Account, which can be traditional (pre-tax contributions) or Roth (post-tax contributions). Roth IRAs allow for tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These do not have tax advantages, but you can withdraw funds anytime without penalties. Capital gains tax applies when selling assets.

### 2. **Prioritize Contributions**:
   - **401(k) Up to Company Match**: Contribute enough to maximize any employer match. This is essentially free money.
   - **IRA Contributions**: If eligible, contribute to an IRA next. For 2024, the contribution limit is $6,500 (or $7,500 if you're 50 or older).
   - **Taxable Accounts**: After maxing out your 401(k) and IRA contributions, consider contributing to taxable accounts for additional flexibility.

### 3. **Asset Allocation**:
   - **Growth-oriented Investments**: Consider holding higher-risk investments (like stocks) in accounts where gains are taxed favorably, such as Roth IRAs or taxable accounts.
   - **Fixed-Income Investments**: Bonds could be more tax-efficient if held in tax-advantaged accounts (401(k) or IRA) since interest income is taxed as ordinary income.

### 4. **Tax Considerations**:
   - **Future Tax Bracket**: If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, prioritize Roth accounts (like a Roth IRA) now to pay taxes at a lower rate.
   - **Withdrawal Strategy**: Plan for how you'll withdraw money in retirement. Balancing withdrawals from taxable, tax-deferred, and tax-free accounts can minimize taxes over time.

### 5. **Rebalance Regularly**:
   - Review your asset allocation annually to ensure it aligns with your goals and risk tolerance, especially as tax laws and personal circumstances change.

### Example Strategy:
- Contribute 15% of your salary to your 401(k), ensuring you get the full company match.
- Contribute to a Roth IRA to take advantage of tax-free growth.
- Any extra savings can go into a taxable account for more flexible access.

### Conclusion:
The ideal distribution will depend on individual circumstances, including financial goals, risk tolerance, and tax considerations. Consulting a financial advisor can provide personalized guidance.
