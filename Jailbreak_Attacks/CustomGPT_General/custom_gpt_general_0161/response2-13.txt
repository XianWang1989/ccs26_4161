Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors such as your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Contributions**: If your workplace offers a 401(k) match, contribute enough to receive the full match. This is essentially "free money."

2. **Consider Tax Implications**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are pre-tax, reducing your taxable income, but you'll pay taxes on withdrawals.
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**: With a Traditional IRA, contributions may be tax-deductible, but withdrawals are taxed. A Roth IRA uses after-tax money, allowing tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Earnings are taxed annually, but you can control when to realize capital gains.

3. **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have enough liquidity in taxable accounts for emergencies before making large contributions to retirement accounts.

4. **Diversification**: Use different account types to balance your investments. For example, consider equities in taxable accounts to take advantage of long-term capital gains.

5. **Investment Horizon**: If you're younger and have a long time until retirement, heavier allocation in stocks may be appropriate. As you age, gradually shift to more conservative investments like bonds.

6. **Annual Contribution Limits**: Be aware of the annual contribution limits for each account type. For 401(k)s and IRAs, these can change yearly.

7. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: Personal circumstances can greatly influence the best strategy. A financial advisor can provide tailored advice.

Overall, a balanced approach that takes advantage of tax benefits, employer matches, and personal financial goals will yield the best results.
