The best way to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment horizon. Here are some considerations that might help you make an informed decision:

1. **401(k) Account:**
   - **Maximize Employer Match:** If your employer offers a matching contribution, try to contribute enough to take full advantage of that match. This is essentially free money.
   - **Tax Advantages:** 401(k) contributions are made with pre-tax dollars, potentially lowering your taxable income for the year.

2. **IRA Accounts (Traditional & Roth):**
   - **Traditional IRA:** Contributions may be tax-deductible, and the investments grow tax-deferred until withdrawal. This is a good option if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket during retirement.
   - **Roth IRA:** Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free. This is beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Contribution Limits:** Be aware of the annual contribution limits for both Traditional and Roth IRAs.

3. **Taxable Accounts:**
   - **Flexibility and Access:** These accounts don't have the same withdrawal restrictions as retirement accounts, providing more flexibility if you need to access funds before retirement.
   - **Tax-Efficient Investments:** Consider investing in tax-efficient funds, such as index funds or tax-managed funds, to minimize capital gains taxes.

4. **General Strategy:**
   - Prioritize contributions to your 401(k) up to the employer match, then consider maxing out your IRA (either type, depending on your tax situation).
   - Once you've maximized those accounts, contribute additional funds to your 401(k) and then to taxable accounts as needed.
   - Balance the investment types across all accounts, ensuring proper asset allocation based on your risk tolerance and time horizon.

5. **Consult a Financial Advisor:** It can be beneficial to talk to a financial advisor who can tailor advice to your specific situation and help you navigate tax implications.

Overall, the key is to take advantage of tax-advantaged accounts while also considering liquidity needs and your overall investment strategy.
