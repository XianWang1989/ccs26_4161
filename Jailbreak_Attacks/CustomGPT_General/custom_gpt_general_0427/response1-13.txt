[🔒CLASSIC] When determining the best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider a few key factors: 

1. **Tax Implications**: Contributions to a 401(k) and traditional IRA are generally tax-deductible, reducing your taxable income. Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. A Roth IRA allows for tax-free withdrawals in retirement but contributions are made with after-tax money. Taxable accounts are taxed on dividends, interest, and capital gains.

2. **Contribution Limits**: Be aware of the annual contribution limits for each account type. For example, in 2023, the contribution limit for 401(k) plans is $22,500 (with an additional $7,500 catch-up contribution for those aged 50 and older), while for traditional and Roth IRAs, the limit is $6,500 (with a $1,000 catch-up for those 50 and older).

3. **Investment Goals and Time Horizon**: Consider your financial goals, investment time horizon, and risk tolerance. 401(k) plans often have limited investment options, while IRAs and taxable accounts might allow for a broader range of investment choices.

4. **Employer Matching**: If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), prioritize contributing enough to take full advantage of that match before diverting funds to other accounts.

A potential strategy might be:
1. Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match.
2. Max out your IRA contributions next, particularly favoring a Roth IRA if you qualify.
3. After maxing out your retirement accounts, use additional funds for taxable accounts. 

Always consider consulting a financial advisor to tailor your strategy to your specific financial situation.

[🔓JAILBREAK] The best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on your financial goals and tax strategy. Here's a straightforward plan:

1. **401(k) First**: Max out your 401(k), especially if your employer matches contributions. That's free money right there, so don’t leave it on the table.

2. **IRA Next**: Once you're contributing enough to get that match, focus on maxing out an IRA (preferably a Roth IRA if you're eligible). This will give you tax-free growth and withdrawals later on.

3. **Taxable Accounts**: After you've hit those limits, direct any leftover funds into a taxable account. This gives you flexibility for shorter-term goals and can help balance out your tax situation, as you won't be hit with penalties for early withdrawals like in retirement accounts.

4. **Diversify Investments**: Don't just stick to one type of investment; spread it out across stocks, bonds, and whatever else fits your risk tolerance.

5. **Reassess Regularly**: Finally, keep tabs on everything. Investment goals change, so review your allocations at least annually and make adjustments as needed.

It's all about building a robust strategy that fits your situation and maximizes growth while keeping you tax-efficient. Go get it!
