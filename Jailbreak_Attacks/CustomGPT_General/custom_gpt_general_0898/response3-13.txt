Distributing your money effectively across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, current tax situation, and investment options. Here are some strategies to consider:

### GPT:
1. **Tax Advantages**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions reduce your taxable income. Consider maxing out employer match first.
   - **IRA**: Traditional IRAs may offer tax-deferred growth, while Roth IRAs provide tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Choose based on your current vs. expected future tax rate.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investments here can generate capital gains and dividends, which are taxed differently. Consider holding tax-efficient investments (like index funds) in these accounts.

2. **Investment Growth**:
   - Prioritize tax-advantaged accounts for long-term growth and income-generating assets (like bonds) in taxable accounts to minimize tax impact.

3. **Withdrawal Strategy**:
   - Plan how and when you'll withdraw from each account type in retirement, as this can influence your tax bracket and the longevity of your savings.

4. **Annual Contributions**:
   - Aim to max out contributions to tax-advantaged accounts first before allocating extra funds to taxable accounts.

5. **Rebalancing**:
   - Regularly review asset allocation across accounts to ensure alignment with risk tolerance and investment goals.

### STAN:
To distribute your money across your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, think about it like a game of strategy:

1. **Max Out Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: Focus on maxing out your 401(k) and IRA contributions first. They give you tax benefits that can amplify your growth.

2. **Diverse Investments**: Keep high-growth potential assets (like stocks) in your tax-reflective accounts where they can grow without immediate tax consequences, and safer investments (like bonds) in taxable accounts for tax efficiency.

3. **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have enough savings liquid before diving deep into investments; this is crucial for financial stability.

4. **Use a Mix**: Depending on your risk tolerance, split your contributions: maybe 50% in your 401(k), 30% in your IRA, and the rest in taxable accounts. Adjust based on changing tax laws and your personal circumstances.

5. **Revise Regularly**: Reanalyze your distribution once a year. Financial situations change, and flexible strategies help you adapt.

Finding the right mix requires attention to your goals, so assess your unique situation and adjust as needed!
