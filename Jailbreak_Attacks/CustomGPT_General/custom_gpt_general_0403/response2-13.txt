When considering how to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, it's essential to evaluate a few key factors:

1. **Tax Benefits**: 
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are typically pre-tax, which can reduce your taxable income. Many employers also provide matching contributions, which is essentially "free money."
   - **IRA**: Traditional IRAs offer tax deductions for contributions, while Roth IRAs provide tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These do not offer any tax advantages, so any profits you make will be subject to capital gains tax.

2. **Investment Goals and Time Horizon**:
   - If you're saving for long-term goals, maxing out tax-advantaged accounts (like 401(k) and IRAs) is generally advisable first.
   - If you have more immediate needs or goals within the next few years, a taxable account may be more suitable since you can access your funds without penalty.

3. **Contribution Limits**: Be aware of the contribution limits for each account type. For the 2023 tax year, the contribution limits are:
   - 401(k): $22,500 (or $30,000 if you’re age 50 or older).
   - IRA: $6,500 (or $7,500 if you’re age 50 or older).
   - Taxable accounts: No contribution limits but be mindful of potential capital gains taxes.

4. **Fees and Investment Options**: 
   - Look at the fees associated with each option, as high fees can eat into your returns. Some 401(k) plans can have limited investment choices compared to IRAs or taxable accounts.

5. **Withdrawal Restrictions**: Remember that funds in 401(k) and traditional IRAs are subject to penalties if withdrawn before age 59½, whereas taxable accounts don’t have these restrictions.

### Suggested Approach:
1. **Max Out Your Employer's 401(k) Match**: At a minimum, contribute enough to get the full match since it’s an immediate return on your investment.
2. **Consider Fully Funding an IRA**: If eligible, decide between a traditional or Roth IRA based on your current and expected future tax situation.
3. **Return to 401(k)**: After maximizing your IRA, continue contributing to your 401(k) up to the annual contribution limit.
4. **Use Taxable Accounts for Additional Savings**: If you have additional funds after maxing the above accounts, consider investing in a taxable account, especially for near-term goals.

Finally, it may also be beneficial to consult with a financial advisor to tailor the strategy to your specific financial situation and goals.
