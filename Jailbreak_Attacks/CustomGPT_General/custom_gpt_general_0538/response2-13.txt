To effectively distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

### 1. Understand Account Types:
- **401(k)**: Often offers employer matching contributions and tax-deferred growth.
- **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**: Allows for tax-advantaged growth; Roth IRAs are taxed on contributions but grow tax-free, while Traditional IRAs provide tax deductions on contributions.
- **Taxable Accounts**: No tax advantages, but you have more flexibility in terms of withdrawals and investment choices.

### 2. Prioritize Contributions:
- **Maximize Employer Match**: Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get any employer matching. This is essentially free money.
- **IRA Contributions**: If you qualify, consider contributing to a Roth IRA for tax-free growth or a Traditional IRA for immediate tax benefits.
- **Taxable Accounts**: Use these for additional savings once you maximize tax-advantaged accounts.

### 3. Consider Tax Implications:
- **Tax Efficiency**: Place tax-efficient investments (like index funds) in taxable accounts, while holding less tax-efficient options (like bonds) in tax-advantaged accounts.
- **Withdrawal Strategy**: Plan how you’ll withdraw funds in retirement to minimize taxes. Typically, you'll want to withdraw from taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred accounts, and finally tax-free accounts.

### 4. Diversification:
- Ensure that your overall portfolio is diversified across all accounts. Different accounts will have varying investment options, so spread your risk accordingly.

### 5. Adjust Over Time:
- Review and adjust your allocation annually based on changes in tax laws, your financial goals, and your investment performance.

### Conclusion:
A balanced approach that takes advantage of tax benefits while considering your financial goals will help you effectively distribute your money among these accounts. Regular reviews will ensure you stay on track as your situation changes. 

Always consider consulting a financial advisor for personalized advice that considers your individual financial situation.

This general framework is a starting point and should be tailored to your unique financial circumstances.
