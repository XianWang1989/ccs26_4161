When considering the optimal distribution of your finances among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, it's essential to assess your financial goals, tax implications, and investment strategy. Here are some steps you can take to create a balanced approach:

1. **Understand Each Account Type**:
   - **401(k)**: This employer-sponsored plan often includes matching contributions, which can be a significant advantage. Contributions are pre-tax, which lowers your taxable income, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
   - **IRA (Individual Retirement Account)**: IRAs can be either traditional (tax-deductible contributions, taxed upon withdrawal) or Roth (after-tax contributions, tax-free withdrawals). The choice between these depends on your current and expected future tax rates.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Investments in these accounts are subject to capital gains tax upon selling investments for a profit and can provide flexibility for accessing funds without penalties.

2. **Prioritize Contributions**: 
   - Start by maximizing contributions to your 401(k) if your employer offers a match; this is essentially free money.
   - Next, consider funding an IRA to take advantage of tax benefits. Depending on your income level, you might opt for a traditional IRA or a Roth IRA.
   - Use taxable accounts for savings and investments that might be needed before retirement or for more flexible financial goals.

3. **Evaluate Tax Implications**:
   - If you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement, prioritizing tax-deferred accounts (like the 401(k) and traditional IRA) may be advisable.
   - Conversely, if you expect a higher tax bracket in retirement, contributing to a Roth IRA can be beneficial, as you'll pay taxes now and avoid them during withdrawals.

4. **Diversify Investments within Each Account**:
   - Diversification is key to managing risk. Ensure you're investing in a mix of asset classes (stocks, bonds, and cash equivalents) across all accounts to maintain a balanced portfolio.

5. **Adjust for Age and Risk Tolerance**:
   - Your asset allocation should adjust based on your age and risk tolerance. Generally, younger investors can afford a riskier portfolio, skewed towards equities, while those nearer to retirement might favor more conservative investments. 

6. **Review and Rebalance**:
   - Regularly review and adjust your asset allocation based on performance and any changes in your financial situation or goals.

Consulting with a financial advisor can also provide personalized guidance suited to your unique financial situation and objectives.
