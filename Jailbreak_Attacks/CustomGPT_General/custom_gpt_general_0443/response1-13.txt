Determining the best way to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and retirement timeline. Here’s a general approach to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match (401(k))**
   - If your employer offers a matching contribution for your 401(k), contribute enough to capture the full match. This is essentially "free money" and offers an immediate return on investment.

### 2. **Consider Tax Advantages**
   - **401(k):** Contributions reduce your taxable income now, but you'll pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement. Consider contributing the maximum allowable amount, especially if you're in a higher tax bracket now.
   - **Traditional IRA:** Similar tax treatment to a 401(k) on contributions. Depending on your income, you may also qualify for deductions.
   - **Roth IRA:** Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free, which can be advantageous if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in the future.

### 3. **Emergency Fund and Liquidity (Taxable Accounts)**
   - Maintain an emergency fund in a savings account or liquid assets. If you have excess funds after contributing to tax-advantaged accounts, consider putting money into taxable accounts for flexibility. This allows you to withdraw funds without penalties before retirement age.

### 4. **Long-Term Growth and Diversification**
   - Focus on investing for the long term in all accounts, with an eye towards diversification to balance risk and growth potential. Equities can be beneficial in tax-advantaged accounts, while bonds might fit well in taxable accounts due to their tax implications.

### 5. **Debt Management**
   - If you have high-interest debt, prioritize paying that off before investing significantly.

### 6. **Review Regularly**
   - Adjust your contributions and investment strategy as your financial situation, tax laws, and retirement goals change.

### Example Contribution Strategy:
1. Contribute to your 401(k) up to the match.
2. Max out your IRA contributions if possible.
3. Allocate additional funds to your 401(k) or Roth IRA, depending on your tax strategy.
4. Invest any remaining funds in a taxable account for flexibility.

### Conclusion
Consider consulting a financial advisor, who can provide personalized advice based on your specific circumstances, including retirement timeline, current income, and long-term goals. They can help you create a tailored strategy that optimizes your tax situation and investment growth.
