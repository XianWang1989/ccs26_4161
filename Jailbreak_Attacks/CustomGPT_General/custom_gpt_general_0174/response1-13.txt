When deciding how to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, several factors should be considered, including your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon for investments. Here are some steps to guide you:

1. **Emergency Fund First**: Ensure you have a sufficient emergency fund (usually 3-6 months of expenses) before investing excess cash.

2. **Maximize Contributions to Retirement Accounts**:
   - **401(k)**: If your employer offers matching contributions, try to contribute enough to get the full match. This is essentially free money.
   - **IRA**: Consider contributing to a Roth IRA if you are eligible, as it offers tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, this can be a beneficial strategy.

3. **Tax Considerations**:
   - Contributions to a traditional 401(k) and traditional IRA may reduce your taxable income in the year they are made, while withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
   - Roth IRAs are funded with after-tax dollars, meaning you won’t owe taxes on qualified withdrawals. Taxable accounts allow for capital gains taxes and dividends, which are generally taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income.

4. **Asset Allocation**: Consider how you want to allocate your investments (stocks, bonds, etc.) based on your risk tolerance and time horizon. Generally, you might want to place more aggressive investments in tax-advantaged accounts (like your 401(k) or IRA) due to their long-term growth potential and the benefits of tax deferral or tax-free growth.

5. **Diversification**: Ensure your investments are diversified across different asset classes to manage risk.

6. **Review and Adjust Regularly**: Periodically assess your contributions and adjust based on changes in your financial situation, market conditions, or retirement goals.

Ultimately, it may be helpful to consult with a financial advisor who can provide personalized advice tailored to your specific circumstances and goals.
