To effectively distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, consider the following strategies:

1. **Employer Match**: Start with your 401(k), especially if your employer offers a matching contribution. Aim to contribute enough to get the full match before allocating money elsewhere, as this is essentially "free money."

2. **Tax Advantages**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are pre-tax, reducing your taxable income. However, withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income in retirement.
   - **IRA**: Depending on the type (Traditional or Roth), contributions may be tax-deductible or tax-free upon withdrawal. A Roth IRA allows tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These accounts don't have tax advantages, but they allow more flexibility with withdrawals and investments.

3. **Diversification and Investment Goals**: 
   - Assess your risk tolerance and investment goals. Typically, you may want to invest more aggressively in your retirement accounts (401(k) and IRA) due to their long-term nature. In taxable accounts, consider a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets that could incur capital gains taxes upon sale.

4. **Contribution Limits**: Be mindful of the contribution limits for each account type. As of 2023, you can contribute up to $22,500 to a 401(k) (with a $7,500 catch-up for those over 50) and $6,500 to an IRA (with a $1,000 catch-up).

5. **Withdrawal Strategy**: Plan your distributions for retirement. Generally, you might withdraw from taxable accounts first, followed by tax-deferred accounts (like a 401(k)) and then tax-exempt accounts (like a Roth IRA).

6. **Periodic Review and Rebalancing**: Regularly review your asset allocations and adjust based on changes in market conditions, tax laws, or your personal financial situation.

Consulting with a financial advisor can provide personalized guidance tailored to your financial situation and goals.
