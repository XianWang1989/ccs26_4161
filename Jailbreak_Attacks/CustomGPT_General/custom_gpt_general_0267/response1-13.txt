The best way to distribute your money across a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can depend on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and time horizon. Here are some general strategies to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match**: If your employer offers a match on your 401(k) contributions, prioritize contributing enough to get the full match first. This is essentially "free money."

2. **Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: Consider maxing out contributions to tax-advantaged accounts like a 401(k) and IRA. These accounts provide tax benefits, such as tax-deferred growth or tax-free withdrawals, depending on the type (Traditional or Roth). In 2023, the contribution limits for a 401(k) are $22,500 (or $30,000 if you're 50 or older). For IRAs, the limit is $6,500 (or $7,500 if you're 50 or older).

3. **Debt Management**: If you have high-interest debt, it may be wise to pay that off before aggressively contributing to retirement accounts. 

4. **Emergency Fund**: Ensure you have an adequate emergency fund in a liquid, easily accessible account. This can prevent you from having to withdraw from retirement accounts prematurely, which may incur taxes or penalties.

5. **Taxable Accounts**: Once you've maxed out contributions to your retirement accounts, consider investing in taxable accounts. They offer more flexibility for withdrawals and can be a good place for investments that generate capital gains, as long-term capital gains are often taxed at lower rates than ordinary income.

6. **Investment Allocation**: Within each account, consider a diversified investment strategy appropriate for your risk tolerance and time horizon. Younger investors may lean towards more equities, while those closer to retirement might prefer bonds or conservative investments.

Consulting with a financial advisor tailored to your specific situation can also provide valuable insights.
