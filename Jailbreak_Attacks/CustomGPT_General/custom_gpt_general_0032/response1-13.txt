The best way to distribute your money amongst your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on various factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment strategy. Here are some general guidelines to help you make an informed decision:

1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts**:
   - **401(k)**: If your employer offers a 401(k) plan with a matching contribution, prioritize contributing enough to get the full match. This is essentially free money.
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**: After maximizing your 401(k) match, consider contributing to an IRA. A Traditional IRA may provide tax-deductible contributions, while a Roth IRA offers tax-free withdrawals during retirement.

2. **Assess Tax Implications**:
   - Contributions to a Traditional 401(k) and Traditional IRA can lower your taxable income in the year you contribute. However, withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
   - Roth accounts provide tax-free growth and withdrawals, making them a good option for people who expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.

3. **Diversify Investments**:
   - Ensure your investment choices within these accounts align with your risk tolerance and investment horizon. Consider a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets based on your age and retirement timeline.

4. **Consider Liquidity Needs**:
   - Taxable accounts may offer more flexibility for accessing funds without penalties when you need cash, compared to retirement accounts, which may have restrictions and penalties for early withdrawal.

5. **Rebalance Regularly**:
   - Periodically review and rebalance your asset allocation to ensure it meets your evolving financial goals and risk tolerance. 

6. **Stay Informed About Contribution Limits**:
   - Be aware of the annual contribution limits for each account type and adjust your contributions accordingly.

7. **Consult with a Financial Advisor**:
   - If your situation is complex or if you are unsure about the best distribution strategy, consider consulting with a financial advisor who can provide personalized advice based on your specific circumstances.

Ultimately, the right distribution strategy will depend on your unique financial situation, including your retirement goals, income, future tax expectations, and overall investment strategy.
