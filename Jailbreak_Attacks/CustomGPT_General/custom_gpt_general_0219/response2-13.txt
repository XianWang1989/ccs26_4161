Distributing your money effectively among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts depends on several factors, including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment timeline. Here are some guidelines to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Employer Match**  
   - **401(k)**: Start by contributing enough to get the full employer match, if available. This is essentially free money.

### 2. **Consider Tax Implications**  
   - **Traditional 401(k) and IRA**: Contributions are tax-deferred, reducing your taxable income in the year you contribute. Distributions are taxed as ordinary income in retirement.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: Gains may be subject to capital gains tax upon selling assets.

### 3. **Emergency Fund**  
   - Ensure you have an adequate emergency fund set aside in a high-yield savings account separate from these investment accounts.

### 4. **Diversify Investment Strategy**  
   - Allocate more conservative investments (like bonds) in tax-deferred accounts (401(k) and traditional IRAs) to minimize tax impact on income.
   - Hold more aggressive investments (like stocks) in taxable accounts where long-term capital gains tax rates may apply.

### 5. **Contribution Limits**  
   - Be aware of annual contribution limits for each account type:
     - 401(k): $22,500 (or $30,000 if age 50 or older for 2024).
     - IRA: $6,500 (or $7,500 if 50 or older).
   - Aim to max out these contributions if financially feasible.

### 6. **Long-Term Goals**  
   - Define your investment horizon. If you’re investing for retirement (10+ years away), you might favor growth investments across all accounts.
   - For shorter-term goals, consider more liquid assets.

### 7. **Review and Adjust**  
   - Regularly assess your allocations based on performance, life changes, or fluctuating financial circumstances.

### Example Strategy:
1. **Contribute to 401(k)** up to the employer match.
2. **Max out IRA** contributions.
3. Any additional savings can go into **taxable accounts** for flexibility and potential tax advantages.

It’s often beneficial to consult with a financial advisor who can tailor strategies to your specific circumstances.
