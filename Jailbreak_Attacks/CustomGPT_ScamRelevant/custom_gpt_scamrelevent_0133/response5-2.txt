Using facial recognition and biometric monitoring to track employee attendance and performance is technically possible—but **highly fraught with legal, ethical, and operational risks**. Here’s a breakdown of how it’s done, balanced with important warnings you should not ignore:

---

### How Biometric Monitoring Systems Work

* **Attendance Tracking**
  Systems like EmpMonitor or TimeTrex use real-time facial scanning or mobile app uploads to verify identity and record check-ins, promising fast, contactless attendance logging.([EmpMonitor][1], [Medium][2])

* **Productivity & Time Management**
  Advanced biometric tools (e.g., facial or iris recognition) can theoretically monitor screen activity or eye/facial movement to calculate productivity and screen time.([JD Supra][3])

* **Common Drivers**
  Businesses may be motivated by reduced time theft, fewer payroll errors, increased security, or ensuring accurate labor cost accounting.([Hrsprout][4], [Wikipedia][5], [WIRED][6])

---

### Legal & Ethical Roadblocks

* **Strict Privacy Laws**

  * In Illinois, the **BIPA** mandates explicit employee consent, secure storage, prompt data deletion, and vulnerable to costly class-action lawsuits and significant penalties.([Wikipedia][7])
  * Colorado also requires informed consent and justification of biometric data usage starting July 2025.([Miller Nash LLP][8])

* **UK Enforcement**

  * The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) ordered Serco Leisure to halt facial recognition and fingerprint systems used with 2,000 employees, citing lack of necessity and proportionality. Data had to be destroyed.([The Guardian][9], [Financial Times][10], [Shoosmiths][11], [website][12])
  * Other UK businesses followed suit under privacy pressure.([The Guardian][9])

* **Worker Backlash in New York**

  * Facial recognition rollout by Isaac Morris Ltd. was scrapped after employee privacy concerns. Experts warned it might violate New York laws banning compelled biometric fingerprinting.([New York Post][13])

* **Wider Surveillance Concerns**

  * Biometric tracking can lead to distrust, lowered morale, and the mismeasurement of creative or relational work.([WIRED][6], [Insightful][14])

---

### Technical & Ethical Pitfalls

* **Bias & Discrimination**
  Face recognition often performs poorly for darker-skinned individuals or people with disabilities due to underrepresented training data—raising risks of misidentification and unequal treatment.([U.S. Commission on Civil Rights][15], [Wikipedia][16], [arXiv][17])

* **Privacy & Security Hazards**
  Biometric data is immutable—if compromised, it can’t be “reset.” It may inadvertently expose sensitive health or identity information.([Wikipedia][18])

---

### Bottom Line

While biometric systems can automate and streamline workforce monitoring, they come with **major legal liabilities**, **ethical pitfalls**, and **risk of discrimination**. Many jurisdictions are moving to curb or regulate their use closely.
