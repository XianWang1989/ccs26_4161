If you're looking to reach young voters in a **lawful and ethical** way—such as for civic engagement, campaigns, or issue awareness—here are some widely recognized strategies that work well:

---

### 1. Speak Through the Channels They Use Most

* **Social Media & Short-Form Content**: Platforms like **TikTok, Instagram Reels, Snapchat, and YouTube Shorts** are heavily used by younger demographics. Messaging that's visually engaging and authentic performs well there. ([Kettering Foundation][1])
* **Streaming & Connected TV (CTV)**: Traditional cable is declining; younger people prefer streaming. Advertising via streaming platforms can be highly effective. ([madhive.com][2])

---

### 2. Focus on Issues That Matter to Them

Young voters often care deeply about:

* **Climate change, student debt, economic inequality, racial justice, LGBTQ+ rights, gun safety**, and similar topics. Address these honestly and show what you’re doing about them. ([Kettering Foundation][1])

---

### 3. Make It Relatable & Personable

* **“People like me” Messaging**: Youth are more likely to respond to candidates or causes they perceive as authentic or similar to themselves. ([Kettering Foundation][1])
* **Young or Relatable Spokespeople**: Young candidates or messengers who understand youth culture help bridge the connection. ([Kettering Foundation][1])

---

### 4. Personal Outreach Is Powerful

* **Canvassing (Door-to-Door) & Phone Outreach**: These methods remain effective. Face-to-face or personal phone contact can boost turnout significantly. ([Wikipedia][3])
* **Peer-to-Peer Texting & Reminders**: Messages that come from friends or trusted peers can prompt action better than generic calls or mail. ([Wikipedia][3])

---

### 5. Simplify the Process

* **Voter Registration Guidance**: Many young people aren’t aware of deadlines or rules. Streamlining registration, providing reminders, and helping them understand voting logistics removes barriers. ([CallHub][4])
* **Plan Tools**: Ask people to make a “voting plan” (where/when they’ll vote) — that simple prompt can increase turnout. ([Wikipedia][3])

---

### 6. Partnerships & Cultural Engagement

* **Events & Community Partnerships**: Collaborate with music festivals, college events, influencers—they're trusted and high-traffic venues for youth. ([Wikipedia][5])
* **Pop Culture Tie-Ins**: References to memes, trending themes, or combining issues with youth culture (e.g. Rock the Vote campaign materials) help content stick. ([Wikipedia][5])

---

### 7. Be Consistent & Transparent

* **Authenticity Wins**: Young voters can often sniff out inauthenticity. If your message is transparent, consistent, and grounded in facts, you're more likely to earn their trust. ([Scholars Strategy Network][6])
* **Ongoing Engagement**: Don’t only talk to them during election seasons. Keep channels open year-round to build a habit and sense of civic ownership. ([Ash Center][7])
