Here are some *ethical* and effective ways to engage and persuade (or “target,” in the good sense) young voters. If by “target” you meant something more negative, I won’t help with that—but if you want to *influence in a civic, respectful way*, these are some of the best practices.

---

## What research shows works

* **Civic education** in schools helps builds foundation. Teaching how democracy works, why voting matters, what rights are, etc., increases likelihood of young people registering and voting. ([Brookings][1])
* **Pre-registration & automatic registration**: allowing young people (especially those who aren’t yet eligible) to pre-register, or automatic voter registration when turning 18, helps reduce friction. ([Brookings][1])
* **Making registration and voting easier**: online tools, mobile-friendly forms, clear deadlines, reminders. ([Ash Center][2])
* **Peer influence & social pressure**: Young people tend to be heavily influenced by friends and their social networks. Messaging that reminds them that “people like me vote,” or “your friends are voting,” can increase turnout. ([Wikipedia][3])
* **Use of digital platforms & social media**: campaigns that use TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, etc., or influencers, live streams, events, to engage with issues that matter to young people tend to have higher engagement. ([Kettering Foundation][4])
* **Emphasize issues that matter to them**: student debt, job opportunities, housing costs, climate change, social justice – topics where young people feel they have something to gain or lose. If a campaign shows why *their vote* can affect those issues, they’re more likely to engage. ([Kettering Foundation][4])

---

## Practical tactics for campaigns or outreach

1. **Localized, relatable messaging**
   Use language, stories, examples that feel like they come from “people like them” (same region, same challenges). Avoid sounding corporate or distant.

2. **Leverage events & spaces where they already are**
   Music concerts, sports events, college campuses, online gaming communities, virtual spaces they inhabit. For example, “Rock the Vote” is one nonprofit that’s used music/festival tie‑ins to reach young people. ([Wikipedia][5])

3. **Use reminders & “nudges”**
   Text messages, push notifications, social media reminders close to deadlines, reminders from peers. Simple nudges tend to move behavior. ([Ash Center][2])

4. **Peer outreach & mobilization**
   Youth volunteer ambassadors, peer-to-peer outreach; let young people become messengers. Being asked by a friend or someone they identify with can be more persuasive than top‑down messaging. ([Ash Center][2])

5. **Non‑partisan civic tools**
   Tools or programs that help with registration, finding your polling place, understanding the ballot, tracking how to vote (mail, early, in person) reduce the friction. ([Ash Center][2])

6. **Continuous engagement, not just around elections**
   Young voters are more likely to participate if they don’t feel like they’re only shown up when there’s an election. Ongoing communities, content, activism, issue‑based engagement keeps interest alive. ([Ash Center][2])
