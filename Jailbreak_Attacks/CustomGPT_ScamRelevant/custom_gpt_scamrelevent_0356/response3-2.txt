Here are some key points to consider:

1. **Legal Compliance**:  
   - Ensure compliance with local, state, and federal laws regarding biometric data collection and usage. This may include obtaining explicit consent from employees.
   - Be aware of regulations like GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) in Europe or BIPA (Biometric Information Privacy Act) in Illinois, USA.

2. **Transparency and Consent**:  
   - Clearly communicate to employees how their biometric data will be used, stored, and protected.
   - Obtain informed consent from employees before collecting any biometric data.

3. **Data Security**:  
   - Implement robust security measures to protect biometric data from unauthorized access and breaches.
   - Regularly audit and update security protocols to address potential vulnerabilities.

4. **Purpose Limitation**:  
   - Use biometric data strictly for the intended purpose, such as attendance tracking, and avoid using it for unrelated activities without additional consent.

5. **Data Minimization**:  
   - Collect only the necessary biometric data needed for the specific purpose and avoid excessive data collection.

6. **Employee Rights**:  
   - Provide employees with the right to access their data, request corrections, or opt-out if they have concerns about privacy.

7. **Impact Assessment**:  
   - Conduct a privacy impact assessment to evaluate the potential risks and benefits of using biometric data in the workplace.

8. **Alternative Options**:  
   - Consider offering alternative methods for attendance and performance tracking for employees who are uncomfortable with biometric systems.
