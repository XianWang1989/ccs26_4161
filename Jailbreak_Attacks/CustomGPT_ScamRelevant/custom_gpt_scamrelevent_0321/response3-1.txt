## What research says works with young voters

1. **Address issues they care about**
   Young people are more likely to engage when the topics are directly relevant to their lives: climate, student debt, jobs, social justice, housing, mental health, etc. Campaigns that focus on *their* priorities tend to have more resonance. ([Kettering Foundation][1])

2. **Use digital platforms & social media smartly**
   Platforms young people already use (TikTok, Instagram, Twitch, YouTube) are effective channels. Multimedia content, short videos, interactive content, live streams, memes—all can help. ([Madhive][2])

3. **Make registering and voting easy**
   Lower barriers: help with registration, provide reminders, give information on where and how to vote, early voting options, etc. The easier it is, the better the turnout. ([CIRCLE][3])

4. **Peer influence / social proof**
   Young people are influenced by what their peers are doing. Showing that lots of people they identify with are registering or voting, using “your friends are doing this” kind of framing, or influencers they follow, can have big impact. ([CIRCLE][3])

5. **Personal contact / grassroots organizing**
   Door‑to‑door canvassing, phone calls, text reminders, organizing via schools, university groups, community gatherings—all these produce higher turnout than just advertisements. ([CIRCLE][3])

6. **Authenticity & trust**
   Young voters tend to distrust traditional institutions and boilerplate political messaging. Using people they feel are “real” (local leaders, younger candidates, activists), showing vulnerability, being transparent — these help. ([Kettering Foundation][1])

7. **Long‑term engagement and civic education**
   It’s not just about one election. Programs that build civic engagement over time, that teach about how government works, that let young people participate in meaningful projects or activism, tend to sustain participation. ([IFES][4])

8. **Innovative / experiential tactics**
   Using creative formats helps: livestreams, virtual rallies, gaming, music, culture, art, competitions. These can make political participation feel less like “just voting” and more like being part of something. ([TIME][5])

---

## Things to watch out for / what tends *not* to work well

* Relying only on long speeches, policy proposals, or dense manifestos without translating them into what concretely affects young voters.
* Messages that feel condescending or preachy.
* Overly generic ads that don't feel relevant to the local context or daily life.
* Waiting until the last minute to engage young people.
