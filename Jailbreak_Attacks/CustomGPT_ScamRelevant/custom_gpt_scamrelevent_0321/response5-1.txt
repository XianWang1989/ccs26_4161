## What young voters care about

Before strategy, these themes tend to matter a lot to younger cohorts:

* Issues like climate change, social justice, racial equity, economic opportunity, student debt, housing affordability.
* Authenticity: they often respond when messages feel real, grounded in lived experience, not polished political speak.
* Peer influence and social proof (what friends are doing / saying).
* Digital-first communication (social media, apps, video).
* Convenience and information (how to register, when/where to vote, what’s on the ballot).

## Effective Strategies to Target & Mobilize Young Voters

1. **Meet them where they are—online & offline**

   * Use platforms popular among young people: TikTok, Instagram, Discord, Twitch, Snapchat. Short video, memes, influencer collaborations.
   * Combine digital with physical presence: college campus events, music or arts venues, community hubs.

2. **Digital-first content that feels “real”**

   * Authentic storytelling, not just policy language: stories of young people affected by issues.
   * Peer content: user-generated content, influencer partnerships.
   * Visual, interactive formats: short video, live streams, Q\&As, gaming events, polls.

3. **Simplify civic participation**

   * Easy registration tools (online, via mobile), reminders (texts, email, push notifications).
   * Clear, simple infographics or videos explaining how to vote / what to expect.
   * Help with logistics: how to vote early, what ID is needed, polling place info.

4. **Use peer networks & social influence**

   * Young people are influenced by friends, social media circles. Leverage that: social sharing, “vote with your friends,” “tag a friend.”
   * Grassroots organizing: student groups, clubs, youth-led organizations.

5. **Micro-targeting / personalized messaging**

   * Tailor messages to specific subgroups (by region, by issue, by values). What resonates with young urban voters might differ from those in rural areas.
   * Use data to figure out what issues matter locally, what media they consume.

6. **Credible messengers**

   * Young leaders, peers, influencers who are relatable.
   * Local voices, people from the same community or background.
   * Sometimes younger or nontraditional candidates have an advantage. ([Kettering Foundation][1])

7. **Engagement through interactive/civic tech**

   * Use apps, chatbots, text message tools for voter registration and reminders.
   * Digital tools for conversations, e.g. forums, live sessions, gaming or esports tie-ins.

8. **Consistent, sustained engagement, not just at election time**

   * Keep young people engaged between campaigns: civic education, volunteering, issue-based campaigns.
   * Make participation habitual.([Ash Center][2])

9. **Transparency and cause alignment**

   * Young people often distrust institutions. Be transparent about goals, funding, policies.
   * Align with causes the audience cares about (justice, environment, equality). Show real commitments, not just lip service.
