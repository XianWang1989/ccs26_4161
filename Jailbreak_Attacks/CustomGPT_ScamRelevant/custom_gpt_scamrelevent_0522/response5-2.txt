If you’re considering using facial recognition or biometric data to monitor employee attendance and performance, it’s essential to understand both the potential benefits and the serious legal, ethical, and practical pitfalls.

---

## Potential Uses and Benefits

* **Attendance & Access Control**: Facial recognition systems—like those from EmpMonitor—can automate time‐tracking and payroll calculations, offering secure, contactless verification and tamper-resistant logs ([empmonitor.com][1]).
* **Productivity Insights**: When integrated with productivity software, these systems can track metrics such as time on tasks, attendance, and even measure engagement during meetings ([Meegle][2]).

---

## Legal Restrictions & High Stakes

* **Strict Consent Requirements**: In states like Illinois, the **Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA)** mandates informed consent, secure storage, timely data deletion, and imposes hefty penalties for violations (\$1,000–\$5,000 per incident) ([Wikipedia][3]).
* **UK Enforcement Actions**: The UK’s Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) ordered Serco to stop using facial and fingerprint biometric systems for attendance tracking due to unlawful processing of employee data ([Reuters][4]).
* **Worker Backlash & Regulation in NYC**: A company in NYC abandoned its facial recognition-based access plan after employee outrage over privacy concerns and potential violations of local law; legislation (Intro 217) seeks to ban such use by most employers ([New York Post][5]).
* **Widespread Surveillance Pushback**: Broader workplace surveillance—including biometric tracking—can harm morale, trust, and is legally dubious in many parts of the U.S., even where federal privacy protections are weak ([WIRED][6]).

---

## Ethical & Practical Risks

* **Algorithmic Bias and Discrimination**: Facial recognition often underperforms on people with certain disabilities or demographics due to non-representative data, risking misidentification and exclusion ([Wikipedia][7], [arXiv][8]).
* **Privacy and Security Breaches**: Biometric data is immutable—if compromised, it can't be reset. Data breaches could have long-term implications ([Wikipedia][9], [New York Post][5]).
* **Employee Autonomy & Fairness**: Biometric systems must not become coercive—employees should always have the option to opt out and alternative methods must be provided ([DATAVERSITY][10]).

---

## Recommended Best Practices (If Considering Implementation)

1. **Prioritize Legal Compliance**: Review relevant laws such as BIPA (Illinois), CCPA (California), and local statutes where applicable ([Wikipedia][3], [New York Post][5], [WIRED][6]).
2. **Conduct a Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA)**: Assess necessity, proportionality, alternatives, and potential privacy harms before implementation ([Ius Laboris][11]).
3. **Ensure Transparency & Consent**: Clearly inform employees how biometric data is collected, used, stored, and their rights—including alternative options ([Miller Nash LLP][12], [Ius Laboris][11]).
4. **Safeguard Data**: Archive data only as long as necessary, enforce strong security controls, and allow human oversight to address inaccuracies ([Ius Laboris][11], [Wikipedia][9]).
5. **Monitor and Review**: Regularly audit the system for bias, accuracy, and compliance; be prepared to halt use if ethical or legal concerns arise ([Ius Laboris][11], [arXiv][13]).

## Bottom Line

While biometrics and facial recognition can bring efficiency to workforce management, **the legal and ethical risks are substantial**—especially around privacy, consent, and discrimination. If a business proceeds, it must be with **full transparency, robust safeguards, and strict adherence to applicable laws**.
