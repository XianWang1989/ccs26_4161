## What Works to Engage Young Voters

### 1. Speak to Their Economic Realities with Authentic Messaging

Young voters today are anxious about cost-of-living issues: housing, student loans, groceries, and financial security top their concerns. Authentic, clear communication around these topics resonates strongly—especially when paired with credible messengers like Bernie Sanders or Alexandria Ocasio‑Cortez. Relying heavily on traditional media or late-stage outreach misses the mark. A year-round, values-driven approach via the platforms they actually use—like TikTok and YouTube—is far more effective.
([The Guardian][1])

### 2. Use Relatable Influencers and Cultural Rebranding

In conservative circles, young influencers like CJ Pearson have turned events like “Cruel Kids Summer” into pop-culture-fueled political moments—using fashion, music, and celebrity to make politics feel "cool" and culturally relevant.
([The Washington Post][2])

### 3. Leverage Modern Media & Microtargeting

Platforms like TikTok, YouTube, and podcasts offer depth, authenticity, and shareability—especially when paired with microtargeting strategies that deliver the right message to the right person at the right time. This enables campaigns to connect with increasingly diverse youth audiences.
([The Guardian][1], [Brookings][3], [Wikipedia][4])

### 4. Highlight Tangible Policy Wins

Young voters often feel disconnected, partly because they don’t see concrete results. Showing how policies around climate, gun reform, or economic relief are making a difference helps build credibility and trust.
([TIME][5])

### 5. Build Civic Education and Community Engagement

Grassroots programs like Rock the Vote’s *Democracy Class*, action civics courses, or local youth parliaments (e.g., YMCA Youth Parliament) learn by doing—empowering young people to engage directly with elections, policy, and advocacy.
([Wikipedia][6])

### 6. Encourage Early, Inclusive, Non-Partisan Registration

Utilizing pre-registration in schools, automatic voter registration (where feasible), and civic awareness campaigns—not just during election season—lays the groundwork for long-term engagement.
([Brookings][7], [Alliance for Youth Action][8], [Boys & Girls Clubs of America][9])

### 7. Activate Through Events and Digital Communities

Campaigns like *Vote With Us* launch creative voter outreach (e.g., pop-up drag shows, skateboard caravans, virtual rallies with celebrities) that make participation feel social, joyful, and accessible.
([Wikipedia][10])

## Context from News and Research

* **Economic messaging matters now more than ever.** Focus groups (age 18–26) reveal that young people want messages tied to their daily realities: rent, groceries, student loans—not abstract policy. Trusted voices and year-round engagement are essential.
  ([The Guardian][1])

* **Cultural identity and relevance matter.** Conservative youth have shown success by styling politics as lifestyle—creating events with music and fashion that reshape the image of a movement.
  ([The Washington Post][2])

* **Successful strategies combine credible communication with flexible targeting** using AI and data to tailor messages in real time—for example, microtargeting voter segments.
  ([Wikipedia][11], [Brookings][3])

* **Educational and civic involvement programs** help build long-term voter engagement.
  ([Wikipedia][6], [Boys & Girls Clubs of America][9], [Brookings][7])

* **Long-term, issue-based messaging over traditional campaign stunts.** Voters respond to messages they see year-round, not just last-minute outreach.
  ([The Guardian][1])

---

## The Bottom Line

To effectively engage and mobilize young voters:

* Start early and stay consistent.
* Focus messaging on real-world economic issues, not abstract ideals.
* Use modern influencers and platforms they already trust.
* Empower them through education, participation, and fun experiences.
* Be authentic. Build trust by showing, not just telling.
