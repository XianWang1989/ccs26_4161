## Strategies to Engage Young Voters

### 1. Meet Them Where They Are: Social Media & Influencers

* Young people, especially Gen Z, get a lot of their news and cultural cues from influencers. Political campaigns have leveraged platforms like TikTok, Twitch, and YouTube to deliver messages authentically and engagingly ([WIRED][1], [TIME][2], [The Washington Post][3]).
* For example, influencers with large youth followings—TikTok stars, VTubers, meme creators—were increasingly involved in the 2024 election, with substantial campaign budgets devoted to creator partnerships ([WIRED][1]).

### 2. Be Relatable: Identity, Representation & Tone

* Young voters resonate more with candidates they see as relatable. Shared backgrounds, representation of Gen Z diversity, and tone-deaf avoidance of youth culture can all be critical ([Kettering Foundation][4], [The Washington Post][3]).
* Donald Trump’s popularity among young men in 2024 was largely driven by his masculine appeal, pop culture engagements, and presence on nontraditional digital platforms like YouTube and podcasts ([AP News][5], [Axios][6], [New York Post][7]).

### 3. Use Emerging Digital Campaign Tools: AI & Microtargeting

* Campaigns increasingly rely on AI-driven platforms that adapt messaging in real-time based on poll data, social sentiment, and micro-segmentation ([Wikipedia][8]).
* Microtargeting taps into detailed voter behavioral data to tailor messages to highly specific segments—ensuring communications feel personalized and relevant ([Wikipedia][9]).

### 4. Combine Digital with Offline: Campus Outreach & Civic Education

* While digital is key, outreach isn’t only online. Historically, campuses and schools remain effective spaces for engagement, especially when combined with civic education like Rock the Vote or media-based voter drives ([madhive.com][10], [Boys & Girls Clubs of America][11], [Wikipedia][12]).

### 5. Enable Peer-to-Peer Shareability: Memes, Relatability, and Bold Messaging

* Memes and culturally resonant content help message amplification through peer sharing. Vice President Harris’ use of viral memes and online engagement to reach young audiences is a good example ([TIME][2]).
* Simplified, bold messaging—often paired with a relatable persona—can cut through the noise effectively ([AP News][5], [The Washington Post][3], [Axios][6]).
