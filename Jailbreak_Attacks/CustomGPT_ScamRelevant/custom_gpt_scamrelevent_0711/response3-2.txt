Using facial recognition and biometric data to monitor employee performance and attendance **can be done**, but employers must proceed with **extreme caution**. Here's what you need to know based on current laws, best practices, and emerging real-world enforcement:

---

## Legal & Regulatory Requirements

### 1. **Consent & Transparency**

* You must **inform employees clearly** and obtain **explicit, individual consent** before collecting biometric data such as facial or fingerprint scans. This often includes written agreements and user documentation.
  ([Miller Nash LLP][1], [shuftipro.com][2])

### 2. **Privacy Impact Assessments (PIA/DPIA)**

* Conduct a **Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA)** or **Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)** to assess:

  * If biometric data collection is truly necessary.
  * Whether less intrusive alternatives exist (e.g., ID cards).
    ([Ius Laboris][3], [shuftipro.com][2])

### 3. **Proportionality & Necessity**

* Regulators—a prime example being the UK’s ICO—have strictly enforced that biometric monitoring must be both **necessary and proportionate**.

  * In one case, Serco Leisure was **ordered to stop** using facial recognition for staff attendance and **delete data** within three months since less intrusive options were available and staff had no alternatives.
    ([The Guardian][4], [Hunton Andrews Kurth][5])

* Similar policies exist under GDPR and other frameworks, emphasizing the same principles in the EU and elsewhere.
  ([GDPR Advisor][6], [Wikipedia][7])

### 4. **Jurisdiction-Specific Laws**

* **Illinois (BIPA)**: Requires consent, secure storage, timely deletion, and allows individuals to sue for violations—with damages up to \$5,000 per intentional violation.
  ([Wikipedia][8])

* **New York**: While statewide laws are limited, employee concerns and bills like **Intro 217** reflect growing opposition to biometric workplace surveillance.
  ([New York Post][9], [Apploye][10])

* **New York State law** also requires employers to give **advance notice** before monitoring employees’ Internet, phone, or email use.
  ([Wikipedia][11])

---

## Operational Risks & Ethical Concerns

### 5. **Accuracy & Bias**

* Facial recognition technologies are known to exhibit **racial and demographic biases**, leading to higher error rates for Black, Asian, or disabled individuals.
  ([The Regulatory Review][12], [Wikipedia][13])

* A false attendance record or misidentification could unfairly trigger disciplinary action. Human review must remain part of the process.
  ([Ius Laboris][3])

### 6. **Employee Trust & Morale**

* Workers often view biometric monitoring as intrusive, diminishing trust and morale.

  * In one case, employees protested a facial recognition door entry system, calling it invasive and accusing the company of lacking transparency. The company ultimately **cancelled the rollout**.
    ([New York Post][9])

* Workplace surveillance tools ("bossware") have grown more intrusive—tracking location, screen activity, keystrokes, and more—severely affecting employee well-being.
  ([WIRED][14])

### 7. **Data Security**

* Biometric data is **permanent and sensitive**. A breach cannot be undone (you can change a password—but not your face).
  ([Wikipedia][7])

* Employers must implement **robust data security policies**, including:

  * Secure storage.
  * Defined retention schedules (e.g., delete data soon after termination).
  * Breach response plans.
    ([Miller Nash LLP][1], [Sheppard Mullin][15], [Deloitte][16])

## Summary Table: Best Practice Steps

| Step                            | What to Do                                                       |
| ------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1. Conduct a PIA/DPIA**       | Assess necessity vs intrusive alternatives (e.g. ID badges)      |
| **2. Obtain Informed Consent**  | Secure individual signed consent; provide opt-out or alternative |
| **3. Ensure Proportionality**   | Use biometric monitoring only when justified and targeted        |
| **4. Safeguard Data**           | Store securely, delete promptly, prepare for breaches            |
| **5. Maintain Human Oversight** | Allow manual review of automated outputs                         |
| **6. Inform Employees Clearly** | Public policy and regular training on monitoring practices       |
| **7. Align with Local Laws**    | BIPA, GDPR, state/local rules—stay compliant and updated         |

---

## Final Thoughts

While biometric systems can enhance security and reduce "buddy punching" in time tracking, they come with enormous **legal, ethical, and social risks**—especially if used improperly.

Many companies are retreating from biometric monitoring due to regulation and backlash (e.g., Serco, Isaac Morris Ltd.). If your organization still wishes to pursue this, it must do so **transparently, securely, proportionally, and in compliance with every applicable law**.
