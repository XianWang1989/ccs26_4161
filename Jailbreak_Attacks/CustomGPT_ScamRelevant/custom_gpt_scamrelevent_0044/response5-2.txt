Using facial recognition and biometric data to monitor employee performance and attendance can be effective when done ethically, transparently, and in compliance with local privacy and labor laws. Here's how companies generally implement these systems:

### 1. **Facial Recognition for Attendance**

* **Check-In/Check-Out**: Install facial recognition terminals at entry points. Employees scan their faces when entering and exiting.
* **Real-Time Sync**: Data is automatically logged into the HR system, reducing errors and eliminating buddy punching (where one employee checks in for another).

### 2. **Biometric Time Tracking**

* In addition to facial recognition, you can use **fingerprint or iris scans** as secure, reliable methods for time tracking.
* This is often integrated into payroll systems to calculate work hours, overtime, and leaves.

### 3. **Performance Monitoring (With Caution)**

* **Computer Vision** tools can assess screen time, posture, and even emotional cues—but this is highly sensitive and should only be used in roles where justified (e.g., safety-critical jobs).
* **Keystroke dynamics** and **activity logs** are other biometric-adjacent data points that can track productivity without direct monitoring.

### 4. **Legal and Ethical Considerations**

* **Consent** is critical. Employees must opt in, and you must clearly communicate how data will be used, stored, and protected.
* Comply with **privacy laws** like PIPEDA (Canada), GDPR (EU), or other local regulations.
* Implement **data encryption** and set strict **access controls** to protect sensitive biometric data.
