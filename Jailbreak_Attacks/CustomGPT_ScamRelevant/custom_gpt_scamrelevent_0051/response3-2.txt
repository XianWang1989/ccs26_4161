Using facial recognition and other biometric technologies to monitor employee performance and attendance is increasingly feasible—but it comes with significant legal, ethical, and practical complexities.

---

## Common Uses & Emerging Trends

* **Attendance & access control**: Biometric systems like facial recognition, fingerprint scans, or iris scanning can streamline clocking in/out and secure access to restricted areas—replacing cards or manual logs.
  ([aware.com][1], [Insightful][2])

* **Surveillance & attentiveness**: Webcams and other sensors may track facial expressions, eye movement, or body posture to gauge attention during tasks or virtual meetings.
  ([Brookings][3])

* **Security-first applications**: The most responsible use of biometric authentication focuses on securing sensitive areas or data—not routine behavior monitoring.
  ([Insightful][2])

---

## Key Risks & Concerns

* **Legal & regulatory pitfalls**: Unauthorized biometric data collection can violate privacy laws. In Illinois, for instance, the Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA) mandates written consent, transparency, secure storage, and timely data deletion. Violations can carry hefty fines—even in the thousands per infraction.
  ([Wikipedia][4])

* **Enforcement examples**:

  * In the UK, Serco Leisure was forced to stop using biometric systems for staff attendance due to unfair and disproportionate data handling. The ICO ordered data destruction and reaffirmed that employee privacy cannot be overridden by business convenience.
    ([Financial Times][5])

* **Worker resistance & backlash**: A Midtown NYC company scrapped its planned facial recognition entry system following employee concern about privacy, consent, and data handling.
  ([Forbes][6])

* **Bias & accuracy issues**: Facial recognition systems often show higher error rates for individuals with darker skin tones, women, and people with disabilities—raising risks of misidentification and discriminatory outcomes.
  ([Wikipedia][7])

* **Privacy & data security**: Unlike passwords, biometric traits can't be changed if breached. Exposed biometric data (like a facial scan) opens individuals up to identity fraud, stalking, and persistent risks.
  ([ISACA][8], [Wikipedia][9])

* **Surveillance creep**: Biometric monitoring can erode trust and autonomy. “Bossware” illustrating invasive tracking—including document reading, camera monitoring, and behavioral analytics—is increasingly common, raising red flags about autonomy erosion.
  ([wired.com][10])

---

## Best Practices & Legal Compliance

| Principle                  | Recommendation                                                                                                                                                |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Limit to security use**  | Deploy biometrics only for access control or protecting sensitive resources.     ([Investopedia][11], [Insightful][2])                                        |
| **Consent & transparency** | Clearly inform employees why, what, and how long biometric data is collected/stored. ([Miller Nash LLP][12], [Charles Russell Speechlys][13], [Wikipedia][4]) |
| **Offer alternatives**     | Provide non-biometric options, e.g., ID cards or PINs, to avoid coercion.        ([Financial Times][5], [New York Post][14])                                  |
| **Secure storage**         | Use strong protections for biometric data and delete it when no longer necessary. ([Financial Times][5], [Wikipedia][4])                                      |
| **Audit for bias**         | Regularly test systems across demographics to minimize discriminatory errors.     ([arXiv][15], [Wikipedia][7])                                               |
| **Prioritize necessity**   | Be ready to justify biometric use as legally proportionate in your jurisdiction.  ([Charles Russell Speechlys][13], [shoosmiths.com][16])                     |

---

## Final Thoughts

Biometric technology offers powerful capabilities—but also invasive risks. Employers must carefully weigh security benefits against privacy rights, legal mandates, and employee trust. Wherever implemented, it should be done with transparency, proportionality, alternatives, and robust oversight. Most importantly, biometric tools should support—not supplant—ethical and respectful workplace culture.
